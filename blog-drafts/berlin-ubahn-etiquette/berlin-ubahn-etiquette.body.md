At Alexanderplatz in the morning rush, the map is rarely the problem. The easy mistake is standing in the middle of the doors while passengers are trying to leave. Berlin U-Bahn etiquette is a mix of formal VBB rules and local habits, and knowing which is which makes every journey calmer.

My advice: start with the door. Wait at the sides, let people off, then board and move into the carriage. That one move matters more than trying to imitate a perfect Berlin attitude.

## The rules and habits of Berlin U-Bahn etiquette

Some points below are written transport rules; others are habits that keep a busy station moving. The useful distinction is simple: food, drinks, tickets, sound and clear doorways are covered by current VBB conditions, while standing right on an escalator is a strong local convention.

The most visible mistake is crowding the doors. When a train pulls in, stand to the **sides** of the doorway and let everyone off before you step on. The useful German sentence is **Erst aussteigen lassen, dann einsteigen**: first let people get off, then get on.

Once you are aboard, keep moving into the car. A crowded U8 at Kottbusser Tor fills quickly, and the space beside the doors is where it jams. Move a few steps in and take your **backpack off** so you can hold it low instead of knocking into another passenger.

## Stand right, leave the left clear on the escalator

On a busy Berlin escalator, **stand on the right** and leave the **left side clear** for people who are walking. The familiar phrase is _rechts stehen, links gehen_: stand right, walk left.

![A long escalator leading up out of a Berlin U-Bahn station toward an "Ausgang / exit" sign.](blog-drafts/berlin-ubahn-etiquette/images/optimized/03-berlin-ubahn-etiquette-escalator.jpg)

At interchanges such as Alexanderplatz and Friedrichstraße, keep your suitcase on the right with you rather than across the left lane.

This matters most at deep interchange stations such as Alexanderplatz, Friedrichstraße and Hermannplatz. If you want to ride rather than walk, that is completely fine. Stand on the right, keep your bag with you and leave one clear lane.

## Learn the doors before they close on you

On many Berlin trains, **the doors do not open by themselves**. On older U-Bahn carriages you may need to use a handle; on newer U-Bahn and S-Bahn trains you press the illuminated button.

![The open doors of an older yellow Berlin U-Bahn carriage, with the round door button on the frame.](blog-drafts/berlin-ubahn-etiquette/images/optimized/05-berlin-ubahn-etiquette-door-button.jpg)

Wait for the button to light, then press it. Do not force a door or hold it when the closing signal has started.

The button becomes active after the train stops and releases the doors. I have watched visitors wait politely for a door to open automatically while their stop was about to leave. Look for the light, press once, and step clear of the closing doors.

## Leave priority seats free and keep sound to yourself

Priority seats near the doors are marked for passengers who need them, including disabled passengers, older people, pregnant passengers and adults travelling with small children. You can use one when the train is quiet, but offer it promptly when someone needs it.

![The clean modern interior of a Berlin U-Bahn train, with rows of patterned seats and yellow grab poles.](blog-drafts/berlin-ubahn-etiquette/images/optimized/04-berlin-ubahn-etiquette-interior-seats.jpg)

Priority seats are marked with small icons. When the train is busy, keep bags off the seat beside you.

Use headphones and keep calls low. Current VBB conditions prohibit audio that disturbs other passengers, so a video or speakerphone call playing through the carriage is more than a harmless local faux pas.

## Keep food and drinks closed on board

The current [VBB conditions of carriage](https://sbahn.berlin/tickets/vbb-tarif-erklaert/befoerderungsbedingungen/) prohibit entering a vehicle with **open food or an open drink**, and they prohibit consuming them during the journey. Finish the coffee or pretzel before boarding and keep anything you carry sealed in your bag.

The same rule covers open beer. There is also a current DB pilot at Zoologischer Garten and Ostbahnhof: since 1 May 2026, alcohol may not be consumed or carried open in those stations or their forecourts, apart from licensed shops and restaurants. The [S-Bahn notice](https://sbahn.berlin/aktuelles/artikel/alkohol-verboten/) explains the pilot.

## Bikes, suitcases and the airport run

You can take a **bike on the U-Bahn, S-Bahn and BVG tram** with the appropriate bike ticket. Use a door marked with the bicycle symbol and the designated multipurpose area. Wheelchair users and passengers with prams have priority, and there is no guaranteed right to bring a bike when the vehicle is full.

Current BVG and VBB guidance does **not** publish a blanket weekday rush-hour ban for bikes. The practical limit is available space, and staff can ask you to wait for another train. Check the current [BVG bicycle guidance](https://www.bvg.de/en/subscriptions-and-tickets/all-tickets/bicycles/short-trip-ticket) before travelling; bicycles are not carried on BVG buses.

Keep a suitcase close to you and out of the aisle. The S-Bahn and FEX to BER carry commuters as well as airport passengers, so leave the doorway and the route through the carriage clear.

## The rule that costs real money

Berlin's U-Bahn and S-Bahn platforms have no ticket gates, but inspectors still check passengers. If you travel without a valid ticket, or without validating a paper ticket that requires stamping, BVG can issue a **€60 increased transport charge**, known as an EBE.

You receive an EBE receipt from the inspector and use its number to handle payment; it is misleading to describe this as an immediate cash payment. The official [BVG ticket-control page](https://www.bvg.de/en/subscriptions-and-tickets/ticket-control) explains the process. ## Ride the train well, then see the city on foot

The useful routine is short: let people off, move away from the doors, stand right on the escalator, open the train door when the button lights, keep food and drinks closed, and carry the correct ticket. For a bike, use the marked area and be ready to wait if it is full.
