The Berlin U-Bahn is easy to use and almost impossible to get properly lost on. What trips up visitors is not the map. It is the handful of small, unspoken habits that every Berliner follows without thinking, and that you only notice when you break one and feel the whole carriage quietly notice you.

Nobody will tell you off. Berlin does not work that way. You just get the sigh, the look, the little shift of a shoulder. This is a guide to the things that sign never mentions, so you can ride the U-Bahn and S-Bahn like you have done it a hundred times.

## The unwritten rules of Berlin U-Bahn etiquette

None of this is posted on a wall. It is just how the city moves. Get the basics right and you disappear into the flow, which is exactly what you want on a busy morning train.

The single most visible tourist habit is crowding the doors. When a train pulls in, Berliners stand to the **sides** of the doorway, not in the middle, and they let everyone off before stepping on. There is even a word for it, **Einsteigen lassen**, letting people board, which really means letting people off first. Plant yourself in the middle of the door and you become the small blockage that everyone has to flow around.

Once you are on, keep moving into the car. A crowded U8 at Kottbusser Tor fills fast, and the space by the doors is where it jams. Move a few steps in, and take your **backpack off** so it hangs by your knees instead of swinging into three people's faces.

## Stand right, walk left on the escalator

This one is close to sacred. On every escalator in Berlin you **stand on the right**, single file, and you leave the **left side clear** for people walking up or down. Germans put it plainly: _rechts stehen, links gehen_, stand right, walk left.

![A long escalator leading up out of a Berlin U-Bahn station toward an "Ausgang / exit" sign.](blog-drafts/berlin-ubahn-etiquette/images/optimized/03-berlin-ubahn-etiquette-escalator.jpg)

At deep stations like Alexanderplatz and Friedrichstraße the left lane is a moving staircase for people in a hurry.

At the deep interchange stations, this matters most. Alexanderplatz, Friedrichstraße and Hermannplatz all have long escalators where somebody is always trying to walk up on the left. Stand two-abreast, or block the left with your suitcase, and you will feel the impatience stack up behind you. If you just want to ride up and rest your legs, that is completely fine. Keep to the right and you are doing it exactly the way the city expects.

## Learn the doors before they close on you

Here is the practical rule that catches out almost every first-timer: on many Berlin trains, **the doors do not open by themselves**. You open them. On the older U-Bahn carriages you lift a handle, and on most S-Bahn and newer trains you press a button that lights up.

![The open doors of an older yellow Berlin U-Bahn carriage, with the round door button on the frame.](blog-drafts/berlin-ubahn-etiquette/images/optimized/05-berlin-ubahn-etiquette-door-button.jpg)

Wait for the button to light, then press. Standing and waiting for the door to slide open on its own is how you watch your stop leave without you.

The button only works once the train has released the doors at a stop, so it lights up a second after you come to a halt. Watch for the light, then press. I have seen more than one visitor stand politely in front of a closed door, waiting for it to slide open like an airport, while their stop slips away. If the light is on and nothing happens, press it a bit harder. It is a real button, not a touch panel.

## Leave the priority seats, keep the noise down

Most carriages have a few **priority seats** marked with little icons near the doors. They are for elderly passengers, disabled passengers, pregnant women, and people travelling with small children. You can sit there when the train is quiet, but the deal is simple: if someone who needs it gets on, you get up before they have to ask.

![The clean modern interior of a Berlin U-Bahn train, with rows of patterned seats and yellow grab poles.](blog-drafts/berlin-ubahn-etiquette/images/optimized/04-berlin-ubahn-etiquette-interior-seats.jpg)

Priority seats sit near the doors and are marked with small icons. When it is busy, keep bags off the seat beside you.

A Berlin carriage is also quiet by default, and that surprises people. Keep phone calls **short and low**, and use headphones for anything with sound. Nobody wants your video or your speakerphone playlist, and nobody will say so out loud. They will just look, and in Berlin the look does the work.

## Food, drink and the smell rule

You can eat on the U-Bahn. There is no formal food ban on the BVG, so a pretzel from the bakery, a coffee, or a quick snack is completely normal. What locals quietly dislike is **hot, greasy or strong-smelling food** in a packed carriage. An unwrapped döner at rush hour is the classic example. Save it for a bench on the platform and everyone, including you, has a better ride.

Alcohol is the grey area. It is **technically allowed** on the U-Bahn and S-Bahn, and a quiet beer on the way to a summer evening by the canal is very Berlin. A loud round of cans on a crammed morning train is not. Read the carriage before you open anything.

## Bikes, suitcases and the airport run

Berlin is a cycling city, and you can absolutely take a **bike on the U-Bahn and S-Bahn**, but there are rules. Every bike needs its own **bike ticket** on top of your normal ticket, you board at the door marked with a bike symbol, and only about two bikes fit per marked area. Crucially, bikes are **not allowed during weekday rush hours**, roughly 6 to 9 in the morning and 2 to 5 in the afternoon. A fully folded folding bike counts as hand luggage and rides free. The official rules live on the [BVG bicycle page](https://www.bvg.de/en/bicycle).

Suitcases follow the same spirit. Keep a wheelie case tight against you or in the door bay, not parked in the middle of the aisle. The S-Bahn and the FEX out to BER airport carry commuters as well as travellers, so leave a clear walking path down the car.

## The one rule that costs real money

Everything above is about not annoying anyone. This one is about your wallet. Berlin's platforms are **open**, with no gates or barriers, which fools a lot of visitors into thinking nobody checks. Plain-clothes inspectors board trains without warning, and being caught without a valid ticket, or with a ticket you were supposed to validate and did not, means a **60 euro fine** on the spot.

So buy a ticket, validate it if it needs stamping, and keep it until you leave the station. If you are still working out which ticket and how to buy it, my guides on [the U-Bahn fine](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-u-bahn-fine), [buying tickets on your phone](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/buy-berlin-transport-tickets-on-your-phone) and [the difference between the U-Bahn and S-Bahn](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/u-bahn-vs-s-bahn-berlin) cover the practical side. The [BVG usage policy](https://www.bvg.de/en/service-and-support/usage-policy) is the official word if you want to read the rules yourself.

## Ride like a local, then come up for air

Get these habits right and the U-Bahn stops feeling like a test and starts feeling like the fastest, cheapest way to move through one of Europe's great cities. Let people off, stand right, open the door yourself, keep it quiet, and always have a valid ticket. That is most of it.
