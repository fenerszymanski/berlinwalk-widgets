The first time most visitors really notice the Ringbahn, they are standing on a platform watching a train marked "S41 Ring" roll in, with no clear idea whether it is going their way. Berlin's circle line is one of the most useful things in the city once it clicks, and quietly confusing until it does. So here is the honest version from someone who rides it: what the Ringbahn is, which way each train goes, what it costs, and whether it is actually worth your time.

## What the Berlin Ringbahn actually is

The Ringbahn is a single S-Bahn line bent into a full circle around inner Berlin. Two services share the same loop of track: the **S41 and the S42**. It has 27 stations, runs about 37 kilometres all the way round, and takes roughly **60 minutes for one complete circle**. Berliners look at its rounded, slightly lopsided shape on the map and call it the **Hundekopf**, the dog's head.

The one thing to understand before anything else: the Ring **circles the historic centre, it does not cross it**. It draws a loop around the Brandenburg Gate, Museum Island and Alexanderplatz without ever passing them. If your mental picture is a scenic tour past the landmarks, that is [Bus 100](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/bus-100-berlin-the-4-sightseeing-tour-locals-don-t-want-you-to-know-about), not the Ring. The Ringbahn is the band of everyday Berlin that wraps around the middle: the neighbourhoods, the parks, the railway yards and the markets.

## S41 or S42: which way around the circle

This is the part that trips people up, and it is simpler than it looks. The **S41 runs clockwise** and the **S42 runs anticlockwise**. Same circle, opposite directions, sharing the track like two runners going opposite ways around a stadium.

You do not need to memorise the whole loop. Just do what locals do: **look at the front of the train and the destination it shows**. If the next big corner it lists sits in the direction you want to go, hop on. If not, the platform opposite has a train going the other way that reaches the same stop, only by the long route. Either train gets you there in the end, so if one is already pulling in, take it. The clockwise or anticlockwise choice only matters when you want the faster half of the circle.

For a wider picture of how the S-Bahn fits with the U-Bahn, trams and buses, my guide to [the U-Bahn and S-Bahn](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/u-bahn-vs-s-bahn-berlin) is worth a read before you travel.

## One ticket, one loop: what the Ringbahn costs

Good news for your wallet: the **entire ring sits inside fare Zone AB**, so any standard Berlin ticket covers all 27 stations. There is no special Ringbahn fare.

The catch is the ticket type. A **single AB ticket is 4.00 euros** in 2026 and is valid for two hours in one general direction, but it is **not valid for a round trip**. That matters here, because riding the full circle back to where you started counts as a round trip. If you want to sit on the Ring for the whole loop and end up where you began, buy a **day ticket (AB, 11.20 euros)**, which allows unlimited travel including circles. If you are just hopping from one neighbourhood to another, the single ticket is fine. You can dig into the zones in my post on [Berlin's AB and ABC ticket zones](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-ab-abc-ticket-zones), and buy from any machine or the official [BVG tickets page](https://www.bvg.de/en/subscriptions-and-tickets).

One more thing: **the airport is not on the Ring**. Berlin Brandenburg (BER) sits out in Zone C, so a plain AB ticket will not get you there. You change off the Ring onto an airport train and pay the ABC fare.

## The four corners that hold the ring together

Once you see the Ring as four corners, the whole thing becomes readable. Four big interchange stations sit at roughly north, east, south and west, splitting the circle into quarters:

- **Gesundbrunnen** in the north, a short walk from [Mauerpark](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/mauerpark-berlin-sunday) and its Sunday flea market.
- **Ostkreuz** in the east, the busiest of them all, rebuilt into a cathedral of steel and glass and your gateway to Friedrichshain.
- **Südkreuz** in the south, where fast trains and the airport line meet the Ring.
- **Westkreuz** in the west, the change for the trade-fair grounds and the western suburbs.

If you remember only one name, make it **Ostkreuz**. It is where more lines cross than anywhere else in the city, and where you will almost certainly change trains at some point.

## What each side of the ring reaches

Each quarter of the circle is a different Berlin, and this is where the Ring earns its keep. The interactive map below lets you tap any station to see which neighbourhood it opens up, and follow the loop clockwise or anticlockwise.

The **north and east side**, from Gesundbrunnen round to Ostkreuz, is where most visitors already stay. Schönhauser Allee drops you into café-lined [Prenzlauer Berg](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/prenzlauer-berg-berlin), and Frankfurter Allee opens onto Friedrichshain and a short walk to the East Side Gallery stretch of the Wall.

The **south-east side**, from Ostkreuz down to Südkreuz, is the food-and-green side. Treptower Park hides one of the most powerful sights in the city, Sonnenallee is the heart of Neukölln's Arab quarter, and Tempelhof drops you at a former airport turned enormous park.

![The wide axial view of the Soviet War Memorial in Treptower Park, with sunken lawns, trees and a distant statue under a big sky](blog-drafts/images/berlin-ringbahn/optimized/04-treptower-park-soviet-memorial.jpg)

Treptower Park, a few minutes from its Ring station: the vast Soviet War Memorial, free to walk into and far bigger than photos suggest.

![The former airport runway at Tempelhofer Feld stretching to the horizon under a wide pastel sky, with a single figure standing on the tarmac](blog-drafts/images/berlin-ringbahn/optimized/05-tempelhofer-feld-runway.jpg)

Tempelhofer Feld, reached from the Tempelhof stop: a former airport runway that is now a free public park for cycling, kites and picnics.

The **south-west side**, from Südkreuz round to Westkreuz, is quieter and greener, all comfortable Schöneberg and Wilmersdorf, with the far end of the Ku'damm near Halensee. The **north-west side**, from Westkreuz back up to Gesundbrunnen, is the working city: the exhibition grounds, the inland harbour, freight yards and everyday [Wedding](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-public-transport-explained-for-tourists-u-bahn-s-bahn-tram-bus). Honest tourists can ride straight past most of that last quarter.

## Is the Ringbahn worth riding? My honest take

If you are asking whether to ride the full circle as a sightseeing loop, my answer is: only if you enjoy watching a city go by from a train window. You will see rooftops, backyards, allotment gardens, canals and railway land, not monuments. As a landmark tour it loses to Bus 100 every time.

Where the Ring genuinely shines is as a tool. **It is the fastest, cheapest way to reach the ring of neighbourhoods around the centre**, and a single quiet hour on it teaches you the real shape of Berlin better than any map. My advice: do not force the whole loop. Pick one side, ride it to a stop that interests you, get off, and let the neighbourhood be the point. I have watched people spend an hour circling the city and step off exactly where they started, a little bored. Ride one quarter with intent instead.

The Ring wraps around the old core, but the old core is best seen on foot. That is where I come in: I run a [free walking tour from Alexanderplatz](https://www.berlinwalk.com/book-berlin-walking-tour/berlin-free-walking-tour-tip-based) through the historic centre the Ring never quite touches, about 2 hours on foot. Do the centre with me, then use the Ringbahn to reach whichever neighbourhood you want next.
