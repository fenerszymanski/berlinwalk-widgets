You booked a flight to Berlin, and somewhere in the planning your search history filled up with three names: Tegel, Schönefeld, and BER. A friend mentions Tegel. An old blog post lists SXF. Your booking confirmation says BER. So which one do you actually land at, and how many airports does this city have?

Here is the short, calming answer, and it is one of the easiest things to get right about a Berlin trip. There is only one airport now.

## Berlin airports in 2026: one airport, one name

For years Berlin was the city with the airport situation that never ended. Two working airports, a third one famous for closing, and a new one that took so long to open it became a national joke. That era is over. In 2026, all commercial flights to Berlin land at one place: **Berlin Brandenburg Airport, code BER**, officially named after Willy Brandt.

BER sits about **18 km southeast of the city centre**, out past Schönefeld. It fully opened in October 2020, and on that day the old airports stopped taking passengers. So when you compare Berlin airports today, you are really only comparing terminals inside one airport, plus a couple of famous ruins you can still visit.

If a booking site or an old guide still shows **TXL** or **SXF** as your Berlin airport, it is out of date. Every flight now uses **BER**. That is the single most useful thing to know before you fly.

## Terminal 1, Terminal 2, and the ghost of Terminal 5

BER has two passenger terminals, and they are close together.

**Terminal 1 (T1)** is the big main building, the one in most photos. Lufthansa and its Star Alliance partners use it, along with many other airlines, full-service and budget alike. If your ticket does not say otherwise, assume T1.

**Terminal 2 (T2)** is a smaller building right next to T1, a short covered walk away. It is used at busier times and by several low-cost carriers, easyJet among them. Do not panic if your boarding pass says T2. It is not a separate airport, it is a two-minute walk from its neighbour, and the same train station sits underneath both.

Then there is **Terminal 5**. You may still see it mentioned, so it is worth clearing up: T5 was the old Schönefeld Airport building, kept on as an overflow terminal when BER opened. It has since been **closed for good**. No flights use it. If any itinerary points you at Terminal 5, treat it as a mistake and check with your airline.

## Wait, what happened to Tegel?

Tegel is the airport people miss. **TXL closed in November 2020**, a few weeks after BER opened, and for a lot of Berliners that was an emotional goodbye. Its hexagonal terminal was famously efficient: you could get from taxi to gate in minutes, because the whole building was designed as one tight ring of short walks.

Today the Tegel site is being turned into a research and housing district, the **Urban Tech Republic** and the Schumacher Quartier. The runways are gone as an airport, but the distinctive hexagon still stands. So if your travel memories or a well-meaning friend send you to Tegel, gently redirect: there are no flights there, and there have not been since 2020.

## And Schönefeld and Tempelhof?

Two more names that confuse first-time visitors, and both have simple answers.

**Schönefeld (SXF)** did not vanish, it got absorbed. Its terminal became BER's Terminal 5, and as noted above that terminal is now closed. The name Schönefeld still exists as a place and a regional train stop, but as an airport it is part of BER's story, not a separate destination.

**Tempelhof (THF)** is the one worth going out of your way for. It closed all the way back in **2008**, and instead of being demolished it became **Tempelhofer Feld**, an enormous free public park on the old runways and apron. On a summer afternoon you will find Berliners cycling down the old taxiways, flying kites, and barbecuing on the grass between the landing strips. It is one of my favourite easy recommendations for a relaxed few hours, and you can read more in my guide to [Tempelhof Airport and its park](/post/tempelhof-airport-berlin).

## Getting from BER into the city

Landing at one airport instead of three makes arrival simple, and the train underneath the terminals does most of the work. The rail station is called **Flughafen BER – Terminal 1-2**, and it sits directly below the two terminals. Follow the signs down, and you have a few good options.

The fastest is the **FEX, the Flughafen-Express**. It runs about **every 15 minutes** and reaches **Berlin Hauptbahnhof in around 23 minutes**, stopping at Ostkreuz and Gesundbrunnen on the way. For most visitors heading to central or western Berlin, this is the one to look for on the board.

**Regional trains** (RE7, RB14, RB22 and others) also connect BER with central stations and are just as valid on the same ticket. The **S-Bahn S9** is slower but runs day and night, and it can be the easiest choice if you are staying in the eastern part of the city. A taxi or ride-hailing car takes roughly 30 to 45 minutes and costs somewhere around **50 to 60 euros** depending on traffic and where you are going.

One ticket rule matters here: **BER is in fare zone C**, so any train to or from the airport needs a **Berlin ABC ticket**, which is **5.00 euros** for a single in 2026. Buy it from a BVG or VBB machine or the app before you board, and if it is a paper ticket, stamp it in the small validator on the platform. ## A simple arrival plan

If you want the whole thing in one breath: you are flying into **BER**, you will arrive at **Terminal 1 or Terminal 2**, and you will walk down to the station below to catch the **FEX or a regional train** into town on an **ABC ticket**. That is the entire airport puzzle solved.

The old names are worth knowing only so you can ignore them. Tegel is a building site with a beautiful hexagon, Schönefeld is a closed terminal, and Tempelhof is a park. None of them will be printed on your boarding pass. For a smoother first few hours overall, my [first-time visitor mistakes](/post/berlin-first-time-visitor-mistakes-12-things-to-know-before-you-go) guide covers the small things that trip people up on day one, and my [departure guide](/post/berlin-ber-airport-departure-guide) handles the trip back out when it is time to leave.

Once you are in the centre and over the flight, the best way to make sense of Berlin is on foot. My free walking tour starts at Alexanderplatz and spends about 2 hours on the historic core, and it is an easy, low-pressure way to get your bearings on the first or second day. However you arrive, the airport part is the simplest it has been in decades.
