# 10 German Words Every Tourist Should Know Before Visiting Berlin

You do not need fluent German to visit Berlin.

In many hotels, museums, restaurants and cafés, English will be enough. Berlin is international, and people are used to visitors.

But a few German words change the texture of the trip.

They make small moments easier: ordering coffee, asking for the bill, finding the right platform, getting past someone on the U-Bahn, understanding a sign, or tipping without making the waiter guess what you mean.

They also show respect. You are not pretending to be local. You are just meeting the city halfway.

This is not a full language course. It is a short, practical list of the German words and phrases I would put in your pocket before a first Berlin trip.

## First, Do You Actually Need German in Berlin?

For most tourist situations, no.

You can visit Berlin with English and have a perfectly good trip. The airport, train stations, major museums, restaurants in central areas and most tourist-facing services are used to English speakers.

But German helps in three ways.

- **It softens the first second:** a simple **Hallo** or **Entschuldigung** makes an interaction start better.
- **It handles practical moments:** tickets, bills, directions, menus and payment words appear everywhere.
- **It helps you read the city:** Berlin signs, announcements and everyday notices often make more sense once you know a few key words.

## A Quick Pronunciation Note

German pronunciation looks scarier than it is.

For a tourist trip, you do not need a perfect accent. You just need to be clear enough that the person understands the word and sees you are making an effort.

Three tiny rules help a lot:

- **German W sounds like English V:** **Wasser** sounds closer to "vah-ser" than "wah-ser."
- **German V often sounds like English F:** **viel** sounds like "feel."
- **IE and EI are different:** **die** sounds like "dee," but **ein** sounds like "ine."

The German **ch** sound is the tricky one. You hear it in words like **ich** and **nicht**. Do not worry if it comes out imperfectly. People will still understand from context.

The real Berlin rule is simpler: say it with confidence, smile if you get stuck, and do not turn the moment into a performance.

Most people are not judging your accent. They are just trying to sell you a coffee, answer a question, or get through the queue.

## 1. Hallo

**Meaning:** hello.

**Pronunciation:** HA-lo.

This is the easiest word on the list and probably the most useful.

Use **Hallo** when you enter a small shop, approach a counter, step up to a café bar or start a simple interaction.

In Berlin, you do not need to sound overly formal. **Hallo** is neutral, friendly and safe almost everywhere.

Examples:

- **Hallo.** - Hello.
- **Hallo, eine Kaffee bitte.** - Hello, one coffee please.
- **Hallo, sprechen Sie Englisch?** - Hello, do you speak English?

You will also hear **Guten Tag**, which means good day, and **Guten Morgen**, which means good morning. Those are fine too. But if you only remember one greeting, remember **Hallo**.

## 2. Danke

**Meaning:** thank you.

**Pronunciation:** DAHN-keh.

This is the word that quietly improves your whole trip.

Say it when someone gives you directions, hands you a receipt, brings your drink, opens a door, checks your ticket or answers your question.

You can make it warmer:

- **Danke schön** - thank you very much.
- **Vielen Dank** - many thanks.
- **Danke dir** - thanks to you, informal.

For most tourist situations, plain **Danke** is enough.

Berlin can sound direct, but people still appreciate basic politeness. This word costs nothing and works everywhere.

## 3. Bitte

**Meaning:** please, you're welcome, here you go.

**Pronunciation:** BIT-teh.

**Bitte** is a tiny word with several jobs.

Use it when asking for something:

- **Ein Wasser, bitte.** - One water, please.
- **Die Rechnung, bitte.** - The bill, please.

Use it when someone says **Danke**:

- **Danke.**
- **Bitte.** - You're welcome.

You may also hear it when someone hands you something, like a coffee, receipt or ticket. In that case it means something like "here you go."

If German politeness had a pocket tool, it would be **bitte**.

## 4. Entschuldigung

**Meaning:** excuse me, sorry.

**Pronunciation:** ent-SHOOL-dee-gung.

This one looks long, but it is worth learning.

Use **Entschuldigung** when you need someone's attention, when you are squeezing past people, when you bump into someone, or when you want to ask a question politely.

Examples:

- **Entschuldigung, wo ist der Ausgang?** - Excuse me, where is the exit?
- **Entschuldigung, ist dieser Platz frei?** - Excuse me, is this seat free?
- **Entschuldigung.** - Sorry.

In everyday Berlin speech you may hear the shorter **'Tschuldigung**.

You do not need to master the short version. If you can say the full word slowly and clearly, people will understand.

## 5. Sprechen Sie Englisch?

**Meaning:** do you speak English?

**Pronunciation:** SHPREKH-en zee ENG-lish.

This is the rescue phrase.

Start in German, then ask this. It shows effort and gives the other person a clear way to switch languages.

The full polite version is:

**Entschuldigung, sprechen Sie Englisch?**

That means: excuse me, do you speak English?

In central Berlin, the answer will often be yes. Outside tourist areas, with older people, or in more bureaucratic settings, it may be less guaranteed.

If someone says **ein bisschen**, that means "a little bit."

That is usually enough.

## 6. Wo ist...?

**Meaning:** where is...?

**Pronunciation:** voh ist.

This is the phrase that unlocks directions.

You put the place after it:

- **Wo ist der Bahnhof?** - Where is the train station?
- **Wo ist die Toilette?** - Where is the toilet?
- **Wo ist der Ausgang?** - Where is the exit?
- **Wo ist Alexanderplatz?** - Where is Alexanderplatz?

The grammar changes depending on the noun, but do not panic.

If you say **Wo ist Toilette?** with a confused face, people will understand what you mean. Perfect grammar is not the point. Getting help is the point.

Useful signs to recognise:

- **Ausgang** - exit.
- **Eingang** - entrance.
- **Bahnhof** - train station.
- **Gleis** - platform or track.

## 7. Eine Fahrkarte, bitte

**Meaning:** one ticket, please.

**Pronunciation:** EYE-neh FAR-kar-teh BIT-teh.

Berlin public transport has ticket machines and apps, so you may not need to say this often.

But it is still useful.

**Fahrkarte** means ticket for a ride. You may see it around transport, ticket offices and older signs.

Examples:

- **Eine Fahrkarte, bitte.** - One ticket, please.
- **Zwei Fahrkarten, bitte.** - Two tickets, please.
- **Ist das die richtige Fahrkarte?** - Is this the right ticket?

For the actual transport system, remember three more words:

- **U-Bahn** - underground train.
- **S-Bahn** - city rail.
- **Straßenbahn** - tram.

If transport makes your head spin, that is normal. Berlin ticket logic has a personality of its own.

## 8. Die Rechnung, bitte

**Meaning:** the bill, please.

**Pronunciation:** dee REKH-nung BIT-teh.

In Germany, the bill often does not arrive until you ask for it.

This is not bad service. It is normal. A waiter may leave you in peace until you signal that you are ready.

Use:

**Die Rechnung, bitte.**

Or, more casually:

**Zahlen, bitte.**

That means: paying, please.

If you are sitting in a restaurant and wondering why nobody brings the check, this phrase is your key.

It is also connected to tipping. In Germany, you usually tell the server the total amount you want to pay, including the tip, instead of silently leaving money on the table.

## 9. Karte

**Meaning:** card, menu, map, ticket, depending on context.

**Pronunciation:** KAR-teh.

This word is useful because it appears everywhere, but it can mean different things.

In a restaurant:

**Speisekarte** means menu.

When paying:

**Mit Karte** means by card.

On transport:

**Fahrkarte** means ticket.

On your phone:

**Karte** can also mean map.

The context usually tells you which meaning is intended.

One very practical question:

**Kann ich mit Karte zahlen?**

That means: can I pay by card?

You will not need this everywhere, but it is still useful because some Berlin places remain surprisingly cash-friendly. If payment is a concern, read my guide to [using credit cards in Berlin](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/can-you-use-credit-cards-in-berlin-a-tourist-s-guide-to-paying-in-germany).

## 10. Stimmt so

**Meaning:** keep the change, that amount is fine.

**Pronunciation:** shtimt zo.

This is one of the most useful restaurant phrases in Germany.

Imagine your bill is EUR 18.40 and you want to pay EUR 20 total.

You hand over EUR 20 and say:

**Stimmt so.**

That tells the server they can keep the change. It is not flashy. It is the normal, practical way to tip when paying directly.

If you pay by card, you can also say the total you want to pay:

**Zwanzig, bitte.**

That means: twenty, please.

## Bonus Berlin Words

These are not standard tourist basics, but you will see or hear them in Berlin:

- **Späti:** late-night corner shop.
- **Kiez:** local neighbourhood or home patch.
- **Pfand:** bottle deposit.
- **Moin:** casual hello, less Berlin-specific but still heard.
- **Tschüss:** bye.

For the local flavour version, read my guide to [Berlin slang and Berlinerisch](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-slang-10-words-you-ll-only-hear-in-this-city). That is where words like **ick**, **dit**, **keene**, **Schrippe** and **JWD** belong.

## How to Use German Without Feeling Awkward

The trick is not to force a full German conversation.

Use German as an opening, then switch if needed.

That can look like this:

- **Hallo. Sprechen Sie Englisch?**
- **Entschuldigung, wo ist der Ausgang?**
- **Die Rechnung, bitte.**
- **Danke. Tschüss.**

That is enough.

You are not trying to prove anything. You are making the interaction smoother.

There are also moments when English is simply the better choice. If you are dealing with medical details, a lost passport, a booking problem or complicated transport disruption, clarity matters more than language practice.

In those moments, start politely and then ask for English.

One more Berlin-specific note: do not be offended if the reply is short.

Berlin service style can be direct. A quick answer is not always irritation. Sometimes it is just the city moving at its normal speed.

## Practice Before You Go

You do not need to memorise everything.

Start with this small survival set:

- **Hallo**
- **Danke**
- **Bitte**
- **Entschuldigung**
- **Sprechen Sie Englisch?**
- **Wo ist...?**
- **Die Rechnung, bitte**
- **Stimmt so**

If you can use those calmly, you are already ahead of most first-time visitors.
