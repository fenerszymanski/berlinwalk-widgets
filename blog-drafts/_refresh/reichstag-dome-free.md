# How to Visit the Reichstag Dome for Free

One of the best views in Berlin is completely free.

It is not a secret rooftop bar, a hotel terrace, or a trick. It is the glass dome on top of the Reichstag, Germany's parliament building.

You can walk up inside the dome, look across the government quarter, see the Brandenburg Gate, Tiergarten, the TV Tower and the Spree, and listen to a free audio guide that explains what you are seeing.

The catch is simple:

**You must book in advance.**

Every week, visitors turn up at the Reichstag without a reservation and leave disappointed. The dome is free, but it is not walk-in sightseeing.

And the booking itself works more like a request window than a casual attraction ticket. The Bundestag only accepts dome requests for the current month and the following month, and even confirmed visits can still change if parliamentary business, weather or security intervene.

Here is exactly how to visit the Reichstag dome for free, what to expect, and why I think it is one of the best-value experiences in Berlin.

## The Short Answer

Yes, the Reichstag dome is worth it.

It is free, central, meaningful, and gives you one of the best skyline views in Berlin. Unlike the TV Tower, it is not just a viewpoint. It is also a political symbol: the public walking above the parliament chamber in a glass dome.

That is a very Berlin kind of message.

But the dome requires planning.

You need to register online, receive confirmation, arrive early, pass security, and bring valid photo ID.

If you do those things, the visit is straightforward.

If you do not, you probably will not get in.

## Is the Reichstag Dome Really Free?

Yes.

The official [Bundestag registration page](https://www.bundestag.de/en/visittheBundestag/dome/registration-245686) states that admission is free and advance registration is required.

There is no hidden ticket price.

You do not need to buy a tour, pay a booking agency, or join a package unless you want something extra.

The free visit usually includes:

- **Access to the roof terrace:** the open area around the dome.
- **Access to the glass dome:** when the dome is open.
- **A free audio guide:** usually available in multiple languages.
- **Skyline views:** government quarter, Tiergarten, Brandenburg Gate, Potsdamer Platz, TV Tower and more.

That combination is why I recommend it so strongly.

Berlin has many paid views. This one is free and historically meaningful.

## How to Book the Reichstag Dome

Use the official Bundestag visitor system.

The safest starting point is the [Bundestag dome registration page](https://www.bundestag.de/en/visittheBundestag/dome/registration-245686), which explains the visit and links to the booking process.

The direct booking form is the official [Visiting the Bundestag request page](https://visite.bundestag.de/BAPWeb/pages/createBookingRequest.jsf?lang=en).

Submitting that form is not the same as having a ticket in your hand. The booking page says your visit is only booked once the Visitors' Service sends a confirmation by email.

The booking process is usually:

- Choose the visit type for the dome or roof terrace.
- Choose an available date and time.
- Enter visitor details, including names and dates of birth.
- Submit the request.
- Wait for confirmation by email.
- Bring the confirmation and valid photo ID on the day.

Do not treat the first email as a casual reminder.

Your confirmation matters. Security at the Reichstag is strict, and the names on the reservation must match the visitors who arrive.

If you are booking for a group, enter every person carefully.

One typo can become a problem at the entrance.

If you miss the advance window, there is one fallback. The Visitors' Service runs a service centre near the Reichstag, and if free places remain, it can issue a same-day confirmation at least two hours before the visit. I would treat that as luck, not as a plan.

## When Should You Book?

Book as soon as you know your Berlin dates.

The official booking system says requests can only be submitted for the current month and the following month, so you cannot always book far in advance. But once your dates are inside that window, do not wait.

Summer, weekends, school holidays and sunset slots can disappear quickly.

And even a confirmed slot is not an absolute guarantee. The official booking page says parliamentary business, special events, weather and the security situation can still force last-minute cancellations.

For most visitors, these are the best times:

- **Early morning:** quieter, calmer, good for photos.
- **Late afternoon:** softer light over Tiergarten and the government quarter.
- **Sunset:** best atmosphere, hardest slot to get.
- **Evening:** beautiful city lights, especially in winter.

If your Berlin trip is short, book the Reichstag dome before you book optional paid viewpoints.

It is free, but the limited time slots make it feel like a ticketed attraction.

## Important 2026 Closure Dates

The dome closes several times a year for cleaning and maintenance.

This is easy to miss.

The Bundestag lists 2026 dome closure periods including:

- **15 to 19 June 2026**
- **29 June to 3 July 2026**
- **14 to 18 September 2026**
- **28 September to 2 October 2026**
- **19 to 30 October 2026**

During dome closures, the Bundestag says the roof terrace can still be visited when the dome itself is closed.

That means a closure does not always mean "no visit at all." It may mean no spiral walk inside the glass dome, but possible access to the roof terrace.

It also means you should reset your expectations slightly. The Bundestag notes that the dome audioguide is not available when the dome itself is closed, so a terrace-only visit is still useful, but not quite the full experience.

Always check the official page before planning your day.

Dates can change because parliamentary business, security and weather can affect visits.

## What to Bring

Bring less than you think.

The Reichstag is a parliament building, not a normal tourist attraction.

You should bring:

- **Passport or official photo ID:** the same identity details must match your booking.
- **Booking confirmation:** digital is usually fine, but save it offline.
- **A charged phone:** for confirmation, photos and maps.
- **A light jacket:** the roof terrace can be windy.
- **Patience:** security is normal here.

Do not bring large luggage.

If you are moving between hotel and train station, store your bags elsewhere before visiting. The Bundestag says there are no luggage storage facilities on site.

Also arrive early.

I would aim for 20 to 30 minutes before your time slot. The old advice of arriving exactly on time is too risky if there is a line at security or if you need to find the entrance.

## Where Is the Entrance?

The visitor entrance is near the Reichstag building, beside Platz der Republik.

Most visitors use the standard entrance, but the Bundestag notes that people with disabilities, frailer visitors, parents with prams and rooftop restaurant guests may be routed through **West C**.

The closest public transport stop is usually **Brandenburger Tor**, served by U-Bahn, S-Bahn and buses.

From Brandenburg Gate, it is an easy walk north to the Reichstag.

Do not confuse the Reichstag with the nearby Chancellery or other government buildings. The Reichstag is the historic building with the glass dome on top and the inscription **Dem Deutschen Volke** above the main facade.

If in doubt, follow the signs and the security fencing.

Give yourself extra time if you are coming from Alexanderplatz, Museum Island or Kreuzberg. Berlin transport is good, but a missed connection can cost your slot.

## What Happens on the Day

The visit is simple once you arrive.

You check in, show your confirmation and ID, pass security, and wait to be taken up by lift.

From there, you reach the roof terrace and dome area.

Inside the dome, a spiral ramp leads you upward around a mirrored cone. The design brings natural light down into the parliament chamber and gives visitors a slow 360-degree view as they climb.

That is the clever part.

The dome is not only decorative. It turns the visit into a symbol: people walking above parliament, looking down into the democratic chamber, with the city visible all around.

Most visitors spend **30 to 45 minutes** on the roof and in the dome.

Do not rush it.

The view changes as you move, and the audio guide is actually useful.

## What You Can See From the Dome

The Reichstag dome view is not the highest view in Berlin.

It is not trying to be.

The strength is context.

From the roof and dome, you can usually spot:

- **Brandenburg Gate:** just to the south-east.
- **Tiergarten:** the huge green park stretching west.
- **The TV Tower:** far across the city at Alexanderplatz.
- **Potsdamer Platz:** modern towers to the south.
- **The Spree:** curving through the government quarter.
- **The Chancellery:** the large modern building nearby.
- **Paul-Löbe-Haus and Marie-Elisabeth-Lüders-Haus:** part of the parliamentary complex.

This is why I prefer the Reichstag dome to many paid viewpoints.

It helps you read the city.

The TV Tower gives you height. The Reichstag gives you meaning.

## Reichstag Dome vs. TV Tower

The TV Tower is higher.

The Reichstag dome is better value.

That is the simplest comparison.

The [Berlin TV Tower](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/is-the-berlin-tv-tower-worth-it-an-honest-guide-for-2026) is a classic paid observation deck, with official tickets starting around EUR 28.50. It gives you a much higher view, and it is iconic.

But it is expensive, indoors, and you cannot see the TV Tower while you are inside it.

The Reichstag dome is free, lower, more architecturally interesting, and historically richer.

If you can do both, do both.

If you only choose one, I would choose the Reichstag dome for most first-time visitors.

It feels more like Berlin and less like a generic skyline ticket.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Most Reichstag problems are avoidable.

Avoid these:

- **Showing up without a reservation:** do not assume you can talk your way in.
- **Booking too late:** sunset and weekend slots go fast.
- **Using the wrong names:** booking details must match the people arriving.
- **Forgetting ID:** no ID can mean no entry.
- **Arriving at the last minute:** security can take time.
- **Ignoring closure dates:** the dome has maintenance closures.
- **Bringing luggage:** this is a parliament building, not a storage locker.

The biggest mistake is treating "free" as meaning "casual."

The Reichstag dome is free, but the process is serious.

## Is the Käfer Rooftop Restaurant Worth It?

There is a restaurant on the roof, operated by Käfer, and this matters more than many visitors realise.

A normal dome slot is still the cleanest option. But if the free slots are gone and you genuinely want the Reichstag experience, a Käfer reservation can be a real paid alternative.

Käfer's own site says restaurant guests enjoy access to the roof terrace and glass dome of the Bundestag. In other words, this is not a myth, but it is a separate reservation system with meal prices attached.

You use the west entrance security route, arrive about 15 minutes early, and bring your reservation confirmation plus valid photo ID.

So treat Käfer as a workaround only if you actually want the meal. Do not book it just because somebody online told you the dome is impossible.

For most visitors, I would separate the decisions like this:

- **Want the view and architecture?** Book the free dome visit.
- **Want a special meal with roof access?** Check Käfer separately and reserve early.
- **Got neither?** Switch to a plan-B viewpoint instead of hovering around Platz der Republik.

The dome itself does not need the restaurant to be worthwhile. If both the dome slots and restaurant times are full, I would move on to a different Berlin view such as the TV Tower, Panoramapunkt, the Victory Column or a simple government-quarter walk.

## The History Most Visitors Miss

The Reichstag is not just a nice building with a glass dome.

It is one of the most loaded buildings in Europe.

It opened in 1894 as the home of the German parliament. In 1918, the republic was proclaimed nearby. In 1933, the Reichstag fire became a key moment in Hitler's consolidation of power.

During the Second World War, the building was badly damaged.

In 1945, Soviet soldiers raised the Red Army flag over the Reichstag, turning it into one of the most famous images of the war's end in Europe.

During the Cold War, the building stood near the Wall, symbolic but politically sidelined.

After reunification, it was restored by Norman Foster and given the glass dome you see today.

That dome was not just an architectural flourish.

It was a statement: transparency, democracy, and public oversight after a century of German political catastrophe.

That is why the visit matters.

Not because the view is pretty.

Because of where you are standing.

## Practical Details

Here is the clean version:

- **Price:** free.
- **Booking:** required through the official Bundestag system.
- **ID:** bring valid photo ID for every visitor.
- **Best time:** early morning, sunset or evening.
- **Visit length:** usually 30 to 45 minutes.
- **Nearest station:** Brandenburger Tor.
- **Security:** airport-style checks.
- **Accessibility:** check the official Bundestag visitor information for current access details.
- **Closures:** check before you go, especially during listed maintenance periods.

For a first Berlin trip, I would book this before the TV Tower.

It is one of the rare attractions where the free option is also one of the best options.
