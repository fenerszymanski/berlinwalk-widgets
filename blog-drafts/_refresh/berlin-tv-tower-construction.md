# Berlin TV Tower Construction: Before, During, and After

Berlin's TV Tower looks as if it has always belonged above Alexanderplatz. It is so familiar now that many visitors treat it like a compass, a postcard shape, or a useful meeting point.

That is the quiet trick of the Fernsehturm. It feels inevitable.

It was not inevitable at all.

The Berlin TV Tower was built between **1965 and 1969** by East Germany, in the middle of a divided city, on top of a badly scarred medieval quarter. It was a broadcasting tower, a political monument, an engineering risk and a giant symbol aimed straight across the Wall.

It also produced one of Berlin's best accidental jokes: the famous cross-shaped reflection on its sphere, nicknamed **the Pope's Revenge**.

This is the story of what stood here before, how the tower was built, what went wrong during construction, and why a GDR prestige project became one of the most loved symbols of reunified Berlin.

## Before the Tower: The Old Centre Under the Concrete

Before the TV Tower, this part of Berlin belonged to the old centre of the city. The area around St. Mary's Church, the Rotes Rathaus and today's Alexanderplatz was part of the historic Marienviertel, a district of tight streets, small plots and older buildings.

The Second World War badly damaged this area. Then East Berlin's planners made a second, more deliberate change. They cleared large parts of the old street pattern and opened the space for a new socialist city centre.

That is why the ground around the TV Tower feels so wide and empty today.

It is not just a plaza. It is an absence.

St. Mary's Church still stands beside the tower, and that contrast is one of the most useful things to notice on the spot. The church points back to medieval Berlin. The tower points to the GDR's future fantasy. Between them is a huge amount of lost city.

I talk about this nearby in my guide to [St. Mary's Church](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/st-mary-s-church-the-medieval-survivor-in-the-shadow-of-the-tv-tower), because it is one of the best places to feel how much of old Berlin vanished before the modern centre took shape.

## Why East Germany Wanted a Tower Here

Officially, the GDR needed a better broadcasting facility. Television and radio were real technical needs, and a high tower in Berlin made practical sense.

But the TV Tower was never only practical.

East Germany wanted a skyline symbol. Walter Ulbricht and the SED leadership wanted something that could be seen from West Berlin, something tall enough to say: the socialist capital can build the future.

That mattered because Berlin was not just any city. It was the front line of the Cold War. Every building, border crossing and boulevard carried political meaning.

The tower did three jobs at once:

- **Broadcasting:** it improved television and radio transmission in and around East Berlin.
- **Prestige:** it gave the GDR a modern landmark in the centre of its capital.
- **Visibility:** it rose high enough to be seen from both sides of the divided city.
- **Urban planning:** it anchored a new socialist centre around Alexanderplatz, the Rotes Rathaus and the cleared Marienviertel.

The official [Berlin TV Tower history page](https://tv-turm.de/en/history/) describes the sphere as a deliberate reference to Soviet Sputnik satellites. It was supposed to feel modern, scientific and cosmic.

The message was simple: this was not old Berlin anymore.

This was the future, built in concrete and steel.

## The First Location Failed

The tower was not originally meant to stand at Alexanderplatz.

In the 1950s, East German planners considered the Mueggelberge hills in southeast Berlin. That would have put the tower far from the historic centre, but high enough for broadcasting.

The plan ran into a serious problem. The site was close to the approach path of Schoenefeld Airport, and a tower of that size would have been a danger to air traffic.

So the project moved.

By the mid 1960s, the political value of a central tower had become too attractive to ignore. A tower at Alexanderplatz would not be hidden on the edge of the city. It would stand in the heart of East Berlin, visible from Unter den Linden, Museum Island, the western sectors and almost every train line approaching the centre.

That move changed the meaning of the building.

It stopped being just a transmitter and became a statement.

## Who Designed the Berlin TV Tower?

No single person can honestly claim the whole tower.

The original idea of the slim, futuristic form is usually connected with architect **Hermann Henselmann**, one of the major figures in East German architecture. The wider project involved architects and engineers from VEB Industrieprojektierung Berlin, often shortened to IPRO, with names including Fritz Dieter, Gunter Franke and Werner Neumann.

Gerhard Kosel, then president of the German Building Academy, was also involved in the early direction of the project.

That sounds like a committee, because it was one. East German prestige architecture often worked that way: politics, engineering, planning offices and symbolic design all pushed against each other.

The result is surprisingly elegant. The tower has a thin concrete shaft, a large steel sphere, and a needle-like antenna. It is easy to draw from memory, which is one reason it became such a powerful logo for Berlin later.

The shape also carried political symbolism:

- **The sphere** suggested Sputnik and space-age socialism.
- **The height** made the tower impossible to ignore.
- **The clean modern form** rejected the ruined, pre-war city around it.
- **The central location** turned East Berlin's skyline into propaganda.

On paper, it was perfect.

Then it had to be built.

## Construction Timeline: 1965 to 1969

Construction began on **4 August 1965**. The timetable was politically loaded from the beginning, because the GDR wanted the tower ready for the republic's 20th anniversary in October 1969.

That deadline mattered. It was not just a construction date. It was a state birthday.

The basic construction story moved quickly:

- **4 August 1965:** work began on the foundations.
- **15 March 1966:** concreting of the tower base began.
- **4 October 1966:** the shaft passed the 100-metre mark.
- **16 June 1967:** the concrete shaft reached its final height of roughly 249 metres.
- **7 October 1968:** the final sphere segment was installed.
- **3 October 1969:** Walter Ulbricht officially opened the tower.
- **7 October 1969:** the public could visit, on the GDR's 20th anniversary.

The [DDR Museum](https://www.ddr-museum.de/en/blog/2016/berlin-tv-tower) gives a good compact account of the construction sequence: the shaft rose with climbing formwork, 8,000 cubic metres of concrete went into it, and the sphere was assembled from hundreds of pieces.

Those dates make it sound smooth.

It was not smooth.

## The Engineering: Climbing Formwork and the Sphere

The shaft was built using **climbing formwork**. In simple terms, the builders poured concrete in sections, then moved the formwork higher and repeated the process.

This allowed the tower to rise quickly. It also meant the project was highly visible while it was still unfinished. Berliners could watch the GDR's new symbol grow above the city month by month.

The sphere was the harder problem.

You can look at the TV Tower today and forget how strange that ball is. It is not decoration. It contains the observation deck, bar, restaurant and technical areas. It had to be lifted and assembled around a concrete shaft more than 200 metres above ground.

Engineers first tested and prepared the sphere segments on the ground near St. Mary's Church and the Rotes Rathaus. Then cranes lifted the pieces into place.

The official TV Tower history notes that the steel frame of the sphere was prefabricated on the ground before the segments were attached high above the city. The DDR Museum describes the exterior as being assembled from **240 segments**.

That is the part I like to point out from below. The tower looks clean and simple from a distance, but the sphere was a complicated piece of industrial theatre.

It had to look effortless.

It was anything but.

## The Cost: Four Times the Plan

The tower was supposed to prove the strength of East Germany. Instead, it quietly exposed some of its weaknesses.

The original budget was far too optimistic. By the time the project was finished, the construction cost had risen to **over 132 million East German marks**, around four times the original plan. The DDR Museum gives that figure directly, and the [World Federation of Great Towers](https://www.great-towers.com/tower/berliner-fernsehturm) lists the same construction cost in its technical facts.

Some of the problem was complexity. A 368-metre tower with a working sphere is not a cheap object.

But there was also political irony. The GDR built the tower to show socialist self-confidence, yet some materials and components had to be sourced from abroad, including from the West. Even the great socialist showpiece depended on the world it was meant to outperform.

That does not make the tower a failure. It makes it more Berlin.

A symbol is rarely as simple as the regime wants it to be.

## The Water Problem Nobody Wanted

Early in 1969, when the deadline was already close, water leaked into the tower's interior and damaged parts of the structure. The sphere had to be resealed.

For an ordinary building, that would have been stressful. For this building, it was politically dangerous.

The opening date could not casually slide into the future. The tower was meant to stand ready for the GDR anniversary. It had to be proof that the system could deliver.

So the work intensified.

By autumn 1969, the interior and the entrance pavilion were complete enough for the official opening. The result was a remarkable construction achievement, even if the politics around it were heavy-handed.

The building opened on **3 October 1969**, just before the GDR's 20th birthday. Walter Ulbricht inaugurated it, and the event also marked the launch of the GDR's second television channel, DFF 2, bringing colour television into the official story.

The tower was doing exactly what it was built to do.

Then the sun came out.

## The Pope's Revenge

The TV Tower was built by an officially atheist state. That is what makes the next part so funny.

When sunlight hits the sphere, the reflection can form a bright cross. It appears on the metal surface, especially on clear days when the light angle is right.

Berliners nicknamed the effect **Rache des Papstes**, or **the Pope's Revenge**.

The joke was brutal because it hit the GDR exactly where the tower was meant to be strongest. This was supposed to be a secular socialist triumph. Instead, the most visible object in East Berlin sometimes displayed a giant Christian-looking cross above the city.

The authorities did not enjoy the joke.

Stories vary about what was tried to reduce the effect, but the important part is simple: the cross stayed. It is still visible today when the light is right.

That is why I never treat the TV Tower only as architecture. It is also a perfect Berlin story: power builds a symbol, then the city gives it a nickname that power cannot control.

For the full version of that story, read my post on [the Pope's Revenge](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/the-pope-s-revenge-how-east-germany-s-tv-tower-backfired).

## After Reunification: From GDR Symbol to Berlin Symbol

When the Wall fell in 1989 and Germany reunified in 1990, the tower's meaning changed almost overnight.

It had been built as a symbol of East German progress. Suddenly East Germany no longer existed.

Some people saw the tower as a leftover of dictatorship. Others saw it as too useful, too distinctive and too beloved to remove. In the end, Berlin kept it.

That decision was important.

The TV Tower survived because it was no longer only a GDR object. It had become part of the city itself. People used it for orientation. Visitors photographed it. Berliners joked about it. It appeared in films, posters, souvenirs and weather reports.

The tower also changed physically and operationally after reunification. Broadcasting equipment was modernised, visitor areas were updated, and the tower became part of the commercial tourist city rather than the socialist showcase city.

The [official travel site visitBerlin](https://www.visitberlin.de/en/berlin-television-tower) describes the shift clearly: the tower went from a GDR symbol to a symbol of the whole city and, eventually, Germany.

That is a rare transformation.

Most political monuments age badly when the regime behind them disappears. The TV Tower somehow became lighter.

## What the Tower Tells You From the Ground

You do not need to go up the tower to understand it.

From the ground, you can read the whole story if you know what to look for:

- **Stand by Neptune Fountain** and look up at the sphere. This is the best place to talk about the Pope's Revenge.
- **Look toward St. Mary's Church** to see the surviving medieval layer beside the GDR layer.
- **Turn toward Alexanderplatz** and you see the modern socialist centre the tower was meant to dominate.
- **Walk toward Museum Island** and the contrast becomes even sharper: imperial museums, medieval church, socialist tower, modern city.

That mix is why the Fernsehturm works so well on a walking tour. It is not just a landmark above you. It is a map of Berlin's broken timeline.

The tower explains power, technology, propaganda, urban destruction and reunification without needing a museum case.

It just stands there.

## Practical Details Today

If you want to visit the tower, check the official site before booking because opening times, closures and ticket categories can change.

The useful basics are:

- **Height:** 368 metres, making it the tallest structure in Germany.
- **Observation deck:** about 203 metres above ground.
- **Restaurant:** about 207 metres above ground.
- **Lift time:** around 40 seconds to the viewing level.
- **Address:** Panoramastrasse 1A, beside Alexanderplatz.
- **Closest station:** S+U Alexanderplatz.

The current [Berlin.de TV Tower page](https://www.berlin.de/en/attractions-and-sights/3560707-3104052-berlin-tv-tower.en.html) lists adult admission from **EUR 28.50** and notes that the tower is not barrier-free for safety reasons. That accessibility detail matters, so check the official information if anyone in your group has limited mobility.

If you are deciding whether the ticket is worth it, I have a separate honest breakdown here: [Is the Berlin TV Tower Worth It?](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/is-the-berlin-tv-tower-worth-it-an-honest-guide-for-2026)

My short version: go up if the weather is clear and you love high viewpoints. Skip it if you mostly want a great photo of the tower itself, because the best photos are from outside.

## Before, During and After in One View

The best thing about the TV Tower is that you can understand the whole construction story in one view.

Before it, there was the old centre of Berlin, damaged by war and then reshaped by East German planning.

During construction, the GDR poured money, concrete and political pressure into a tower that had to prove something to the world.

After reunification, the symbol changed sides without moving an inch.

It no longer says what Walter Ulbricht wanted it to say. It says something more interesting: Berlin keeps strange things, argues with them, jokes about them, and eventually turns them into part of daily life.

That is why the TV Tower is more than a view.

It is a 368-metre reminder that cities are never finished.
