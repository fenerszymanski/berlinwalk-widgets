# Berlin Slang: 10 Words You'll Only Hear in This City

If you have learned a little standard German before coming to Berlin, you may feel ready for the basics.

Then someone in a bakery says, "Dit is mir wurscht," or a friend tells you the bar is "janz weit draussen," and suddenly your textbook feels very polite and very far away.

Berlin has its own way of speaking. People often call it **Berlinerisch** or **Berliner Schnauze**: direct, dry, clipped, funny, sometimes rough at the edges, and often warmer than it sounds at first.

You do not need to speak Berlinerisch to visit Berlin. You do not even need to speak German well. But recognising a few local words helps the city feel less like a mystery and more like a place with its own rhythm.

This is not a formal dictionary. It is a tourist-friendly field guide to the Berlin words you are most likely to notice in shops, bars, bakeries, streets, jokes and casual conversation.

## First, What Is Berlinerisch?

Berlinerisch is the traditional Berlin dialect or city speech.

It is not the same as standard German with a few jokes added. It changes sounds, shortens words, swaps letters, and carries a whole attitude with it.

You will hear less pure Berlinerisch today than you would have heard decades ago. Berlin is an international city, and many younger Berliners speak standard German, English, Turkish, Arabic, Russian, Polish, Spanish or a mix of languages before they ever use old Berlin dialect.

Still, Berlinerisch has not disappeared.

It survives in older voices, local humour, market banter, bakery counters, BVG announcements that make people smile, old films, neighbourhood names, pub talk and everyday words that everyone in Berlin understands.

The key is not to perform it like a costume.

Listen first. Use a few practical words when they are natural. And remember that Berlin's bluntness is not always hostility. Sometimes it is just the city's way of skipping the decorative packaging.

## The Sound of Berlin in 30 Seconds

Before the word list, it helps to know the sound pattern.

Berlinerisch often changes standard German like this:

- **ich** becomes **ick** or **icke**.
- **das** becomes **dit** or **det**.
- **was** becomes **wat**.
- **gut** becomes **jut**.
- **ganz** becomes **janz**.
- **keine** becomes **keene**.
- **auf** can become **uff**.

So a sentence like:

**Ich habe keine Ahnung, das ist mir egal.**

Can become something closer to:

**Ick hab keene Ahnung, dit is mir wurscht.**

That means: I have no idea, I do not care.

Elegant? No.

Very Berlin? Absolutely.

## 1. Ick / Icke

**Meaning:** I, me, myself.

**Standard German:** ich.

This is the classic Berlin sound.

In standard German, **ich** has a soft sound at the end. In Berlin, that often becomes **ick**. If someone wants to emphasise "me," you may hear **icke**.

Examples:

- **Ick wees nich.** - I do not know.
- **Icke? Nee.** - Me? No.
- **Ick bin Berliner.** - I am a Berliner.

Should you use it?

Maybe once, carefully, if you are joking with someone who is already being friendly. Do not walk into a bakery and perform a full dialect routine. Berliners have survived worse, but there is no need to test them.

For visitors, the useful part is recognition. When you hear **ick**, your brain should translate it to **ich**.

## 2. Dit / Det

**Meaning:** this, that, it.

**Standard German:** das.

**Dit** is one of the easiest Berlin words to notice because it appears everywhere in casual speech.

Examples:

- **Dit is jut.** - That is good.
- **Dit kannste vergessen.** - You can forget that.
- **Wat is dit denn?** - What is that?

You may also hear **det**, depending on the speaker.

This word is useful because it unlocks many short Berlin sentences. If you hear **dit**, do not overthink it. It is usually just **das** wearing a Berlin jacket.

## 3. Keene

**Meaning:** no, none, not any.

**Standard German:** keine.

This is another classic sound shift. The standard **ei** sound becomes **ee**.

Examples:

- **Ick hab keene Ahnung.** - I have no idea.
- **Keene Zeit.** - No time.
- **Keene Lust.** - No desire, not in the mood.

You may hear this in everyday frustration, especially when someone is trying to explain why something is not possible.

Berlin has many ways of saying no. **Keene** is one of the most recognisable.

## 4. Jut

**Meaning:** good.

**Standard German:** gut.

In Berlin speech, a hard **g** at the beginning of some words can become a **j** sound.

So **gut** becomes **jut**.

Examples:

- **Alles jut?** - All good?
- **Jut jemacht.** - Well done.
- **Na jut.** - Alright then.

This is one of the warmer words on the list. Berlin is famous for sounding blunt, but **alles jut** can be surprisingly soft. It is the city shrugging and saying: fine, it works, keep going.

## 5. Kiez

**Meaning:** your neighbourhood, local area, home patch.

This is one of the most important Berlin words because it explains how the city feels.

[Duden](https://www.duden.de/rechtschreibung/Kiez) describes **Kiez** as a north-eastern German word, especially Berlinish, for a district or local area. In everyday Berlin life, it means more than a district on a map.

Your **Kiez** is the few streets you actually live in. The bakery you know. The late-night shop you trust. The park corner where people sit. The tram stop you use without checking signs. The place where the city becomes personal.

Examples:

- **Mein Kiez** - my neighbourhood.
- **Kiezkneipe** - local neighbourhood pub.
- **Kiezladen** - small local shop.

Tourists often say "I stayed in Kreuzberg."

A Berliner might say, "My Kiez is around Graefekiez," or "I am over in the Wrangelkiez."

That tells you something important: Berlin is a big city made of small loyalties.

## 6. Späti

**Meaning:** late-night corner shop.

**Full word:** Spätkauf.

A **Späti** is one of the most useful words in Berlin.

[Duden](https://www.duden.de/rechtschreibung/Spaeti) defines it as a late-sale shop, but that sounds much colder than the real thing. A Späti is where you buy water, beer, snacks, cigarettes, emergency chocolate, a lighter, a phone cable if you are lucky, and sometimes the small thing that rescues your evening.

It is also a social place.

On warm nights, people stand outside Spätis with drinks, talking on the pavement. In some neighbourhoods, the Späti is almost a tiny public square.

Examples:

- **Ich geh kurz zum Späti.** - I am quickly going to the Späti.
- **Gibt's hier einen Späti?** - Is there a Späti around here?
- **Späti-Bier** - a beer bought from a Späti.

I wrote a full guide to [what a Späti is in Berlin](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/what-is-a-spati-berlin) because visitors always notice them, even if they do not know the word yet.

## 7. Berliner Schnauze

**Meaning:** Berlin's blunt, dry, direct communication style.

Literally, **Schnauze** means snout or mouth. In Berlin, **Berliner Schnauze** means the city's famously sharp way of speaking.

It can sound rude if you are not used to it.

A cashier may be direct. A bus driver may bark an instruction. A market seller may answer with a joke that feels like a slap until you realise everyone is smiling.

The important thing: **Berliner Schnauze is not pure rudeness.**

At its best, it is quick, honest, dry and funny. It says what it means without wrapping every sentence in soft paper.

At its worst, yes, it can just be rude. Berlin does not get a free pass.

For visitors, the survival rule is simple: do not take every sharp sentence personally. Listen for the humour. Then answer calmly.

## 8. Wurscht / Wurschtegal

**Meaning:** I do not care, it does not matter.

**Standard German:** egal.

This is one of the funniest expressions on the list.

**Wurst** means sausage. **Wurscht** is a casual form. If something is **mir wurscht**, it is "sausage to me," meaning I do not care.

**Wurschtegal** goes even further: completely irrelevant, totally whatever.

Examples:

- **Dit is mir wurscht.** - I do not care.
- **Ist doch wurscht.** - It does not matter.
- **Wurschtegal.** - Completely whatever.

It is useful, but be careful with tone. Said lightly, it is funny. Said coldly, it sounds dismissive.

Berlin gives you both options.

## 9. Schrippe

**Meaning:** bread roll.

**Standard German:** Brötchen.

This one is practical.

In much of Germany, a small bread roll is a **Brötchen**. In Berlin, you may hear **Schrippe**.

[Duden](https://www.duden.de/rechtschreibung/Schrippe) lists the word, and bakeries in Berlin still use it. You do not need to say it, but it is useful when reading signs or ordering breakfast.

Examples:

- **Eine Schrippe, bitte.** - One bread roll, please.
- **Belegte Schrippe** - filled bread roll.
- **Zwei Schrippen** - two bread rolls.

If you say **Brötchen**, people will understand you.

If you recognise **Schrippe**, you will understand Berlin a little faster.

## 10. JWD / Janz Weit Draussen

**Meaning:** far away, out in the middle of nowhere.

**Standard German:** ganz weit draussen.

This is a perfect Berlin phrase because Berliners can make anywhere outside their comfort zone sound like another planet.

**JWD** stands for **janz weit draussen**, or "really far out there."

Examples:

- **Das ist jwd.** - That is way out there.
- **Der Club ist janz weit draussen.** - The club is really far away.
- **Ist das noch Berlin?** - Is that still Berlin?

A place can be 35 minutes away by U-Bahn and still be called **jwd** by someone who does not want to leave their Kiez.

This is not only about distance. It is about mood.

Berlin is huge, but people live in neighbourhood bubbles. Once you understand **Kiez** and **jwd**, the city map starts to make emotional sense.

## Bonus Words You Will Also See

A few extra words are worth knowing:

- **Bulette:** a Berlin-style meatball. You may see it at snack bars or traditional pubs.
- **Pfand:** bottle deposit. Not Berlin-only, but very useful when buying drinks.
- **Wegbier:** a beer for the road. Common Berlin behaviour, even if the word is broader than Berlin.
- **Molle:** an old Berlin word for beer.
- **Knorke:** old-fashioned slang for great or excellent.

You do not need to memorise all of them.

If you remember **Kiez**, **Späti**, **Schnauze** and **jwd**, you already have a better feel for the city than most first-time visitors.

## Should You Actually Use Berlin Slang?

Sometimes.

But use it gently.

A few words are completely normal for visitors:

- **Kiez**
- **Späti**
- **Pfand**
- **Schrippe**
- **Wegbier**

Other words, such as **ick**, **dit** and **keene**, are better for recognition unless you are speaking with friends. Forced dialect can sound like parody very quickly.

Berliners are used to visitors. Nobody expects you to speak like a local. A simple **Hallo**, **Danke**, **Entschuldigung** and **Sprechen Sie Englisch?** will take you much further than a theatrical "Icke bin Berliner."

## Where You Will Hear These Words

You are most likely to notice Berlin slang in ordinary places:

- At bakeries, especially with **Schrippe**.
- Around late-night shops, especially **Späti**.
- In local bars and pubs.
- At flea markets and street markets.
- From older Berliners.
- In jokes about neighbourhoods.
- In casual signs, posters, stickers and T-shirts.

You may hear less of it in hotel lobbies, museums, chain cafes and international restaurants.

That is normal.

The older dialect is not the only language of Berlin. The city also speaks English, Turkish, Arabic, Russian, Polish, Spanish, club-door silence, bureaucratic German and the universal language of someone blocking the bike lane.

Berlinerisch is one layer.

But it is a good layer to recognise.

## Why Slang Matters on a Berlin Walk

Language is one of the quickest ways to feel a city.

Architecture tells you what a government wanted. Monuments tell you what a state wanted remembered. Slang tells you how people actually move through the day.

That is why I like these words.

**Kiez** tells you Berlin is local.

**Späti** tells you the city is practical and social.

**Schnauze** tells you not to confuse directness with hatred.

**JWD** tells you Berliners can be deeply loyal to a few square blocks.

Together, they make Berlin feel less like a museum and more like a living city.
