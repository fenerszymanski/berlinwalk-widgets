# The Ampelmann: How a Traffic Light Became Berlin's Most Beloved Symbol

There is a small green man with a hat who tells you when to cross the street in Berlin, and he is one of the most loved things in the entire city.

He has his own shops. He is printed on socks, mugs, umbrellas and keyrings. Tourists stop in the middle of the pavement to photograph a pedestrian signal, which is not something that happens in most capitals.

His name is the Ampelmann, and his story is really the story of Berlin itself: **born in the East, almost erased after the Wall fell, and then quietly winning over the whole reunified city**.

I point him out on almost every tour, because once you know where he comes from, you cannot stop seeing him. This is the full story of how a traffic light figure became a symbol of a vanished country, and where you can still meet the original today.

## What Exactly Is the Ampelmännchen?

The Ampelmännchen, the little traffic light man, is the figure inside Berlin's pedestrian signals. There are two of them:

- **The red man** (the Steher, or "the one who stands"): arms stretched wide, blocking your way. He tells you to wait.
- **The green man** (the Geher, or "the one who goes"): striding forward, hat tilted, clearly in a hurry. He tells you to cross.

What makes them special is that they are not the plain, generic stick figures you see in most of the world. They have personality.

The green man looks cheerful and busy. The red man looks almost apologetic about stopping you. That character is not an accident, and it is not decoration. It was designed on purpose, by a man whose entire job was understanding how people behave in traffic.

## Who Created the Ampelmann?

The Ampelmann was born in East Berlin, the capital of the German Democratic Republic, in 1961. His creator was **Karl Peglau**, a traffic psychologist who submitted his proposal on **13 October 1961**.

Peglau had noticed a serious problem on East German streets. The pedestrian signals of the time were weak: small, dim, and using the same plain symbols as everywhere else.

On a busy street, competing with shop windows, advertising and bright sunlight, those little lights simply disappeared. People did not see them, and people who do not see a signal do not obey it.

For a traffic psychologist, that was not a design detail. It was a safety failure that cost lives.

Peglau was not guessing, either. He spent around thirty years as a traffic psychologist inside the East German transport system, studying crossings, accidents and near misses. When he sat down to redesign the signal, he was solving a problem he had watched play out on real streets for decades.

His solution was to make the signal impossible to ignore. Instead of a thin, anonymous symbol, he gave the light a whole human figure that filled the lens with colour and shape. More glowing surface meant a brighter, clearer signal that a child or an older person could read from far away.

He understood something most engineers missed: a sign works better when people actually like looking at it. The Ampelmann was not a doodle. It was applied science dressed up as a friendly cartoon.

## The Design: Why the Hat, the Belly and the Round Hands

Every part of the Ampelmann was chosen for a reason. As a psychologist, Peglau knew that people pay attention to figures they find warm and a little familiar. Here is what he built into the little man, and why:

- **The rounded belly:** an approachable, almost cuddly shape that looks more like a friendly neighbour than a cold warning sign. A signal you like is a signal you actually watch.
- **The hat:** the detail everyone remembers, and it does two jobs. It adds more lit surface to the top of the figure, making the signal bigger and easier to see. Peglau is also said to have been unsure about it until he noticed the East German leader Erich Honecker wearing a straw hat.
- **The round hands:** he first drew proper fingers, but the fine detail was too delicate to reproduce on the glass lenses of the time. The fingers became the friendly paddle-like hands you see today.
- **The direction he walks:** the green man first strode to the right, was later turned to walk left after a survey (a choice often read through the politics of the era), and the modern brand has since turned him back to the right.

None of this was random. It was a psychologist quietly engineering affection into a piece of street furniture.

## From Drawing Board to the Street Corner

Ideas move slowly through any bureaucracy, and it took years for Peglau's figure to reach the public.

The Ampelmännchen finally started directing real traffic in **1969**, and by **1972** he had spread to crossings across the GDR. His first public appearance was at the junction of Unter den Linden and Friedrichstraße, in the historic heart of East Berlin, a corner you can still stand on today.

He did not stay only on the street. In East Germany the Ampelmann became a teaching tool and then a small celebrity, appearing in road safety education for children, in cartoons, and in playful media that taught a generation how to cross the road.

For East German kids, he was not a regulation. He was a character, the cheerful little guardian of the corner.

That is the part outsiders often miss. The Ampelmann was completely ordinary, and that is exactly why he mattered. He was woven into the smallest, most repeated moment of daily life: the pause at a crossing that everyone makes a hundred times a year without thinking.

When something that ordinary belongs to your childhood and your city, losing it does not feel like a change in regulations. It feels like someone quietly removing a piece of home.

## How He Nearly Disappeared

Everything changed on 9 November 1989, when the Berlin Wall fell, and then again in 1990, when Germany reunified.

Suddenly two countries with two different systems had to become one. That meant standardising thousands of small things, including road signs and traffic signals.

The default assumption was simple and brutal: the West German way would become the German way. Across the former East, the distinctive GDR signals were taken down and replaced with the plainer Western figure.

To the planners it was just administration, a tidy harmonisation of the rulebook. To many East Germans it felt like something else entirely. Their everyday world was being switched off, piece by piece, and the friendly little man from the corner was vanishing with it.

For a few years it really looked as if the Ampelmann would survive only in memory. What saved him was not nostalgia alone. It was one designer who saw value where others saw scrap.

## How a West German Designer Saved Him

In the mid 1990s, a designer named **Markus Heckhausen** came to Berlin and noticed crates of discarded East German traffic light covers being thrown out as the old signals were removed.

Instead of rubbish, he saw a design icon about to be lost. He began making lamps from the original light covers, and from that idea the AMPELMANN brand was born **around 1995**.

Those first products were lamps that glowed with the genuine Ampelmann, taken straight from decommissioned signals. People were quite literally buying back a piece of the East that the city had thrown away.

Then he did something honest. He sought out Karl Peglau himself. The two men became close friends and worked together for years, until Peglau's death in 2009. It meant the Ampelmann's second life was built with the blessing of the man who created him, not stolen from him.

At the same time, ordinary people were fighting for the figure. A campaign to **"save the Ampelmännchen"** gathered momentum in the late 1990s, with a committee and real public pressure to keep the Eastern design on the streets.

The combination worked. The Ampelmann was no longer something to be quietly removed. He became something Berlin chose to keep, and then something Berlin was proud of.

## From Traffic Light to Brand Empire

What started with a few lamps grew into a genuine business. AMPELMANN opened shops, expanded into dozens of product lines, and turned the little green man into one of Berlin's defining souvenirs. By the early 2000s the brand was selling around forty product variations, with a reputation that reached far beyond Germany.

This is where the Ampelmann became tangled up with a feeling called **Ostalgie**, a blend of the German words for East and nostalgia.

After reunification, many people from the former East felt their entire way of life had been dismissed as inferior and swept away. The Ampelmann offered a rare, happy piece of that world that everyone could love. Buying an Ampelmann mug was never really about the mug. It was about holding on to something warm from a country that no longer existed.

There is a quiet irony here that I always enjoy. An Eastern safety design, rescued by a Western designer, sold to visitors from all over the world, in a city that spent decades split in two.

The Ampelmann shop has become one of the few places in Berlin where the old East and West story has a genuinely happy ending. Nobody loses, the little man wins, and everyone goes home with an umbrella.

Today he is impossible to escape. The green man waves from fridge magnets, T-shirts, bottle openers and children's books, in shop windows from the airport to Museum Island. A figure designed to be noticed has ended up being noticed absolutely everywhere.

## The Ampelfrau and the Spread Across Germany

Success invites company. In 2004 a designer named Joachim Roßberg created the **Ampelfrau**, a female counterpart with a skirt and pigtails, first installed in cities including Zwickau, Dresden and Fürstenwalde.

She sparked her own debates about tradition, equality and whether a traffic light needs a gender at all, which is a very Berlin kind of argument to have about a lamp.

The Eastern figure also did something nobody expected: he travelled west. Rather than the Western symbol erasing the Eastern one, the charming GDR design spread into former West German cities such as Saarbrücken and Heidelberg.

Other towns went further and made him their own. Fulda introduced a version based on Saint Boniface, and Coburg gave its little man a local bratwurst to carry. The figure Peglau drew in 1961 had become a template that proud cities wanted to copy.

The little man who was supposed to be replaced ended up conquering the country that replaced his homeland.

## Why the Ampelmann Really Matters

It is easy to treat all of this as a cute story about a logo. But the Ampelmann carries real weight.

He is, by a wide margin, **the most successful everyday object to survive the end of East Germany**. Almost everything else from GDR daily life was replaced, forgotten, or turned into an ironic joke. The Ampelmann was kept, loved and exported.

There is even evidence that the East simply did the design better. A study by **Jacobs University in Bremen in 2013** found the bold, high-contrast Eastern figure was more effective and easier to recognise than the thinner Western version.

The friendly little man was not just sentimental. He worked.

That is why I find him such a useful character on a walk. He is small enough to miss and large enough to explain an entire era.

Most of the obvious markers of the GDR are gone or behind glass in a museum. The checkpoints are memorials. The Wall is mostly a painted line in the pavement. The grand socialist buildings have been renamed and repurposed.

The Ampelmann is different. He is still doing his original job, on a real street corner, for free, every ninety seconds. There are not many places left where you can touch the divided city simply by waiting to cross the road.

## Wait for the Green Man: Berlin's Crossing Culture

If you spend a day in Berlin, you will notice something. **Locals often wait for the green man even when the street is completely empty** and there is not a car in sight.

Part of it is the German respect for rules. Part of it is the quiet authority of that little figure, especially when children are watching. Step out on red while a parent waits beside you, and you will feel the look.

You do not have to join in. But once you know the Ampelmann's story, waiting for him stops feeling like obeying a rule. It starts to feel like a small, friendly Berlin ritual.

## Where to See the Real Ampelmännchen in Berlin

If you want to meet the original, look down, and pay attention to which part of the city you are standing in. As a rule of thumb, the Ampelmännchen lives in the former East. The best places to spot him are all in the historic centre:

- **Unter den Linden:** including the original 1969 crossing at Friedrichstraße, where he first appeared.
- **Around [Alexanderplatz](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/the-weltzeituhr-why-alexanderplatz-has-a-world-clock):** the busy heart of the old East, and where my walking tour begins.
- **The streets of Mitte:** quieter corners where he is easy to photograph without a crowd.

There is one trick worth trying. If you cross between the former [East and West](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/how-berlin-was-divided-a-simple-guide-to-east-vs-west), you can sometimes watch the figure change from the cheerful hatted man to the plainer Western version. It is an invisible border, drawn in pedestrian signals.

You will not have to hunt for him. On a single walk through Mitte you will pass dozens of them, which is part of the charm. He is not a monument you queue for. He is just there, doing his job hundreds of times a day.

For the best photo, wait for the green man and shoot straight on as he glows against a darker background. It is a small, free, very Berlin souvenir that no shop can sell you.

## Where to Find the AMPELMANN Shops

When you do want to take him home, the brand has several shops and cafés in the centre, all easy to reach on foot:

- **Flagship store, Unter den Linden 35:** right on the grand boulevard between the Brandenburg Gate and Museum Island.
- **Hackesche Höfe, Rosenthaler Straße 40 to 41:** a lovely shop tucked into the courtyards near [Hackescher Markt](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/hackescher-markt-before-and-after-from-rubble-to-berlin-s-coolest-neighborhood), one of the prettiest corners to browse.
- **Alexa mall and the Kurfürstendamm:** smaller shops and cafés, by Alexanderplatz and out in the West.

Inside, you will find everything from sensible umbrellas to slightly absurd gummy sweets shaped like the little man.

Even if you buy nothing, the shops are worth a look. They show how completely a humble safety signal has been turned into a piece of Berlin identity. For more on the official history and products, the [AMPELMANN](https://www.ampelmann.de/en/) brand tells its own side of the story.
