# Alexanderplatz Then and Now: From Medieval Market to Modern Chaos

Alexanderplatz is not Berlin's prettiest square.

That is usually the first honest thing to say about it.

It is loud, wide, exposed, full of trains, trams, shopping centres, fast food, tour groups, office workers, street performers and people who are very clearly trying to find someone under the World Clock.

But if you only judge Alexanderplatz by beauty, you miss the point. **Alex is not a postcard square. It is a cross-section of Berlin.**

Stand there for ten minutes and you are standing on the edge of medieval Berlin, the market city, the imperial capital, the Weimar metropolis, the bombed ruin, the socialist showcase, the protest stage of 1989 and the commercial hub of reunified Berlin.

Few places in the city carry so many versions of Berlin at once.

That is why I like starting my walk here. Alexanderplatz looks chaotic because Berlin's history is chaotic. Once you learn how to read it, the mess begins to make sense.

## What Alexanderplatz Is Today

Today, Alexanderplatz is one of Berlin's busiest public spaces and transport hubs.

U-Bahn, S-Bahn, trams, buses, regional trains and crowds all meet here. The [Berlin TV Tower](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/the-tv-tower-10-things-you-didn-t-know-about-berlin-s-most-famous-landmark) rises above it. The [Weltzeituhr](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/the-weltzeituhr-why-alexanderplatz-has-a-world-clock) turns slowly at street level. The Rotes Rathaus, St. Mary's Church and Nikolaiviertel sit just beyond the square.

For visitors, Alex often feels like a practical place: meet here, change trains here, buy something here, then move on.

For Berlin, it is more than that. Official city guides describe it as a major meeting place and tourist starting point, and [Berlin.de](https://www.berlin.de/en/attractions-and-sights/3560109-3104052-alexanderplatz.en.html) still frames it through its long history as a market, parade ground and central square. [VisitBerlin](https://www.visitberlin.de/en/alexanderplatz) points to the same strange mix: GDR landmarks, shopping, transit and modern city life all piled into one place.

That pile-up is the real story.

Alexanderplatz is a square that keeps being redesigned without ever becoming calm.

## Before Alexanderplatz Had a Name

Long before the TV Tower, long before the department stores, long before the word "Alexanderplatz", this area sat outside the old town of Berlin.

Medieval Berlin was not a grand capital. It began as two small trading settlements, Berlin and Cölln, joined by the Spree and by practical needs: river crossings, tolls, markets, craftsmen, churches and walls.

The area that became Alexanderplatz was outside the old city gate on the north-eastern approach into town. It was useful because it was just beyond the tight medieval centre. That is where animals, carts, wool, grain and people could gather before entering the city.

This is the first important thing to understand: **Alexanderplatz began as an edge, not a centre.**

It was not built to impress visitors. It was a working space. A market space. A threshold.

That older geography still matters. When you walk from Alexanderplatz toward St. Mary's Church, Nikolaiviertel and the Spree, you are not wandering randomly. You are walking from the old outside edge toward the core of medieval Berlin.

That is why the square makes more sense on foot than it does from a train platform.

## From Gate to Alexanderplatz

The square's modern name arrived in 1805.

King Frederick William III renamed it in honour of Russian Tsar Alexander I, who visited Berlin during the Napoleonic period. That diplomatic gesture gave the place a name that sounded grander than its daily function.

But for much of the 19th century, Alexanderplatz was still practical rather than elegant.

It served as a market, a parade and drill ground, and a growing transport node. Farmers and merchants used it. Soldiers drilled there. Berlin expanded around it as industrialisation pulled more people into the city.

By the late 1800s, the old edge had become central.

This is a pattern Berlin repeats often. A place starts as a margin, then the city grows around it until the margin becomes the middle.

The 1889 image of Alexanderplatz is useful because it shows a city already in motion. It is not medieval anymore, but it is not yet the roaring 20th-century square either. You can still read a lower, denser, more street-based Berlin.

The later open plaza had not yet swallowed the scene.

## The Department Store City

Around 1900, Alexanderplatz entered one of its great urban phases.

Berlin was becoming a huge industrial capital, and Alex became one of its busy commercial hearts. Department stores, hotels, restaurants, electric trams and railway connections changed the rhythm of the square.

This was the age when shopping became spectacle.

Big stores did not simply sell goods. They taught people how to look, desire, compare and move through the modern city. Alexanderplatz was perfect for that because it was noisy, connected and democratic. It was not only for the aristocratic west or the imperial boulevard. It was a square for workers, clerks, shoppers, travellers and night crowds.

The writer Alfred Döblin captured that atmosphere in his 1929 novel *Berlin Alexanderplatz*. The book did not make the square famous by describing it as orderly. It made it famous by catching its overload: advertising, traffic, voices, temptation, poverty, ambition and motion.

That is still one of the best ways to understand Alex.

It has never been a quiet square. It has always been a machine for urban energy.

## War Turns the City into Rubble

The Second World War broke that world apart.

Bombing and the final Battle of Berlin devastated the area around Alexanderplatz. The dense pre-war streets, commercial buildings and older urban fabric were badly damaged or destroyed.

After 1945, Berlin was not simply repairing a square. It was deciding what kind of city should replace the ruins.

That question became political very quickly.

Alexanderplatz ended up in East Berlin, the capital centre of the German Democratic Republic. The GDR did not want to rebuild the old commercial square exactly as it had been. It wanted to create a new socialist city centre.

That decision is why the square feels so different from the older city just a few minutes away.

Instead of tight streets and shopfronts, planners created a wide, open, modernist space. The goal was scale, clarity, mass movement and symbolic confidence. It was a city centre designed for a state that wanted to look modern, collective and future-facing.

You can still feel that decision under your feet.

## The GDR Redesign

The 1960s were the key decade for the Alexanderplatz you see today.

East German planners remade the square and the wider city centre around it. The most famous result was the Fernsehturm, opened in 1969. At 368 metres, it became the most visible landmark in East Berlin and remains Germany's tallest structure. [VisitBerlin's TV Tower history](https://www.visitberlin.de/en/berlin-television-tower) describes how the tower was presented as a symbol of socialist progress.

The same year, the World Clock arrived.

The [Berlin.de World Clock page](https://www.berlin.de/en/attractions-and-sights/3561749-3104052-world-clock.en.html) notes that Erich John designed it during the socialist redesign of Alexanderplatz, and that it was presented to the public on 30 September 1969. The clock has since become one of Berlin's easiest meeting points, which is exactly why it works so well at the start of my tour.

The GDR also added the open pedestrian plaza, large modern buildings, the Centrum department store and a new sense of monumental emptiness.

To some people, this was progress.

To others, it erased too much.

Both reactions are understandable. The redesign gave Berlin one of its clearest East German urban landscapes, but it also flattened much of the older street feeling. Alex became bigger, brighter and more ideological.

It also became less intimate.

## The Square of 1989

Alexanderplatz was not only a showcase for the GDR. In 1989, it became one of the places where the GDR publicly began to lose control.

On 4 November 1989, hundreds of thousands of people gathered at Alexanderplatz to demand freedom of speech, freedom of the press and political reform. [Berlin.de's history of the fall of the Wall](https://www.berlin.de/en/history/8482223-8619314-the-fall-of-the-wall-and-reunification.en.html) describes more than 500,000 East Berliners gathering there just five days before the Wall opened.

That detail matters.

The same square built to project state confidence became a stage for citizens demanding that the state listen.

This is one of the reasons I never treat Alexanderplatz as just ugly concrete. It carries one of the great reversals of modern German history.

The open space that once served official mass politics became a space for public courage.

## After Reunification: Capitalism Returns

After 1990, Alexanderplatz changed again.

The socialist centre was suddenly part of a reunified capitalist city. Shops changed. Ads changed. Ownership changed. Buildings were renovated, rebranded or replaced. The square became a place where East German memory, global retail and Berlin's new tourism economy collided.

That is why Alex can feel so visually confused today.

You see GDR scale, post-reunification shopping, tourist infrastructure, transport engineering, unfinished planning dreams and bits of older Berlin all competing in the same view.

Berlin has been trying to solve Alexanderplatz for decades.

The Senate's official [Alexanderplatz urban development page](https://www.berlin.de/sen/stadtentwicklung/staedtebau/berliner-mitte/alexanderplatz/) traces the 1993 international planning competition and the 1994 Kollhoff and Timmermann masterplan. The idea was to repair the square's urban edges and add high-rise buildings around it.

That project has only partly happened.

Even now, Alexanderplatz remains a planning argument in public. A 2025 Berlin Senate notice on the [Bebauungsplan I-B4a-3](https://www.berlin.de/sen/stadtentwicklung/planung/bebauungsplanverfahren/bebauungsplanverfahren-der-senatsverwaltung/oeffentliche-auslegung/bebauungsplan-i-b4a-3-1649387.php) still discusses the planned location of a 150-metre high-rise near the square.

In other words: **Alexanderplatz is not finished.**

It probably never will be.

## Why Alexanderplatz Feels So Chaotic

Most Berlin squares have one dominant story.

Pariser Platz is the Brandenburg Gate. Gendarmenmarkt is classical elegance. Potsdamer Platz is post-Wall corporate Berlin. Bebelplatz is memory and book burning. Museum Island is monarchy, archaeology and culture.

Alexanderplatz is different.

It has too many stories to behave neatly.

Here are the main layers you are feeling when you stand there:

- **Medieval edge:** the approach to the old town, market life and the route toward the historic centre.
- **Prussian naming:** the 1805 honouring of Tsar Alexander I.
- **Industrial city:** trains, commerce, department stores and the rise of mass urban Berlin.
- **Weimar intensity:** the noisy modern square captured by Alfred Döblin.
- **Wartime rupture:** bombing, battle damage and the loss of older streets.
- **GDR urban planning:** the TV Tower, World Clock, open plaza and socialist centre.
- **1989 protest:** the square as a stage for democratic pressure.
- **Reunified Berlin:** shopping centres, investment, tourism, transit and unfinished high-rise plans.

That is a lot for one square.

No wonder it feels restless.

## How to Read Alexanderplatz on Foot

The trick is not to look for one perfect view.

Alexanderplatz rewards movement more than stillness. Start at the World Clock, then slowly turn.

Look up at the TV Tower and you see the GDR's claim to the future.

Look toward St. Mary's Church and you see medieval Berlin surviving in the tower's shadow.

Look toward the Rotes Rathaus and you see 19th-century civic power beside socialist open space.

Look toward Nikolaiviertel and the Spree and you get the older city structure back.

Look at the shops and station entrances and you see Berlin's daily practical life doing what it always does: moving, buying, waiting, meeting, crossing, leaving.

That is why I prefer to read Alex as a threshold.

It is the place where visitors arrive, but also the place where Berlin's older story begins to open.

## What Still Survives Nearby

If you want the old Alexanderplatz, do not search for a preserved medieval square. It is gone.

Search for fragments instead.

St. Mary's Church is one of the strongest. It stands near the TV Tower like a quiet reminder that Berlin existed long before the GDR wanted a modern skyline.

The Rotes Rathaus gives you the 19th-century city. Its red brick has nothing to do with communism, which is why I like pointing it out when people assume every red thing in East Berlin must be political.

Nikolaiviertel is more complicated. It looks old, but much of what you see is a GDR-era reconstruction from the 1980s. That does not make it fake in a useless way. It makes it a memory project, a rebuilt idea of old Berlin.

The Spree crossings matter too. Berlin grew because this was a useful place to cross, tax, trade and control movement. The river is not a scenic extra. It is part of the city's original logic.

## Then and Now: What Changed Most

The biggest change is not the skyline.

The biggest change is the scale of public space.

Old Alexanderplatz was denser. Streets pressed in. Buildings defined edges. Commerce happened at closer range. The square belonged to the ground-level city.

Modern Alexanderplatz is much wider, more exposed and more vertical. The TV Tower pulls your eye upward. The open plaza spreads the crowd out. The station system runs underneath and around everything. The buildings sit farther apart than they would in a traditional European square.

That is why Alex can feel like a gap rather than a room.

But that gap is historical.

It is the result of war damage, socialist planning, traffic systems and post-reunification development all layered over an older city that still peeks through at the edges.

So the question is not "Why is Alexanderplatz so ugly?"

The better question is: **What kind of history would create a square like this?**

The answer is Berlin.

## My Honest Advice for Visiting Alexanderplatz

Do not make Alexanderplatz your romantic Berlin moment.

Make it your orientation point.

Meet at the World Clock. Look at the TV Tower. Notice the width of the square. Walk toward St. Mary's Church. Cross toward Nikolaiviertel. Follow the older city back toward the river.

That route turns Alexanderplatz from a stressful transport hub into a readable historical map.

It also helps you understand [East and West Berlin](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/how-berlin-was-divided-a-simple-guide-to-east-vs-west) without needing a museum first. The difference is in the space itself: the broad East German centre, the surviving older fragments and the post-1990 commercial layer all sit together.

If you have time, combine Alex with the [TV Tower construction story](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-tv-tower-construction-the-cold-war-project-that-changed-the-city-s-skyline) and the World Clock. Those two landmarks explain why 1969 was such an important year for this part of Berlin.

Then walk.

The square is only the beginning.
