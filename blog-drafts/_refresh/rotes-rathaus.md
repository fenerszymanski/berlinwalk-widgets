# Rotes Rathaus: Why Berlin's City Hall Is Red (And It Has Nothing to Do With Communism)

The first question people ask about Berlin's Red City Hall is wonderfully predictable.

Is it red because of communism?

It is a fair question. The building stands in the former East Berlin. It sits near Alexanderplatz, the TV Tower and the broad socialist city centre planned by the GDR. Its colour is deep red. Its name is literally **Rotes Rathaus**, or Red Town Hall.

But the answer is much simpler, and much older.

The Rotes Rathaus is red because it is made of red brick.

That sounds almost too easy, but it is the point. The name has nothing to do with Marx, Lenin, the GDR or the Cold War. The building was already red in the 1860s, long before East Germany existed and before communism became a government system anywhere in Europe.

And once you know that, the building becomes more interesting, not less. It is not a communist symbol that happens to look old. It is a 19th-century civic palace that later found itself in the middle of 20th-century ideology.

## Where Is the Rotes Rathaus?

The Rotes Rathaus stands at Rathausstraße 15, just south of Alexanderplatz and beside the medieval core of old Berlin.

If you walk from the World Clock toward St. Mary's Church, Nikolaiviertel or Museum Island, you will almost certainly pass it. The building is large, symmetrical and hard to miss, with a central tower rising above the red-brick facade.

For visitors, it works best as a stop between Alexanderplatz and the historic centre.

Alexanderplatz gives you modern Berlin and the GDR skyline. Nikolaiviertel and the old town area give you medieval Berlin, even if much of it has been reconstructed. The Rotes Rathaus sits between them like a hinge.

That location is important. Berlin's city government did not put its town hall on a quiet back street. It placed it in the old civic heart, close to trade routes, churches, markets and administrative power.

Today it is still the seat of the Governing Mayor of Berlin and the Berlin Senate. [Berlin.de's official visitor page](https://www.berlin.de/en/attractions-and-sights/3559880-3104052-red-town-hall.en.html) describes it as one of Berlin's famous landmarks and confirms the simple reason for the name: the bright red brick facade.

## So Why Is It Actually Red?

The short answer is brick.

The longer answer is that Berlin and Brandenburg have a long tradition of brick architecture. Stone was not always easy or cheap to get in this region, so brick became a practical and expressive building material.

By the 19th century, brick was no longer only practical. It could be prestigious too. The Rotes Rathaus was designed to show that Berlin was becoming a serious modern city with its own civic confidence.

The building was constructed between **1861 and 1869** according to designs by Hermann Friedrich Waesemann. The [Senate Chancellery's Red Town Hall page](https://www.berlin.de/rbmskzl/en/the-red-town-hall/) calls it Waesemann's most important building and describes it as Neo-Renaissance.

That timing matters.

The German Empire was proclaimed in 1871, after the building was finished. The Russian Revolution happened in 1917. East Germany was founded in 1949.

So the colour cannot have started as a communist reference. The building was already red before those political meanings arrived.

This is one of my favourite Berlin misunderstandings because it reveals how easily the 20th century can swallow everything before it. In Berlin, people often see the Cold War first. But the city is older than its Cold War image.

## A City Hall Built for a Growing Capital

The old town hall could not keep up with 19th-century Berlin.

The city was expanding fast. Industry, railway connections, migration and administration were changing Berlin from a Prussian capital into a large European metropolis. The municipal government needed a building that could handle that growth and symbolise it.

The result was not modest.

The Rotes Rathaus was built around a square plan with four wings and open courtyards. It had ceremonial rooms, administrative offices and a tower visible from far away.

Berlin.de notes that the first meeting of the municipal government took place there just four years after the foundation stone was laid. A commemorative Berlin Senate PDF for the building's 150th anniversary adds another important date: the city council met in the almost completed town hall on **6 January 1870**.

That means the building was not just decoration. It was a working symbol of municipal government.

It told Berliners something clear: the city had become big enough, rich enough and self-confident enough to build a town hall that looked like power.

## The Tower: Not as Tall as the TV Tower, But Older

The tower is the easiest part of the building to notice.

It rises above the facade, with a large clock and a silhouette that feels almost medieval from a distance. Official sources describe its height in two ways. Berlin.de's tourist page says the tower is almost **74 meters** high, while the Senate Chancellery page gives **94 meters** to the top of the flagpole.

Either way, it was meant to be seen.

That matters because the Rotes Rathaus now stands near the Berlin TV Tower, which is far taller and much more famous. The contrast is perfect: a 19th-century civic tower beside a 20th-century socialist television tower.

The two towers say different things.

The Rotes Rathaus tower says city government, local pride and brick-built permanence. The TV Tower says modernity, technology and East Berlin ambition.

If you want to understand the area around Alexanderplatz, look at both together. They are not competing landmarks as much as two political eras in one skyline.

## The Terracotta Chronicle Around the Facade

Most people look at the colour and the tower. The best detail is lower down.

Around the facade is a long terracotta frieze known as the **Chronicle in Stone**. It is easy to miss if you cross the street too quickly.

The 150-year Senate PDF explains that the frieze shows key events and developments in Berlin's political and cultural history from the **12th century** to the end of the 19th century. Four Berlin sculptors worked on the **36 clay panels from 1877 to 1879**.

This is the part of the building that makes me slow down.

The city did not just build offices. It wrapped its own history around the outside of the building, almost like a comic strip in terracotta.

The panels include scenes of medieval city life, trade, justice, Prussian rulers and cultural figures. The final panel refers to the founding of the German Empire in 1871.

That last point is important. The frieze is not politically neutral. It tells a 19th-century story about Berlin rising into power, order and national importance.

It is not communist propaganda. It is civic self-advertising from the age of empire.

The same Senate source notes that **nine panels were destroyed in the Second World War**. Eight could be reconstructed from old images, while one was redesigned.

So even the decorative band around the building has its own before-and-after story.

## War Damage and Reconstruction

The Rotes Rathaus survived, but not untouched.

During the Second World War, the building was heavily damaged by air raids. The 150-year Senate PDF says the town hall sustained severe damage in February and March **1945**, and that part of the nearby Altes Stadthaus was also destroyed.

That damage matters because the building you see today is both historic and reconstructed.

It is not a fake. It is not a new building wearing an old costume. But it is also not untouched 1860s fabric. Like so much of central Berlin, it carries repair work inside its identity.

This is one of the great Berlin truths: survival here often means reconstruction.

The building was rebuilt after the war and became part of East Berlin's political centre. That is where the communist association enters the story, but only as a later chapter.

The bricks gave the building its name. The Cold War gave the name a second meaning.

## East Berlin's City Hall

After the war, Berlin was divided into sectors, and the Rotes Rathaus stood in the Soviet sector.

During the Cold War, East Berlin's government used the Rotes Rathaus. West Berlin's Senate met at Schöneberg Town Hall, the same building where John F. Kennedy gave his famous "Ich bin ein Berliner" speech in 1963.

This created a strange historical coincidence.

A building named for red bricks became the political seat of a communist-led city government. The colour suddenly looked ideological, even though it had not started that way.

That is why the myth makes sense. It is wrong, but it is not random.

If you arrived in East Berlin in the 1970s and saw the red town hall beside the socialist redevelopment of Alexanderplatz, the association would be obvious. The TV Tower, broad roads, state buildings and open squares all made the area feel like a capital of the GDR.

But the town hall was older than that world. It had outlived the Prussian kingdom, the German Empire, the Weimar Republic, Nazi Germany, wartime destruction and the early years of occupation before it became East Berlin's city hall.

The building is red, but its story is not one colour.

## Reunification and the Return of Berlin's Government

After German reunification, Berlin had to stitch together two governments, two symbolic centres and two political memories.

The Rotes Rathaus became the joint seat of government again in **1991**. The Senate Chancellery's 150-year PDF gives the exact date: **1 October 1991**, when it became the official seat of the Governing Mayor of Berlin.

That return was practical, but it was also symbolic.

Berlin did not move its unified government into a neutral new building. It moved back into a town hall that had survived empire, dictatorship, division and reconstruction.

That is why the building matters.

It is not just a pretty red facade beside Alexanderplatz. It is one of the few buildings in the area that lets you connect the 19th-century city, the wartime city, the divided city and the reunified city without changing location.

If you are trying to understand central Berlin, that is gold.

## Can You Go Inside?

Usually, yes, but with an important caveat.

The Rotes Rathaus is an active government building, not a museum. Berlin.de says the building and exhibitions can generally be visited Monday to Friday from **9 am to 6 pm**, with free admission, but it may close temporarily for daily operations or security reasons.

That means you should treat the interior as a bonus, not a guaranteed stop.

If it is open, the scale is larger than the facade suggests. Berlin.de says the building has **247 rooms**, and the rooms worth knowing about include:

- **Wappensaal:** the Hall of Arms, named for the coats of arms in the windows.
- **Main Hall:** once used by the East Berlin Magistrat, now used for receptions and ceremonies.
- **Hall of Pillars:** a beautiful space with a nine-meter-high ceiling, now used for exhibitions and events.

The official Red Town Hall page also says about **80,000 visitors** come each year for exhibitions, tours and ceremonies.

If the doors are open and you have ten spare minutes, step inside. You do not need a long visit to feel the difference between the stern exterior and the more ceremonial rooms within.

## What to Notice Outside

If you only have two minutes, look for three things.

First, look at the colour. It is not paint. It is brick, and that brick is the whole reason for the name.

Second, look for the terracotta frieze. Stand across the street if you can, then scan the band of panels. Once you see it, the facade stops being just a red wall and becomes a history strip.

Third, turn around.

This is the trick most visitors miss. The Rotes Rathaus is not only interesting by itself. It is interesting because of what surrounds it.

St. Mary's Church is nearby, one of the oldest surviving churches in Berlin's historic centre. Nikolaiviertel hints at medieval Berlin, even though much of it was reconstructed in the GDR period. Alexanderplatz and the TV Tower stand just behind you.

That is why this stop works so well on foot. In one small area, you can discuss medieval Berlin, 19th-century civic ambition, wartime destruction, GDR urban planning and reunification.

## Why the Myth Persists

The myth persists because it feels right.

Red building. Former East Berlin. Communist government. TV Tower nearby. It is an easy mental shortcut.

But Berlin is rarely that tidy.

The better version is this: the building was named for its red-brick facade in the 19th century, then history made the name feel political in the 20th century.

That is more interesting than the myth, because it shows how meanings change.

Buildings do not always keep the same message. A town hall built for a growing 19th-century city can become an East Berlin seat of power, then a reunified capital's government building, then a tourist landmark that people photograph on the way to Museum Island.

The brick stayed red. The politics around it kept changing.

That is Berlin in one sentence.
