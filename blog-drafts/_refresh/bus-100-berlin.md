# Bus 100 Berlin: The EUR 4 Sightseeing Route Locals Actually Use

Bus 100 is one of the best cheap tricks in Berlin.

It is a normal public bus, not a tourist bus. It has no guide, no recorded commentary, no souvenir map and no bright red open roof. But it runs straight through the middle of the city, from Zoologischer Garten in the west to Alexanderplatz in the east, passing a ridiculous number of landmarks on the way.

For the price of a standard Berlin AB ticket, you can see the Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church, Tiergarten, the Victory Column, Bellevue Palace, the Reichstag, the Brandenburg Gate, Unter den Linden, Museum Island, the Berliner Dom and the TV Tower.

That does not mean Bus 100 replaces a real tour. It does not tell you the stories. It does not explain why the city looks the way it does. It does not help you understand the Wall, Prussia, the GDR or the scars hiding in plain sight.

But for a first overview, a rainy-hour backup, a cheap ride between two useful parts of town, or a smart way to rest your feet, **Bus 100 is hard to beat**.

## What Is Bus 100 in Berlin?

Bus 100 is a regular BVG public bus route that connects **S+U Zoologischer Garten** with **S+U Alexanderplatz**.

The official [BVG route page for Bus 100](https://www.bvg.de/en/connections/route-overview/100) lists the line as running between Zoo and Alexanderplatz, entirely inside Berlin fare zone A. The full ride takes around 30 minutes in normal traffic.

That route is what makes it special. It cuts across the old symbolic line between West Berlin and East Berlin, then rolls through the government quarter and the historic centre.

[BVG itself calls Bus 100](https://www.bvg.de/en/tourists/sightseeing) the classic sightseeing route, listing sights such as the Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church, Victory Column, Bellevue Palace, Brandenburg Gate, Unter den Linden, Neue Wache, Berlin Cathedral and TV Tower.

So no, this is not a hidden secret in the dramatic sense. Berliners know it. Tourist offices know it. BVG knows it.

The real trick is simpler: many visitors still pay far more for a sightseeing bus before realizing that one of the city's best overview routes is already part of public transport.

## Is Bus 100 Worth It?

**Yes, Bus 100 is worth it if you want a cheap visual overview of central Berlin.**

It is especially useful if you have just arrived and want to understand the city's geography. Berlin can feel spread out and confusing on a first day. Bus 100 gives you a quick mental map: City West, Tiergarten, government quarter, Brandenburg Gate, Unter den Linden, Museum Island and Alexanderplatz.

It is also useful when the weather turns unpleasant. Berlin rain has a special talent for arriving exactly when you planned a long walk. Bus 100 lets you keep seeing the city while staying dry.

The catch is that you only get the surface. You see landmarks, not context.

That is why I like Bus 100 as a companion to a walking tour, not as a replacement for one. Ride it for orientation. Walk later for the stories.

## The Route: What You See From West to East

The classic direction is **Zoologischer Garten to Alexanderplatz**. That gives you a nice story arc from West Berlin into the old eastern centre.

Here is the route in plain tourist language:

- **Zoologischer Garten and Breitscheidplatz:** the City West starting point, with the Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church and the shopping streets around Kurfuerstendamm nearby.
- **Tiergarten:** Berlin's huge central park, with green views on both sides as the bus cuts through the city.
- **Victory Column:** the golden figure at Grosser Stern, one of the best photo stops if you decide to get off.
- **Schloss Bellevue:** the official residence of the German president.
- **Government quarter:** the bus passes near the Haus der Kulturen der Welt, Platz der Republik and the Reichstag.
- **Brandenburg Gate:** the most famous landmark in Germany, seen as the bus reaches Unter den Linden.
- **Unter den Linden:** Berlin's grand boulevard, with embassies, Humboldt University, the State Opera and the Neue Wache nearby.
- **Museum Island and Berliner Dom:** the most dramatic stretch, especially if you get a clear view toward the cathedral dome.
- **Alexanderplatz:** the eastern terminus, under the TV Tower and close to the World Clock.

That is a lot of Berlin for one bus ticket.

If you only have time for one short ride, I would choose the stretch from **Zoologischer Garten to Alexanderplatz**, not the other way around. The movement from West to East makes the city easier to read.

## The Best Seat on Bus 100

If a double-decker bus arrives, go upstairs.

Do not panic if it is not a double-decker. Bus assignments can change, and the route is still useful either way. But the upper deck is obviously the prize.

For the west-to-east direction, I prefer the **right side upstairs** for several key views, including parts of the government quarter and the approach toward the Brandenburg Gate area. If you start at Alexanderplatz and go west, try the left side.

The front row is fun if you can get it, but it is not worth fighting over. Berlin is not improved by elbowing strangers for a bus seat.

The more important tip is to start at one of the termini if you care about the view. Boarding at Zoologischer Garten or Alexanderplatz gives you the best chance of finding a seat before the bus fills up.

## Ticket Price in 2026

In 2026, a standard Berlin AB single ticket costs **EUR 4**.

The [BVG ticket overview](https://www.bvg.de/en/subscriptions-and-tickets/all-tickets) lists the single ticket from EUR 4 and the 24-hour AB ticket from EUR 11.20. The official Berlin.de public transport guide gives the same 2026 prices and explains that a single ticket is valid for one person for a two-hour one-way journey.

That last part matters.

An AB single ticket lets you travel in one direction for up to two hours. You can interrupt the journey, get off, and continue in the same general direction. It does **not** cover a round trip or a return journey.

So if you ride Bus 100 from Zoo to Alexanderplatz, walk around, then ride back to Zoo later, you need another ticket unless you have a valid day ticket, tourist pass or Deutschlandticket.

## How to Buy and Validate Your Ticket

You can buy Berlin public transport tickets in several ways:

- **BVG ticket machines** in U-Bahn stations.
- **S-Bahn ticket machines** in S-Bahn stations.
- **The BVG app**, which is the easiest option if your phone is set up for payment.
- **Contactless payment on buses**, depending on the vehicle and terminal.
- **Ticket counters** in larger stations.

Berlin.de notes that bus payments can be made by girocard, major credit cards, Apple Pay, Google Pay or BVG prepaid card, but **cash is not accepted on buses**.

If you buy a paper ticket before boarding, stamp it before or at the start of your journey. Unvalidated paper tickets are treated as invalid.

The fine for travelling without a valid ticket is **EUR 60**. Inspectors are often in plain clothes, and "I am a tourist" is not a magic spell.

My simplest advice: if you are doing more than Bus 100 that day, buy a 24-hour AB ticket and relax. If Bus 100 is your only public transport ride, a single AB ticket is enough.

## Bus 100 vs Hop-On Hop-Off Tours

Bus 100 wins on price.

A hop-on hop-off ticket can cost many times more than a normal Berlin transport ticket. It usually gives you an audio guide, a broader tourist loop, easier landmark labeling, and sometimes a more comfortable sightseeing setup.

Bus 100 gives you none of that.

What it gives you is frequency, authenticity and value. It is a real city bus. People are commuting, shopping, visiting friends, going to appointments and occasionally wondering why someone upstairs is photographing every traffic light.

Here is the honest comparison:

- **Choose Bus 100** if you want a cheap overview, you are comfortable using public transport, and you do not need commentary.
- **Choose hop-on hop-off** if you want a packaged route, recorded explanations, and less planning.
- **Choose a walking tour** if you want to understand what you are seeing, not just pass it.

That is the sweet spot. Bus 100 shows the city. A good walk explains it.

## Bus 100, Bus 200 or Bus 300?

Bus 100 is the classic route, but it is not the only useful sightseeing bus.

**Bus 200** also connects the City West with the Alexanderplatz area, but it runs via Potsdamer Platz and Checkpoint Charlie territory. It is a good choice if you want a different angle on modern Berlin, the Philharmonie, Leipziger Platz or the edge of the old border zone.

**Bus 300** is newer and useful for a route through cultural and historical sights such as Potsdamer Platz, Brandenburg Gate, Bebelplatz, Museum Island, Rotes Rathaus, Nikolaiviertel and the East Side Gallery area.

If this is your first time in Berlin, start with Bus 100. It gives the cleanest classic overview.

If you already rode it, try Bus 200 or 300 next.

## Where Should You Get Off?

You can simply ride the whole route, but the better version is to choose one or two stops.

These are the stops I would consider:

- **Grosser Stern:** get off for the Victory Column and a walk through Tiergarten.
- **Reichstag/Bundestag:** get off if you have booked the Reichstag dome in advance.
- **S+U Brandenburger Tor:** get off for the Brandenburg Gate, the Holocaust Memorial and Unter den Linden.
- **U Museumsinsel:** get off for Museum Island, the Berliner Dom and the Lustgarten.
- **S+U Alexanderplatz/Memhardstrasse:** get off for the TV Tower, World Clock and the start of the old eastern centre.

Do not try to get off at every famous name. You will turn a useful bus ride into a slow administrative project.

Pick one stop, enjoy it properly, then continue.

## My Favorite Cheap Berlin Combo

If you want a simple half-day plan, do this:

Start at Zoologischer Garten. Ride Bus 100 east to the Brandenburg Gate or the Reichstag area. Get off, walk around the Gate, the parliament quarter and the Holocaust Memorial.

Then either continue by bus to Museum Island or walk along Unter den Linden if the weather is good.

Finish at Alexanderplatz. That is where my free, tip-based walking tour begins, at the World Clock.

This combination works because Bus 100 gives you the broad frame first. Then the walk fills in the missing history.

It is also budget-friendly. You spend your money on transport you would probably use anyway, not on a bus with a recorded voice telling you that Berlin has a long history.

## What Bus 100 Does Not Show You

Bus 100 is good, but it has limits.

It does not take you to the Berlin Wall Memorial at Bernauer Strasse. It does not explain Checkpoint Charlie. It does not show you the courtyards around Hackescher Markt. It does not take you through the medieval lanes near Nikolaiviertel in any meaningful way. It does not pause at small details like bullet holes, missing buildings or street names that quietly tell you what happened here.

It also does not give you emotional scale.

From a bus window, the Reichstag is a building. On foot, it becomes a story about empire, fire, dictatorship, division and democracy.

From a bus window, Alexanderplatz is a busy square. On foot, it becomes a clue to medieval Berlin, socialist planning, Cold War ambition and the strange chaos of the modern city.

That is the difference between sightseeing and understanding.

## Practical Tips Before You Ride

Here is the short version I would give a friend:

- **Check the BVG app before you go**, because construction and diversions happen.
- **Start at Zoo or Alexanderplatz** if you want a better chance of a seat.
- **Ride west to east** for the clearest first-time route.
- **Use a 24-hour ticket** if you will take more public transport later that day.
- **Do not expect commentary**, because this is a normal public bus.
- **Keep your ticket valid**, especially if you buy a paper ticket.
- **Avoid rush hour** if you want a relaxed ride and better views.

Morning can be pleasant if you are already in the west. Late afternoon can be beautiful in good light, but traffic may be slower.

If the bus is packed, wait for the next one. The route is frequent enough that a few extra minutes can save the whole experience.
