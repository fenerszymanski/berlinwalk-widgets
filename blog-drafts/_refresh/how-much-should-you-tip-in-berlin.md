# How Much Should You Tip in Berlin? A Simple Guide to Tipping in Germany

Tipping in Berlin is not difficult, but it does confuse visitors.

The rules are not American. They are not Japanese. They are not completely optional in the sense that nobody notices. Berlin sits in the middle: **tipping is appreciated, normal in many situations, but still voluntary**.

The short answer is simple.

In a sit-down restaurant or cafe, round up or tip about **5-10%** if service was good. In a bar, a small round-up is fine. In a taxi, round up to the next euro. On a free walking tour, tip what the experience was worth to you.

The part that feels strange is not only how much to tip. It is how to do it. In Berlin, you usually tell the server the final amount while paying, instead of leaving money on the table after you walk away.

This guide gives you the practical version: restaurants, cafes, bars, hotels, taxis, delivery, free walking tours, and the little mistakes that make tourists feel awkward.

## The Short Answer

If you only remember one rule, remember this: **round up for small bills, tip 5-10% for good table service, and do not feel forced into American-style percentages**.

That covers most situations in Berlin.

Here is the useful tourist version:

- **Sit-down restaurants:** round up or tip 5-10% if service was good.
- **Cafes with table service:** round up to a clean number, or tip a little more for good service.
- **Bars:** round up by 50 cents to EUR 1 per drink or round.
- **Counter service:** no tip expected, though a tip jar is fine if you want.
- **Taxis:** round up to the next euro, or add EUR 1-2 for helpful service.
- **Hotels:** EUR 1-2 per bag for porters, EUR 2-3 per day for housekeeping if you choose.
- **Free walking tours:** EUR 10-15 per person is a solid common tip for a good 2 hours tour.

You can always tip more for excellent service. You can also tip nothing for poor service.

Berlin is direct like that.

## Why Tipping Feels Different in Germany

The official [Berlin.de tipping etiquette guide](https://www.berlin.de/en/tourism/travel-information/1758234-2862820-tipping.en.html) says tipping is voluntary and used to show satisfaction with a service. That sentence is the key.

A tip is not a hidden tax. It is not a fixed extra charge. It is not a moral emergency every time someone turns a payment screen toward you.

It is a signal that you were happy with the service.

That is why Berlin tipping feels more relaxed than in the United States. A server does not expect 20% as the default. At the same time, leaving exactly EUR 43.70 on a EUR 43.70 restaurant bill after good service can feel a bit cold.

The local middle ground is a clean final number.

If the bill is EUR 27.60, making it EUR 30 feels normal. If the bill is EUR 48.40, making it EUR 52 or EUR 53 is fine. If the service was genuinely excellent, EUR 55 is generous.

You are not solving a math test.

You are making the payment feel fair.

## How to Tip When You Pay

This is the part tourists most often get wrong.

In many Berlin restaurants, you do not leave the tip on the table after the server walks away. You tell the server the final amount when you pay.

Example:

- The bill is EUR 27.60.
- You want to pay EUR 30 total.
- You hand over cash or card and say: **"Make it EUR 30, please."**
- The server charges or gives change based on EUR 30.

If paying in German, you can simply say the final number: **"Dreissig, bitte."**

If you pay cash and want the server to keep the change, you can say **"Stimmt so"**, which means something like "that's fine" or "keep the change."

Do not say "thank you" too early if you are handing over a bigger note and still expect change. In some situations, "thank you" can sound like "keep the change." If you want change, say the final amount clearly.

Card payments are similar. Say the total including tip before the terminal is processed. Some newer card machines show tip prompts, but you do not have to choose the biggest percentage on the screen.

Cash is still useful for small tips in Berlin. If you want the broader payment picture, read my guide to [using credit cards in Berlin](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/can-you-use-credit-cards-in-berlin-a-tourist-s-guide-to-paying-in-germany).

## Restaurants and Sit-Down Cafes

For restaurants and cafes with table service, **5-10%** is the normal range when service was good. Berlin.de gives the same range for restaurants, cafes and bars, and notes that rounding up is standard among locals.

For small bills, rounding up is often better than calculating a percentage.

Examples:

- EUR 8.70 coffee and cake: pay EUR 10 if you were happy.
- EUR 17.40 casual lunch: pay EUR 19 or EUR 20.
- EUR 34.80 dinner: pay EUR 38.
- EUR 86.00 nicer meal: pay EUR 92 or EUR 95.

You do not need to tip 20% because the card machine suggests it. You also do not need to punish yourself with exact percentage math.

The best local question is: what final number feels clean and fair?

If service was rude, chaotic or careless, pay the bill and leave. A tip is not compulsory.

## Bars, Beer Gardens and Counter Service

Bars are more casual.

If you order a beer for EUR 4.50, paying EUR 5 is normal. If you order two drinks for EUR 11.20, paying EUR 12 is fine. If you sit at a table and get real service for a few rounds, you can add a little more.

At a counter, the expectation is lower.

For bakeries, fast food, takeaway windows, self-service places and market stalls, tipping is not expected. If there is a tip jar and you want to drop in coins, nice. If you do not, nobody should care.

Digital tip screens are becoming more common, especially in tourist-heavy areas. Treat them as an invitation, not an obligation.

The "no tip" button is allowed to exist for a reason.

## Hotels, Housekeeping and Cloakrooms

Hotels are simple:

- **Porter:** EUR 1-2 per bag.
- **Housekeeping:** EUR 2-3 per day if you choose.
- **Concierge or reception help:** EUR 5-10 for unusually useful help.
- **Hostels and budget stays:** no tip expected.

Berlin.de gives similar hotel guidance and notes that tips are not required in cheap accommodation such as hostels or campsites.

For cloakrooms at clubs, theatres or restaurants, tip only if there is no fixed fee or if the service was helpful. Around 50 cents to EUR 2 is enough.

No one expects a dramatic gesture.

A few coins can do the job.

## Taxis and Airport Rides

For taxis, do not overthink it.

The official [Berlin.de taxi rules page](https://www.berlin.de/en/public-transportation/1756978-2913840-taxi-phone-numbers-fares-rules.en.html) says tipping is not necessary, but locals usually round up to the nearest euro if the ride was pleasant. If the driver helped with luggage or gave unusually good service, EUR 1-2 extra is a nice gesture.

Examples:

- EUR 13.40 ride: pay EUR 14.
- EUR 27.20 ride: pay EUR 29 or EUR 30.
- Airport ride with luggage help: add EUR 2-5 if it felt helpful.

For ride-hailing apps, tipping is optional. The app may prompt you, but the same Berlin logic applies: pleasant service gets a small thanks, not a mandatory surcharge.

## Food Delivery

Food delivery tips are optional, but common when the weather is bad, the building is hard to find, or the order is heavy.

A practical range is **EUR 1-2** for a normal order, more if the driver did extra work.

Cash is often better if you want to be sure the driver receives it directly. App tips can vary by platform and restaurant setup, so do not assume every digital tip feels the same on the worker side.

If the delivery was late because the restaurant was overwhelmed, that is not always the driver's fault.

Berlin teaches patience one delivery order at a time.

## How Much to Tip on a Free Walking Tour

Free walking tours are the one tipping situation where visitors feel the most pressure, because there is no fixed price on the booking page.

Here is the honest version.

On a tip-based walking tour in Berlin, **EUR 10-15 per person** is a solid common tip for a good tour. EUR 5 is modest but completely acceptable if you are on a tight budget. EUR 20 or more is generous when the guide made the city click for you.

For a 2 hours tour, think of it like this:

- **EUR 5:** modest, budget-friendly, still appreciated.
- **EUR 10:** normal and fair for a good tour.
- **EUR 15:** strong tip for a very good tour.
- **EUR 20+:** generous, especially if the tour was a highlight.

The best rule is simple: tip what the experience was worth to you.

I would rather have someone join, learn the city, and tip modestly than skip the tour because they were anxious about the amount. The whole point of a tip-based tour is that people with different travel budgets can still come.

Bring cash if you can. It makes the end of the tour much easier.

## When Not to Tip

You do not need to tip everywhere in Berlin.

Do not tip:

- **Supermarket cashiers**
- **Museum ticket desks**
- **Public transport staff**
- **Fast food chains**
- **Self-checkout machines**
- **Counter service where nobody brings anything to your table**
- **Bad service**

That last one matters.

Tipping is voluntary. If someone is rude, dishonest, pushy or careless, paying the bill is enough.

This is especially important around tourist hotspots. If a restaurant tries to pressure you with "tip not included" language, stay calm. Tips are not compulsory in Berlin. Decide what you want to pay and do not let a payment screen bully you.

## Common Tourist Mistakes

The first mistake is tipping too much because you are afraid of being rude.

Generosity is lovely. Panic-tipping is unnecessary.

The second mistake is leaving coins on the table and walking out without saying anything. It may be understood, but it is less typical than telling the server the final amount while paying.

The third mistake is assuming every service worker expects a percentage. A bakery counter is not a restaurant table. A museum desk is not a hotel porter. A taxi round-up is not a 20% restaurant tip.

The fourth mistake is having no small cash.

Berlin is better with small euro notes and coins. They help with tips, toilets, lockers, markets, small cafes and the tiny practical moments that make travel smoother.

## The Quick Reference

Here is the whole guide in one list:

- **Restaurant with table service:** 5-10% or round up.
- **Casual cafe:** round up to the next clean number.
- **Bar:** 50 cents to EUR 1 per drink or round.
- **Counter service:** no tip expected.
- **Taxi:** round up to the next euro.
- **Hotel porter:** EUR 1-2 per bag.
- **Housekeeping:** EUR 2-3 per day if you choose.
- **Delivery:** EUR 1-2 for a normal order.
- **Free walking tour:** EUR 10-15 is a solid common tip for a good 2 hours tour.
- **Bad service:** no tip required.

When in doubt, round up.

That one habit will get you through almost every normal Berlin tipping situation.
