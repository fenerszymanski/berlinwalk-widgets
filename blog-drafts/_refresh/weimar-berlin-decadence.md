# Was Berlin Really the Most Decadent City in the 1920s?

If you know 1920s Berlin from *Babylon Berlin*, *Cabaret*, old photographs or half-remembered stories about jazz, drag, cocaine and smoky nightclubs, you probably imagine the city as one long party before disaster.

That picture is not completely false.

It is just dangerously incomplete.

Berlin in the 1920s really was one of Europe's most experimental cities. It had cabarets, cinemas, political satire, queer bars, sex reformers, modern art, department stores, neon, dance halls, sharp journalism and a hunger for everything new. Visitors came because Berlin looked freer, louder and more permissive than almost anywhere else.

But the same city also had war trauma, hunger memories, housing pressure, street violence, inflation, political extremism and a democracy that never felt secure.

So was Berlin really decadent?

Yes, in places. Yes, for certain people. Yes, for a few dazzling years.

But the better answer is this: 1920s Berlin was not a carefree party city. It was a nervous, brilliant, wounded metropolis that turned instability into culture until the instability swallowed the culture.

## The Berlin of the 1920s Was Suddenly Huge

The story starts with size.

Berlin was already important before the 1920s, but the city became something different almost overnight. The [Greater Berlin Act](https://www.berlin.de/en/history/8481401-8619314-greater-berlin-act.en.html) came into force on **1 October 1920**, joining old Berlin with surrounding towns, rural communities and estate districts.

Berlin.de puts the change clearly: the population grew to around **3.8 million**, and Berlin became the third largest city in the world after London and New York. The city area expanded from old Berlin's compact core to a huge modern metropolis.

That matters because the famous 1920s Berlin was not just a small old capital becoming naughty.

It was a new urban machine.

Charlottenburg, Schöneberg, Neukölln, Wilmersdorf, Spandau, Köpenick, Lichtenberg and other places were pulled into one political city. Different classes, districts, theatres, factories, newspapers, cinemas, bars and transport networks were suddenly part of the same metropolitan story.

Berlin was not only becoming freer. It was becoming larger, faster and harder to control.

## What People Mean by "Decadent Berlin"

When people say "decadent Berlin", they usually mean a few overlapping things.

They mean cabaret: political songs, satire, erotic performance, jokes about authority, gender play, performers who could mock the respectable world while selling tickets to it.

They mean queer Berlin: bars, clubs, magazines, drag balls, lesbian spaces, gay networks, sexual science, legal battles and a visibility that shocked visitors from more conservative places.

They mean nightlife: dance halls, jazz bands, late drinking, foreign tourists, sex work, drug stories and a city that seemed to turn private desire into public spectacle.

They mean art: George Grosz, Otto Dix, Hannah Höch, Dada, New Objectivity, sharp theatre, cinema, newspapers and posters that made the modern city look both exciting and ugly.

They mean the feeling that everything old had cracked.

The Kaiser was gone. The war had ended in defeat. The republic was new. Censorship had loosened. Women had the vote. Old moral rules still existed, but they no longer looked unbreakable.

That is the Berlin people remember because it photographs well and makes a good story.

It was real.

It was also not the whole city.

## The City Was Freer Because the Old Order Had Broken

The freedom of Weimar Berlin did not arrive in a calm, healthy society.

It came after disaster.

Germany lost World War I. The monarchy collapsed in 1918. Revolution shook the streets. The Weimar Republic was founded as a fragile democracy, not as a confident national agreement.

The [Deutsches Historisches Museum](https://www.dhm.de/en/exhibitions/permanent-exhibition/weimar-republic/) describes the Weimar Republic as a young democracy threatened by inflation, economic crisis, and left-wing and right-wing extremists fighting the democratic order. It also notes a brief phase of relative stability when the economy recovered and avant-garde culture came into bloom.

That phrase, **brief phase**, is important.

The classic "Golden Twenties" version of Berlin mostly belongs to the middle of the decade. The early years were marked by crisis. The later years slid toward crisis again.

The brilliant cultural moment sat between two cliffs.

That makes the nightlife easier to understand. Some Berliners were exploring new freedom. Some were escaping fear. Some were simply trying to survive.

The same cabaret table could hold artists, tourists, political cynics, sex workers, journalists, criminals, hungry students and wealthy people spending foreign currency in a city made cheap by crisis.

That mixture is what gave the decade its electricity.

It is also what made it unstable.

## Hyperinflation Was Not Glamorous

One of the biggest problems with the glamorous version of 1920s Berlin is that it smooths over the pain.

Hyperinflation in 1923 was not a colourful anecdote. It destroyed savings, wages, pensions and trust. Money lost value so quickly that ordinary people had to think about bread, rent, heat and dignity in a completely different way.

The famous stories sound almost comic: wheelbarrows of money, prices changing during the day, banknotes used like waste paper.

But for people living through it, inflation meant terror.

If you had savings, they could vanish. If you were paid in marks, the wage could lose value before you reached the shop. If you were poor, the city was not an exciting stage set. It was a daily emergency.

That is why "decadence" is such a tricky word.

For some people, Berlin's nightlife looked like liberation. For others, it was desperation. Sex work, illegal selling, petty crime and underground economies did not grow only because people were open-minded. They also grew because normal economic life had failed.

The myth turns survival into style.

The history is harsher.

## Queer Berlin Was Real, Visible and Vulnerable

The most important part of the 1920s Berlin story is not only the cabaret glamour.

It is queer Berlin.

This is where the city really was extraordinary. [VisitBerlin's history of homosexuality in Berlin](https://www.visitberlin.de/en/history-homosexuality-berlin) notes that in the 1920s the city had become a haven for gays and lesbians, with around **170 clubs, bars and pubs** for gay and lesbian life, plus political associations fighting for equal rights.

That number is astonishing even today.

Places like Eldorado became famous not because Berlin had abolished prejudice. It had not. Male homosexuality remained criminalized under Paragraph 175. Police attention, blackmail, social danger and legal risk were real.

The point is more complicated: queer life was visible despite the danger.

People found spaces. They made networks. They published. They danced. They organized. They built culture under pressure.

Magnus Hirschfeld stands at the centre of that world. The [Magnus Hirschfeld Society](https://magnus-hirschfeld.de/ausstellungen/institute/) records that his Institute for Sexual Science opened on **6 July 1919**. It combined research, advice, medical work, public education and reform politics in a way that made Berlin central to early sexual science and queer rights history.

This is one reason the "decadent Berlin" myth can be misleading.

Calling the whole scene decadent can make it sound like empty excess.

Much of it was actually political, scientific and personal courage.

## Cabaret Was Not Just Entertainment

Cabaret is another word that gets flattened.

People imagine feathers, smoke and songs. That existed.

But Weimar cabaret also worked as a political pressure valve. It mocked authority, class hypocrisy, militarism, sexual norms and the new republic itself. It could be playful, cruel, intelligent, vulgar and sharp within the same evening.

Berlin's cabaret culture thrived because the city had the audience for it: journalists, artists, workers, tourists, intellectuals, foreigners, bohemians, political obsessives and people who simply wanted to forget the day.

That is why it mattered.

Cabaret did not sit outside politics. It performed politics as entertainment.

The same was true for art. George Grosz and Otto Dix did not paint Weimar Germany as a clean golden age. They painted mutilated veterans, profiteers, sex work, hypocrisy, violence and grotesque bodies. The art of the period often looks harsh because the society was harsh.

That is the part postcards miss.

The culture was brilliant because it was looking directly at the damage.

## The "Golden Twenties" Were Very Short

The phrase Golden Twenties makes the decade sound longer than it was.

It was not ten golden years.

The most stable stretch ran roughly from **1924 to 1929**. The currency stabilized after hyperinflation. Foreign loans helped the economy. Culture bloomed. Berlin became internationally magnetic.

Then the 1929 Wall Street crash hit.

Germany was deeply exposed to international finance. The crisis fed unemployment, fear and political radicalization. The democracy was already fragile, and the economic shock gave its enemies more room.

By **1933**, Hitler was chancellor.

That is the brutal timeline behind the glamour: 1924 to 1929 for the classic golden window, then collapse, then dictatorship.

When you stand in Berlin today and imagine the Roaring Twenties, remember how short the roar really was.

Five years can make a legend.

Five years can also disappear very fast.

## How It Ended

The end was not gradual for everyone.

For some people, it arrived as immediate violence.

The United States Holocaust Memorial Museum's article on [Nazi book burnings](https://encyclopedia.ushmm.org/content/en/article/book-burning) explains that the largest book burning took place in Berlin on **10 May 1933** at Opernplatz, today Bebelplatz. Around **20,000 volumes** were burned there, including materials confiscated from Magnus Hirschfeld's Institute for Sexual Science.

The museum's [Magnus Hirschfeld article](https://encyclopedia.ushmm.org/content/en/article/magnus-hirschfeld-2) adds that the Nazis vandalized the institute, forced it to close and destroyed much of its archive in the book burning.

That is one of the clearest symbols of the end of Weimar Berlin.

The city that had made room for research, sexual reform, queer visibility, satire and avant-garde art became the city where those books burned in public.

The cabarets closed or were forced into conformity. Artists fled, adapted, were silenced or were persecuted. Jewish Berliners, queer Berliners, leftists, modernists and many others became targets of the new regime.

The "decadent city" did not simply fade.

It was attacked.

## What Remains Today

The original Weimar city is hard to find because Berlin has been broken and rebuilt so many times.

Some places survive physically. Many survive only as addresses, memories or echoes.

KaDeWe still exists in the west. Babylon cinema still shows films in Mitte. Nollendorfplatz and Schöneberg remain central to Berlin's queer geography. Bebelplatz has a memorial to the book burning. Alexanderplatz is still a place of crowds, transport, noise and urban restlessness, even though the built environment changed almost completely.

Other places are gone.

The Romanisches Café, once a legendary meeting point for writers and artists near the Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church, was destroyed in the war. Many cabaret venues vanished. Much of the city that Christopher Isherwood, Alfred Döblin, Marlene Dietrich, Hirschfeld, Grosz and Dix knew has disappeared.

That is very Berlin.

The past is present, but rarely intact.

## Was Berlin More Decadent Than Paris, London or New York?

This is the question behind the myth.

Was Berlin really the most decadent city in the world?

It depends what you mean.

Paris had modern art, nightlife and sexual reputation. New York had jazz, speakeasies, money and mass culture. London had clubs, theatre and imperial wealth. Vienna had intellectual life and its own post-imperial crisis.

Berlin was different because everything seemed compressed.

War defeat, revolution, inflation, sexual reform, mass media, cinema, political violence, queer visibility, poverty, modern transport, right-wing rage, left-wing hope, international tourism and artistic shock all occupied the same city at the same time.

That compression made Berlin feel extreme.

It was not necessarily freer for everyone. It was not safer. It was not richer. It was not more stable.

But it was more exposed.

Berlin put the contradictions of modern Europe on stage.

That is why the myth survived.

## The Better Way to Understand 1920s Berlin

I would not describe 1920s Berlin only as decadent.

I would describe it as intensely modern.

Modern in its speed. Modern in its mass transport. Modern in its newspapers and cinemas. Modern in its sexual politics. Modern in its consumer culture. Modern in its anxiety. Modern in its ugliness. Modern in the way it turned crisis into images, songs, jokes and performances.

Decadence was one surface of that modernity.

Underneath it was a city trying to work out what freedom meant after empire, war and collapse.

That is what makes the period so fascinating. It was not simply a party before fascism. It was an experiment in democracy, culture and identity under terrible pressure.

Some parts were glamorous.

Some parts were brave.

Some parts were exploitative.

Some parts were doomed.

All of them were Berlin.
