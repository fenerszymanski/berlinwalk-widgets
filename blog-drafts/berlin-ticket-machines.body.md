The first thing a lot of visitors do in Berlin is stand in front of a ticket machine, look at a wall of German words, and quietly panic while a train pulls in behind them. I understand that feeling, and I have watched it happen a hundred times at Alexanderplatz. The good news is that the machine is simpler than it looks, it speaks English, and once you know the four or five taps that matter, buying a **Berlin ticket machine** ticket takes about thirty seconds.

This guide walks you through the whole thing: where the machines are, how to switch to English, which fare to choose in 2026, how to pay, and the one step at the end that catches out more tourists than any other.

## Where the ticket machines actually are

Berlin has no ticket barriers. You will not tap in or out like in London or Paris, and nobody checks your ticket to get onto the platform. That freedom is exactly why the machine matters: it is the only place your journey becomes legal, and it is easy to walk straight past it.

You will find the machines **on the platform itself**, usually near the stairs or the entrance, not in a separate ticket hall. There are two kinds, and for a visitor the difference barely matters:

- **BVG machines** stand on U-Bahn platforms. The older ones are the classic yellow and white boxes; newer ones are bigger with a large touchscreen.
- **Red Deutsche Bahn (DB) machines** stand on S-Bahn platforms, usually labelled _VBB-Fahrkarten_.

Both sell the same tickets. A ticket you buy from a red S-Bahn machine is valid on the U-Bahn, the tram, and the bus, and the other way round. So use whichever machine is in front of you and do not overthink it.

## Step one: wake the screen and choose English

Here is the small thing that confuses people first. The machine usually shows a rolling advert, not a menu, so it can look switched off or broken. It is not. **Touch the screen anywhere** and the menu appears.

The moment the menu loads, look for a language row, often shown as small flags, and pick English. The machines run in **German, English, French, Turkish, and Spanish**, so you are not stuck reading _Einzelfahrausweis_ unless you want to. If you would like a head start on the German words you will still see printed on the ticket and the signs, my guide to [reading German signs in Berlin](/post/german-signs-in-berlin) covers the ones that matter.

## Step two: choose the right ticket

This is where most of the real decision happens, and it is worth thirty seconds of thought because the wrong ticket is either a waste of money or a fine. These are the 2026 fares for the Berlin zones, straight from the official [BVG ticket pages](https://www.bvg.de/en/subscriptions-and-tickets/all-tickets/single-tickets/single-ticket) and [berlin.de](https://www.berlin.de/en/public-transportation/1772016-2913840-tickets-fares-and-route-maps.en.html).

### Single ticket AB, 4.00 euros

The standard tourist ticket. It covers **one journey in one direction** across zones A and B, which is the whole city centre and almost everywhere you will want to go. It is valid for **120 minutes** and you can change between the U-Bahn, S-Bahn, tram, and bus as much as you like, as long as you keep moving in roughly one direction. No return trips.

### Kurzstrecke (short trip), 2.80 euros

The cheap one, with a catch. A Kurzstrecke covers **3 stops on the U-Bahn or S-Bahn** (one change allowed) or **6 stops on a bus or tram**. If your hotel is genuinely three stops from the museum, this is the smart buy. If you might go further, buy the AB single, because a Kurzstrecke used beyond its limit counts as no ticket at all.

### Day ticket AB (Tageskarte), 11.20 euros

Unlimited trips in zones A and B until **3:00 the next morning**. The maths is simple: from about three rides in a day, the day ticket beats buying singles. On a normal sightseeing day where you are hopping on and off, it usually pays for itself and saves you queueing at every machine.

### The airport ticket: ABC

BER airport sits in **Zone C**, outside the normal AB city zone. So for any trip to or from the airport you need a **Single ABC (5.00 euros)** or a **Day ticket ABC (12.90 euros)**. A plain AB ticket will not cover the airport run, and it is a common, expensive mistake. If you want the full airport route rather than just the fare, I wrote a separate guide on [getting from Berlin airport to Alexanderplatz](/post/how-to-get-from-berlin-airport-to-alexanderplatz-the-easy-way). For how the zones fit together in general, see my [Berlin AB and ABC ticket zones](/post/berlin-ab-abc-ticket-zones) explainer.

## Step three: pay by card, phone, or cash

Once you confirm the ticket, the machine asks for payment. Modern Berlin machines are comfortable with cards: they take **girocard, Visa, Mastercard, and Maestro**, and most now accept **Apple Pay and Google Pay** too, so a contactless tap is usually the fastest way through. You may be asked for your PIN.

If you prefer cash, the machines take **coins from 5 cents up to 2 euros** and **notes of 5, 10, and 20 euros**. One warning worth repeating: the machines carry **limited change**. Feeding a 20 euro note for a 2.80 euro Kurzstrecke is a good way to get a fistful of coins back, or occasionally a machine that cannot make change at all. Pay with something close to the price when you can.

![A yellow onboard ticket machine inside a Berlin tram, with buttons and a coin slot.](blog-drafts/images/berlin-ticket-machines/optimized/06-tram-onboard-ticket-machine.jpg)
Trams have their own small machines onboard, and they usually take coins only. Buy before you sit down.

A quick note on **trams and buses**, because they work differently. Trams have a **small machine onboard**, often coin-only and button-based rather than a touchscreen, so carry some change if a tram is your plan. On buses you buy from the **driver**. If you would rather skip machines entirely, you can buy the same tickets in the BVG app, which I cover in [how to buy Berlin transport tickets on your phone](/post/buy-berlin-transport-tickets-on-your-phone).

## Step four: the one that saves you 60 euros

This is the step that turns a valid ticket into a fine if you skip it, so read this part twice.

A paper ticket from the machine is **not valid until you stamp it**. Buying it is not enough. Before you board, you push the ticket into a **small yellow or red box** on the platform, mounted on a pole or a wall near the stairs. It is marked **_Bitte hier entwerten / Please validate here_**. The box stamps the date, time, and station onto your ticket, which is what starts the clock on that 120 minutes.

![A yellow Berlin validation box labelled Bitte hier entwerten, Please validate here, with a slot for stamping tickets.](blog-drafts/images/berlin-ticket-machines/optimized/05-validate-ticket-entwerter.jpg)
The validation box. Push your paper ticket into the slot until it clicks, before you get on the train.

Because there are no barriers, plain-clothes inspectors ride the trains and check tickets at random. An unstamped ticket counts as **_Fahren ohne gültigen Fahrschein_**, travelling without a valid ticket, and the fine is **60 euros** paid on the spot. I have watched tourists get caught with a ticket in their pocket that they simply forgot to stamp, and the inspector has heard every excuse. If you want the full picture on how validation and fines work, I go deeper in [do you really need to validate your ticket on Berlin trains](/post/do-you-really-need-to-validate-your-ticket-on-berlin-trains) and [the Berlin U-Bahn fine explained](/post/berlin-u-bahn-fine).

The one exception: tickets bought in the **BVG app are already validated** the moment you buy them, so there is nothing to stamp. This only applies to paper tickets from the machine.

![A yellow BVG ticket machine in an ornate tiled Berlin U-Bahn station, with a traveller walking past.](blog-drafts/images/berlin-ticket-machines/optimized/04-bvg-machine-ubahn-platform.jpg)
Machines sit right on the platform, so buy and stamp before your train arrives, not after.

## The honest shortcut

If you are only in Berlin for a couple of days and you find the machines stressful, here is what I actually tell my guests: buy a **day ticket** in the morning, stamp it once, and forget about the machine for the rest of the day. You stop calculating fares at every station, you never ride unstamped by accident, and the cost is close to what three separate singles would be anyway. For a full-day itinerary it is the calmest option.

However you buy it, the machine is a thirty-second job once you know the four steps: **touch the screen, choose your ticket, pay, stamp**. Do that and Berlin's transport becomes one of the easiest big-city systems in Europe to use.
