Most first-time visitors never leave the old historic core, and for two or three days that is exactly the right call. But if you have a spare afternoon, or a slow morning before a late flight, Tiergarten is where Berliners actually go to breathe. It is 210 hectares of woodland, lakes and formal garden sitting right between the Brandenburg Gate and Zoologischer Garten station, open around the clock, and it costs nothing to walk in.

My advice: do not treat Tiergarten Berlin as one landmark to tick off. It is not built around a single photo stop the way the Brandenburg Gate or the Reichstag are. Decide first how much time you actually have, then follow one corner of the park properly, rather than trying to cross the whole thing in one pass. That is the difference between a good hour here and a tired two-hour walk that never quite arrives anywhere.

## Tiergarten Berlin: From a Prince's Hunting Ground to Everyone's Park

Tiergarten Berlin started as exactly what the name says: a hunting ground. The Elector of Brandenburg fenced it off in 1527 to stock deer and wild boar for the court, well outside the city walls of the time. By the late 1600s, Friedrich III had a different idea, and opened part of it as a pleasure garden for ordinary Berliners rather than just the hunt.

The park you actually walk through today was shaped later, between 1833 and 1838, by Peter Joseph Lenné, Prussia's leading landscape gardener. Lenné had studied English landscape design, and he redrew the old formal hunting paths into the winding, lake-dotted, deliberately informal layout that still defines Tiergarten. That English influence became literal a century later with the addition of an actual English Garden, which I will get to below.

## What the War Did to the Park, and How Berlin Grew It Back

Here is the detail most visitors never hear, and it changes how you see every tree in the park: **nearly everything you walk under today was planted after 1949.**

By the end of the Second World War, Tiergarten was close to gone. Coal was impossible to find in a freezing, blockaded city, so Berliners cut the trees for firewood. Of more than 200,000 trees that once lined its paths, only around 700 survived. Every bridge was destroyed. The lakes silted up. What was left of the open ground was carved into roughly 2,550 allotments for growing potatoes and vegetables, because the park's usefulness as a park was, for a while, completely beside the point.

The rebuild started on 17 March 1949, when Lord Mayor Ernst Reuter planted a linden tree to mark the beginning of the replanting. Over the following decade, around 250,000 young trees arrived from across West Germany as donations, some of them flown into the blockaded city during the airlift. Walk under the tall trees near the Straße des 17. Juni today and you are standing in a forest that is, in tree years, barely older than the Berlin Wall was when it came down.

![The Soviet War Memorial in Berlin's Tiergarten, a colonnade of red granite with a bronze soldier statue, flanked by flowerbeds](blog-drafts/images/tiergarten-berlin/optimized/02-tiergarten-berlin-soviet-war-memorial.jpg)

_The Soviet War Memorial, inaugurated on 11 November 1945 while the rest of the city around it was still in ruins._

The clearest physical link to that period stands right on the Straße des 17. Juni, close to the Brandenburg Gate: the **Soviet War Memorial**. It commemorates roughly 80,000 Soviet soldiers who died in the Battle of Berlin in April and May 1945, and it went up astonishingly fast, inaugurated that same November. Two T-34 tanks stand guard on either side of a curved colonnade built from red granite, much of it reportedly taken from the ruined Reich Chancellery nearby. If you have already read my guide to [Cold War Berlin's key sites](/post/cold-war-berlin-in-5-key-locations-you-can-still-visit), this memorial belongs on that same list, and it sits inside a Western-controlled sector, which made it an odd, closely guarded piece of Soviet territory throughout the Cold War.

## The Landmarks People Actually Come For

Two more sights anchor the park and pull most of the day-trip traffic.

The **Victory Column**, at the Großer Stern roundabout in the middle of the park, is Tiergarten's obvious centrepiece. It was actually moved here in 1938 and 1939 as part of the Nazi regime's plan to widen the surrounding avenues; before that it stood outside the Reichstag. I have written a full separate guide to [visiting the Victory Column](/post/victory-column-berlin), including the climb and ticket prices, so I won't repeat all of that here. Treat it as the pin you can see from almost anywhere in the park, not a reason on its own to make the trip.

![The white facade of Bellevue Palace in Berlin, the official residence of the German President, seen across its front lawn](blog-drafts/images/tiergarten-berlin/optimized/03-tiergarten-berlin-bellevue-palace.jpg)

_Bellevue Palace, the German President's official residence, on the park's northwestern edge._

**Bellevue Palace** sits on the park's northwestern edge, closest to Bellevue S-Bahn station. It has been the official residence of Germany's President since 1994. There is no public access inside, so do not plan a visit around getting through the door. The point of coming here is simpler: it is a genuinely handsome late-1700s building set against open lawn, and it is one of the few spots in central Berlin where you get real breathing room in front of a landmark instead of a crowd.

## The Quieter Half of the Park

Once you move away from the Victory Column and the memorial, Tiergarten gets noticeably calmer, and this is the part most tourists skip entirely.

The **Englischer Garten**, or English Garden, sits in the park's northwest corner near Bellevue. It was added in the 1950s, largely funded by donations from Britain's Royal House and the British City Commander during the occupation years, as a deliberate homage to the English landscape style Lenné had already brought to the wider park. One current detail worth knowing before you go: the little Teehaus inside the garden burned down in September 2024, and the district of Mitte has plans to rebuild it, so do not be surprised if you find scaffolding instead of tea.

![A lush green path through the Englischer Garten in Berlin's Tiergarten, framed by rhododendron bushes and mature trees](blog-drafts/images/tiergarten-berlin/optimized/04-tiergarten-berlin-englischer-garten.jpg)

_The Englischer Garten in late spring, added in the 1950s as a direct nod to English landscape style._

A little further south, crossing one of the park's waterways, is the **Löwenbrücke**, a listed footbridge guarded by four stone lions. It is easy to miss on a map and easy to love in person, one of those small, well-made details that Lenné's original design was full of.

![The lion-guarded Löwenbrücke footbridge over a waterway in Berlin's Tiergarten, surrounded by autumn foliage](blog-drafts/images/tiergarten-berlin/optimized/05-tiergarten-berlin-loewenbruecke-lions.jpg)

_The Löwenbrücke, a 19th-century footbridge that most visitors walk straight past without noticing._

Nearby, the **Neuer See** is the park's most romantic spot for an actual slow afternoon: rowboats and pedal boats are for rent at Café am Neuen See, usually from around midday through the warmer months, for roughly 10 EUR an hour. It is a proper local move, not a tourist trap, and one I would genuinely recommend if your schedule allows for it.

## Getting There Without Wasting a Transfer

Tiergarten is unusual among Berlin's big sights because there is no single "front door." Pick your entry point based on what you actually want to see first.

- **Brandenburger Tor** (U55/U5, S-Bahn): the closest entrance to the Soviet War Memorial and the eastern half of the park, and where most people arrive after visiting the [Brandenburg Gate](/post/brandenburg-gate-berlin-visitors-guide) itself or the [Reichstag dome](/post/how-to-visit-the-reichstag-dome-for-free-and-why-you-should-book-now).
- **Bellevue** (S-Bahn S3/S5/S7/S9): right next to Bellevue Palace and the Englischer Garten, the better choice if you want the quiet northwest corner first.
- **Tiergarten** (S-Bahn S5/S7/S9): a short walk south of the Straße des 17. Juni, convenient if you are coming from Hauptbahnhof.
- **Zoologischer Garten** (S-Bahn and U-Bahn): the park's southwestern corner, next to Berlin Zoo, and the busiest of the entry stations.

The Straße des 17. Juni runs straight through the middle of the park, connecting the Brandenburg Gate to Ernst-Reuter-Platz, with the Victory Column roundabout roughly at its midpoint. If you get lost, that road is your spine; every landmark above sits within a few hundred metres of it.

## A Few Practical Notes

Tiergarten is free and technically open 24 hours a day, but treat it the way you would any large city park after dark: stick to the lit main paths like the Straße des 17. Juni and Spreeweg rather than cutting through the trees, and go with someone if you can. During the day it is thoroughly safe and heavily used by joggers, cyclists and families.

It is not on my own [walking tour route](/berlin-walking-tour-route), which stays in the medieval core around Alexanderplatz, Museum Island and Hackescher Markt. That is by design. My [free walking tour](/the-guide) covers a lot of ground in two hours, and Tiergarten is genuinely a different kind of Berlin, worth its own slower half-day rather than a rushed add-on.
