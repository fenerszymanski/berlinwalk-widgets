Walk out of Alexanderplatz station on a summer morning and you will see them before you see anything else: teal, green and black rental e-scooters, parked in loose rows along Karl-Liebknecht-Straße, waiting for someone to scan them. They look like the easiest way to cover Berlin's flat, wide centre, and for short hops they genuinely are. What catches tourists out is not the riding. It is the small pile of rules underneath it, because in Germany an e-scooter is treated as a proper vehicle, not a toy.

I get asked about this on almost every tour, usually by someone who has just been shouted at for riding on the pavement, or who is quietly worried about the beer they had at lunch. So here is the honest version: how to rent one, where you are actually allowed to ride, and the handful of mistakes that turn a three-euro trip into a real fine.

## Renting one: Bolt, Dott, Lime and Voi

Four companies run the shared scooters you see on the street: **Bolt, Dott (the brand that used to be Tier), Lime and Voi**. Each one has its own app, and there is no single Berlin app that unlocks all of them, so most people end up with two installed and just grab whichever scooter is nearest.

The process is the same everywhere. Download the app, add a payment card, then scan the QR code on the handlebars to unlock. You ride, you park, and you end the trip in the app. Pricing is by the minute: usually **around 1 euro to unlock plus roughly 0.20 to 0.28 euro per minute**, sometimes with a day pass option.

That maths matters. A quick ten-minute run from Museum Island to Hackescher Markt is cheap and fun. A long cross-city ride is often slower and pricier than a normal BVG ticket, and you can read how those tickets work in my guide to [Berlin public transport](/post/berlin-public-transport-explained-for-tourists-u-bahn-s-bahn-tram-bus). My rule: scooters for the last flat kilometre, the U-Bahn for everything else.

## Where you can ride e-scooters in Berlin

This is the part that gets people in trouble, so it is worth being blunt. **You ride in the bike lane. Where there is no bike lane, you ride on the road.** You do not ride on the pavement, and you do not ride through pedestrian zones. The top speed is capped at 20 km/h, so there is no way to "keep up with traffic" on a big road anyway.

![A man rides a stand-up e-scooter on a Berlin street beside cyclists and parked rental bikes](blog-drafts/images/e-scooters-in-berlin/optimized/03-riders-and-bikes-berlin.jpg)

The right place for a scooter is the bike lane or the road, moving with the cyclists.

Berlin has good, wide cycle lanes on most of the main routes, and once you commit to using them the city opens up. Ride predictably, keep to the right, and put your arm out to signal a turn the way cyclists do. If you have ever cycled here, it is the same etiquette, and I go deeper on that in my post on [Berlin's bike lanes](/post/berlin-bike-lanes-tourists). The pavement feels safer to a nervous first-timer, but it is exactly where you get a warning or a ticket, and it is where you frighten people who are just trying to walk.

## Where you cannot ride, even when it looks fine

Some of the most tempting shortcuts are off-limits, and they are not always obvious.

- **Parks and green spaces.** Tiergarten, Park am Gleisdreieck and many others are posted no-scooter zones. You will see red and white signs, and the apps geofence a lot of green space so the scooter slows to a crawl or refuses to let you finish the ride there.
- **Pedestrian zones and market streets.** Anywhere signed for people on foot is out, even if it is empty.
- **The wrong side, the wrong way.** No riding against traffic, and no weaving between pedestrians "just for a second".

![A red and white sign in a Berlin park reading E-Scooter verboten](blog-drafts/images/e-scooters-in-berlin/optimized/04-e-scooter-verboten-sign-park.jpg)

These signs are real and common. A blocked or slowed scooter in a park usually means you have crossed into a banned zone.

## Parking: where tourists actually get charged

Riding fines get talked about, but **parking is where most tourists lose money**, because it is easy to end a trip badly without noticing.

The basic rule is simple. Park the scooter **parallel, right at the edge of the pavement**, and leave about **2.30 metres clear** so people, prams and wheelchairs can pass. Never leave it in the middle of a walkway, in a doorway, on a tram track, or blocking a kerb ramp.

![TIER and Lime rental e-scooters lined up neatly at the kerb edge on a central Berlin boulevard](blog-drafts/images/e-scooters-in-berlin/optimized/05-parked-scooters-karl-liebknecht.jpg)

Parked properly: tucked against the edge, out of the walking line.

In the central districts, Berlin is steadily moving to **designated parking bays**. In those zones you can only end the ride inside a marked bay, shown in the app with a blue outline or a Jelbi "J" symbol, and if you dump the scooter anywhere else the app keeps charging you. Wrong parking typically costs **15 to 30 euros**. Before you tap "end ride", glance at the map and walk the extra few steps to a proper spot.

![Several rental e-scooters toppled in a pile half-blocking a Berlin pavement](blog-drafts/images/e-scooters-in-berlin/optimized/06-toppled-scooters-berlin.jpg)

This is what pushed the city toward parking bays and fines. Do not be the pile.

## The one mistake that can ruin a trip: riding after drinks

If you remember nothing else, remember this. **An e-scooter is legally a motor vehicle, so alcohol counts exactly like it would in a car.** The 0.5 per mille limit applies, and from 1.1 per mille it becomes a criminal offence, not just a fine.

The penalties are not small. A first offence around the limit runs to roughly **500 euros, points, and a driving ban**, and above the criminal threshold you are looking at a possible loss of your driving licence and a court case. If you are under 21 or still on a probationary licence, the limit is a flat **zero**.

I have watched visitors talk themselves into "it's just one stop" after a beer garden. It is the worst trade in Berlin: a three-euro scooter ride against your licence back home. After a drink, walk it or take the U-Bahn. The night trains and buns are covered in my [night transport guide](/post/berlin-night-transport), and honestly, staying off the scooter is part of a sensible night out here, the same way [general Berlin safety](/post/is-berlin-safe-to-visit-an-honest-2026-guide) is mostly about a few small habits.

## The rest of the rules, in one line each

- **One rider per scooter.** No passengers, ever. It is heavily enforced and it voids the insurance.
- **Minimum age 14** to ride under German law, though most apps require you to be **18** to unlock one.
- **No helmet legally required**, but I would wear one, especially on cobbles.
- **No phone in your hand** while riding, same as cycling.

## My honest take

E-scooters are a good tool for the right job. On a hot day, for a flat kilometre between two sights, or to save tired feet at the end of an afternoon, they are quick and cheap. For actually seeing Berlin's centre, where the whole point is the layers of history packed into a few streets, walking still wins, and a scooter zips you straight past the thing you came to understand.

That is really the trade. Use the scooter to move between areas, then slow down and walk the areas themselves. If you want the walking part done properly, with the context that makes the centre make sense, that is exactly what I do on my [free walking tour](/book-berlin-walking-tour/berlin-free-walking-tour-tip-based).
