Ask a Berliner where the Kurfürstendamm is and you will get a shrug and a direction. Ask a visitor and you get a plan for the whole afternoon. That gap is worth understanding before you go, because the Kurfürstendamm is 3.5 kilometres long and only the first stretch of it does what people expect.

I send guests west all the time, but I send them to a specific 300 metres of it, not to the street as a project. This is what is actually there, where the good part ends, and how to get in and out without wasting an afternoon on shop windows.

## Where the Kurfürstendamm actually starts

Here is the first thing almost everyone gets wrong. **KaDeWe is not on the Kurfürstendamm.** The department store sits on Tauentzienstraße, the shorter street that runs from Wittenbergplatz to Breitscheidplatz. The Kurfürstendamm only begins at Breitscheidplatz, at the broken church, and runs southwest from there to Rathenauplatz.

Together the two streets make one continuous walking axis of about 4.25 kilometres. Locals treat the whole thing as "Ku'damm" in conversation, which is why the confusion survives. It matters for one practical reason: if someone tells you to meet them "on the Ku'damm" near KaDeWe, they are describing a spot on a different street.

![Aerial view along Tauentzienstraße toward the Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church in Berlin](blog-drafts/kurfurstendamm-berlin/images/optimized/02-kurfurstendamm-berlin-tauentzienstrasse-axis.jpg)

_Tauentzienstraße seen from KaDeWe, looking west toward Breitscheidplatz. The Kurfürstendamm starts at the church in the distance._

The street itself is older than it looks. It began as a causeway out to the Grunewald hunting lodge, and from the 1870s it was widened into a 53 metre boulevard with four rows of plane trees on Bismarck's initiative. The date locals cite as its birthday is 5 May 1886, when the steam tram from Zoologischer Garten to Halensee started running.

## The first 1.4 kilometres are the part worth walking

Breitscheidplatz to Uhlandstraße is about 1.4 kilometres from Wittenbergplatz, roughly 18 minutes at a normal pace. Nearly everything a visitor comes west for is inside that stretch, along with the densest run of shops on the whole boulevard. Here is what sits in it.

![Shops and a U-Bahn entrance on the Kurfürstendamm with the Memorial Church tower behind](blog-drafts/kurfurstendamm-berlin/images/optimized/03-kurfurstendamm-berlin-street-ubahn-entrance.jpg)

_The busy stretch between Breitscheidplatz and Uhlandstraße. The U1 entrance here is your easy way back east._

### The Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church

The broken tower on Breitscheidplatz is the single most important thing on this street. The church was hit in November 1943 and the ruin was deliberately left standing when the new blue-glass church went up beside it. Entry is free. The memorial hall inside the old tower is open Monday to Saturday from 10:00 to 18:00, and shorter hours on Sunday.

Ten minutes inside that hall explains West Berlin better than an hour of walking does. I have written a longer piece on [how to visit the Memorial Church](/post/kaiser-wilhelm-memorial-church-berlin) if you want the detail.

![The Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church and the boulevard at Breitscheidplatz in Berlin](blog-drafts/kurfurstendamm-berlin/images/optimized/01-kurfurstendamm-berlin-cover.jpg)

_Breitscheidplatz on a normal summer afternoon. The Kurfürstendamm officially begins here._

### Bikini Berlin and a free view into the zoo

Directly behind the church is Bikini Berlin, a 1950s block turned into a concept mall. The building is worth five minutes on its own, but the reason to go in is the roof terrace, listed as open daily from 9:00 to 21:00, which looks straight down into the monkey enclosure of [Zoo Berlin](/post/zoo-berlin-vs-tierpark-berlin). It costs nothing. The mall's own shops run Monday to Saturday, so a Sunday visit is a terrace visit only.

The Monkey Bar upstairs in the 25hours hotel next door has the same view with a drink in your hand, and it appears in my list of [Berlin rooftop bars](/post/berlin-rooftop-bars).

![The Bikini Berlin building on Budapester Strasse at dusk](blog-drafts/kurfurstendamm-berlin/images/optimized/04-kurfurstendamm-berlin-bikini-berlin.jpg)

_Bikini Berlin, the 1950s block behind the church. The roof terrace above it is free and looks into the zoo._

### C/O Berlin

Across from Zoologischer Garten station, in the old Amerika Haus, is [C/O Berlin](https://www.co-berlin.org/en), a photography museum that is consistently one of the better exhibition spaces in the city. It is open daily from 11:00 to 20:00. Admission is 15 euros, 8 euros reduced, and free for anyone under 18. Whether you go depends on the current show, but the building alone is a genuine piece of Cold War history: it was the American cultural centre in West Berlin.

**One practical note about this cluster:** the church, Bikini Berlin, the zoo entrance, C/O Berlin and Zoologischer Garten station are all within about 300 metres of each other. If you only have an hour in the west of the city, spend all of it here and do not walk further.

## Savignyplatz is the turn most people should make

About 400 metres north of the Kurfürstendamm, between Uhlandstraße and Adenauerplatz, is Savignyplatz. Two leafy squares split by Kantstraße, restaurants tucked under the S-Bahn arches, and the Bücherbogen bookshops built into the railway vaults.

This is where I go and it is where I send people. **On a warm evening, Savignyplatz beats another kilometre of Kurfürstendamm every time.** The area is old West Berlin in the way that actually survives: no landmark, no ticket, just a neighbourhood that kept its restaurants. Walk up Fasanenstraße or Knesebeckstraße from the boulevard and you are there in five minutes.

![Savignyplatz in Berlin Charlottenburg with trees and old apartment buildings](blog-drafts/kurfurstendamm-berlin/images/optimized/05-kurfurstendamm-berlin-savignyplatz.jpg)

## What is west of Adenauerplatz, and who it is for

Past Adenauerplatz the Kurfürstendamm keeps its width and loses its reasons. Offices, hotels, car showrooms, tall apartment blocks. It is real Berlin and people live good lives on it, but there is nothing there that justifies the walk from Breitscheidplatz.

Two exceptions are worth naming, because both are genuinely good and both deserve a dedicated trip rather than tired feet at the end of a shopping walk.

**The Schaubühne at Lehniner Platz** is one of the most important theatres in Germany, and many performances carry English surtitles. Its building is Erich Mendelsohn's Universum cinema from 1928, curved like a ship's hull and probably the first modernist cinema built anywhere. Mendelsohn's own line about the design was that Buster Keaton did not need a baroque palace. Check the [Schaubühne programme](https://www.schaubuehne.de/en/) before you plan an evening around it.

![The lit Schaubühne theatre sign on the western Kurfürstendamm at night](blog-drafts/kurfurstendamm-berlin/images/optimized/06-kurfurstendamm-berlin-schaubuehne.jpg)

_The Schaubühne occupies Mendelsohn's 1928 Universum cinema. Come for a play, not as the end of a long walk._

**Rathenauplatz**, where the street ends, has two concrete Cadillacs standing on end in the middle of a traffic circle. Wolf Vostell put them there in 1987 for West Berlin's 750th anniversary, when the whole axis was temporarily lined with sculpture. Most of that sculpture boulevard is gone. The Cadillacs stayed, and people still argue about them. It is a good story and a bad plan: you are 4.25 kilometres from where you started, and the nearest useful station is the Ringbahn at Halensee.

## Why West Berlin needed a second city centre

The reason this street exists in its current form is simple and it is the part I find most worth explaining on a tour. When the city was divided, the historic centre, the museums, the cathedral and the old government quarter all ended up in the East. West Berlin was left with no downtown at all.

So it built one here, around a bombed church, on a boulevard that had been a fashionable shopping and cinema street in the 1920s. Department stores, cinemas, the zoo, the main station, the theatres and the bright lights were all concentrated on this axis on purpose. The Kurfürstendamm was not just a shopping street. It was the shop window of the West, three kilometres of neon aimed at people who could see it from the other side.

![Tauentzienstrasse in West Berlin in April 1978](blog-drafts/kurfurstendamm-berlin/images/optimized/07-kurfurstendamm-berlin-tauentzienstrasse-1978.jpg)

_Tauentzienstraße in 1978. For forty years this axis was the whole downtown of West Berlin._

That job ended in 1989. The old centre came back, Mitte took the visitors, and the Kurfürstendamm went from being the centre of a walled-in half-city to being one good shopping street among several. If you want the background, I have written about [how Berlin was divided](/post/how-berlin-was-divided-a-simple-guide-to-east-vs-west) and [why Berlin has no old town](/post/why-berlin-doesn-t-have-a-beautiful-old-town-and-why-that-s-the-point) in more detail.

## Getting there and getting out

The Kurfürstendamm is closer than most visitors think. From Alexanderplatz it is about 15 to 20 minutes.

- **U-Bahn:** U1 and U9 stop at Kurfürstendamm station, right at Breitscheidplatz. U2 and U9 reach Zoologischer Garten. U1, U2 and U3 all stop at Wittenbergplatz.
- **S-Bahn:** the Stadtbahn lines S3, S5, S7 and S9 stop at Zoologischer Garten, which is the easiest arrival if you are coming from Hauptbahnhof, Friedrichstraße or Alexanderplatz.
- **Zones:** all of it is inside zone AB, so a normal Berlin ticket covers it.
- **Getting out:** the useful exits are U1/U9 Kurfürstendamm, U1 Uhlandstraße and U7 Adenauerplatz. West of Adenauerplatz there is no U-Bahn on the street at all, which is the real reason to turn back there.

If you are still deciding where to base yourself, my guide to [where to stay in Berlin](/post/where-to-stay-in-berlin-best-neighborhoods-for-every-type-of-tourist) covers whether the west makes sense for your trip.

## So is the Kurfürstendamm worth it in 2026?

Yes, with a strict limit on how much of it you walk.

Go for Breitscheidplatz: the broken church, the free roof terrace, the zoo, C/O Berlin, and the fact that this square is the physical evidence of a city that had to invent a second downtown. Give it an hour or two. Then either turn north to Savignyplatz for the evening, or take the U-Bahn back east.

Do not walk the full 3.5 kilometres expecting it to keep getting better. It does not, and I have made that mistake myself on a hot afternoon that I do not recommend repeating. The Kurfürstendamm rewards a short, deliberate visit and punishes completionism.

My [free walking tour](/book-berlin-walking-tour/berlin-free-walking-tour-tip-based) stays in the historic centre in the east, so the two do not overlap. If anything they work well as a pair: the tour explains the city that was destroyed and divided, and an afternoon on the Kurfürstendamm shows you what one half of it built instead.
