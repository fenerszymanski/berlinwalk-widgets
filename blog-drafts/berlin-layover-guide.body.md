You land at BER with a long gap before your next flight, and the same question starts turning over in your head. Do you sit in the terminal for six hours, or do you take a train into the city and actually see some of Berlin? A Berlin layover can be one of the best surprises of a trip or a small logistical trap, and the difference is almost always about time and buffers, not distance.

My advice: do not decide with your heart at the gate. Decide with a clock. Berlin's historic centre is genuinely close to the airport, but the airport itself takes time to leave and re-enter, and that is the part travellers underestimate. Work out your real free time first, then choose how much city to attempt.

## A Berlin layover: can you actually leave the airport?

The first thing to check is not the train. It is whether you are allowed to walk out of the airport at all.

If you are flying between two places inside the Schengen area, there is usually no passport control at BER, and if your documents let you enter Germany, you can leave the airport freely. Most visa-exempt travellers and Schengen-visa holders are in this group. The exception matters: a small number of nationalities need an **airport transit visa (an A-visa)** for Germany, and that visa does not let you leave the transit zone. If that applies to you, you stay airside no matter how long the layover is. When in doubt, check the [German Federal Foreign Office transit page](https://www.auswaertiges-amt.de/en/visa-service/buergerservice/faq/33-transit/606710) before you plan anything.

There is also a newer detail. If your onward flight leaves the Schengen area, you now pass an exit check on the way back, including the EU Entry/Exit System face and fingerprint step. It is quick once, but it is one more line to stand in, so it belongs in your time budget.

My advice: sort out the legal question before the fun one. Everything below assumes you are allowed to enter Germany during the layover.

## How long a layover do you really need?

This is where most Berlin layover plans go wrong. People look at the 35 to 45 minute train ride, imagine they have loads of time, and forget everything wrapped around it.

A realistic layover has five parts, not one: getting off the plane and through any arrival checks, the train into the city, your actual city time, the train back, and then security and boarding again at BER. The train is the short part. The buffers are the long part.

Here is the rule I use, and it is deliberately cautious:

- **Be back at BER security about 90 minutes before a Schengen departure**, and closer to **2 hours before a non-Schengen departure**.
- Give the train **45 minutes each way** as a safe planning number, even though it can be faster.
- Add time at the start for leaving the plane and, if you arrived from outside Schengen, passport control.

Do that maths and the pattern is clear. **Under about 4 hours, stay airside.** Around **5 to 6 hours** gives you a real, unhurried couple of hours in the historic centre. **7 hours or more** and you can slow down, sit by the river, or add a museum. If a delay eats into the layover, the honest move is to shrink the plan, not to run for a train.

## Getting from BER into the city

The station is called Flughafen BER and it sits directly under Terminal 1, one level down. You do not need a taxi and you should not pay for one on a layover.

Two trains matter:

- **The S9** runs straight to **Alexanderplatz** in the middle of Berlin, no change needed, roughly every 20 minutes. This is the one I would take on a layover, because Alexanderplatz drops you at the start of the historic centre and the walking route below.
- **The FEX Airport Express** reaches **Berlin Hauptbahnhof** in about 23 minutes, every 15 minutes, and also stops at Südkreuz and Potsdamer Platz. It is faster to the main station but leaves you a little further from the old centre.

BER sits in fare zone C, so you need an **ABC ticket**, not the AB ticket most of Berlin uses. A single ABC ride is **5.00 euros** in 2026, and if you expect more than two trips, the ABC day ticket at **12.90 euros** pays for itself. Buy it from the machines on the platform or in the BVG app before you board, and keep it, because there are ticket checks. Confirm live departures on the [official BER public transport page](https://ber.berlin-airport.de/en/orientation/getting-here/public-transport.html) before you commit, because engineering works can change the timetable. If the zones confuse you, my guide to [Berlin AB and ABC tickets](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-ab-abc-ticket-zones) explains exactly which one to buy, and for the full airport-to-city breakdown, see [how to get from BER to Alexanderplatz](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/how-to-get-from-berlin-airport-to-alexanderplatz-the-easy-way).

## What you can actually see in a couple of hours

Here is the good news that makes a Berlin layover worth it. Once you step out at Alexanderplatz, almost everything a first-time visitor pictures is within a single, flat, walkable line. You do not need to plan a route. You just walk west and the city hands you its landmarks in order.

From Alexanderplatz you pass under the **TV Tower**, cross to the red-brick **Rotes Rathaus**, reach the **Berliner Dom** and the museums of **Museum Island** on the Spree, then continue down **Unter den Linden** past the old university and opera to the **Brandenburg Gate**. That is the core of Berlin, and on foot it is comfortably a couple of hours with photo stops. It is, more or less, the same ground my walking tour covers.

![Unter den Linden boulevard in daylight with the Berlin TV Tower rising in the distance](blog-drafts/images/berlin-layover-guide/optimized/05-unter-den-linden-tv-tower.jpg)
_Unter den Linden points straight back toward the TV Tower. Orient by the tower and you cannot really get lost on a layover walk._

My advice: do not try to add a far-flung sight on a layover. The single biggest mistake is chasing one extra place across town and turning a calm walk into a train-anxiety afternoon. Pick the straight central line and do it properly.

If you want to slow down for ten minutes, the riverbank around Museum Island is the place to do it. The **Berliner Dom** reflected in the Spree, with the colonnade of the Alte Nationalgalerie beside it, is the postcard most people did not know they came for.

![The green-domed Berliner Dom reflected in the calm Spree river beside the Museum Island colonnade](blog-drafts/images/berlin-layover-guide/optimized/04-berliner-dom-museum-island.jpg)
_Museum Island on the Spree. Even with no time to go inside, the walk along the water is one of the best few minutes in central Berlin._

The **Brandenburg Gate** is the natural turning point. Reach it, take your photo, and this is the moment to check your clock and start back toward Alexanderplatz or hop on the U-Bahn or S-Bahn for the return leg. Early in the morning, before the tour groups arrive, the square in front of the gate can be almost empty, which is a quiet reward for the layover traveller who came in on an early flight.

![The Brandenburg Gate in Berlin photographed in the early morning with an empty square in front](blog-drafts/images/berlin-layover-guide/optimized/06-brandenburg-gate-morning.jpg)
_The Brandenburg Gate early in the day. A morning layover often means you get this view with almost nobody in it._

## Where to leave your bag

You will not enjoy any of this dragging a cabin bag down Unter den Linden. Sort out storage before you leave the airport.

**At BER**, there are luggage lockers in Terminal 1 on level U1, roughly 7 to 10 euros a day depending on bag size. **In the city**, the big stations have lockers too, including Hauptbahnhof, Alexanderplatz, Friedrichstraße and Ostbahnhof, so if you come in on the S9 you can lock a bag at Alexanderplatz and start walking straight away. Prices and space vary, and on busy days lockers fill up, so have a backup in mind. My [Berlin luggage storage guide](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/luggage-storage-in-berlin-2026) maps the station lockers and the staffed drop-off apps if the machines are full.

My advice: if your onward flight is checked through and your hold bag is already gone, this gets much easier. If you are still carrying everything, a locker at Alexanderplatz is the move that saves your layover.
