Rush hour on the U8, doors closing at Alexanderplatz, and a first-time visitor is stuck behind three people who are not moving. He says "sorry, sorry" twice, gets a confused glance from one of them, and squeezes past anyway just as the beeping stops. Nobody was rude. Nobody helped either.

My advice: learn one German word before you learn anything else, and it is not "Hallo." I have made that mistake before, walking around Berlin for weeks leaning only on English "sorry" and wondering why people looked briefly puzzled instead of just stepping aside. The word is **Entschuldigung**, and once it becomes automatic, doors open, corridors clear, and small interactions stop feeling like guesswork.

## Why "Entschuldigung" works where "sorry" doesn't

"Sorry" is understood almost everywhere in central Berlin. That is not the problem. The problem is what it signals.

In German, **Entschuldigung** does three jobs at once: it means excuse me, sorry, and pardon me, depending on the moment. Say it before you interrupt someone, say it while you squeeze past, say it to open a question to a stranger. English "sorry" only really covers the second one, so when a tourist uses it to get someone's attention or to ask a question, there is a half-second of confusion before the other person switches languages and responds. It usually still works. It is just slower, and it reads as "visitor" rather than "someone who has been here a few days."

The word itself comes from **die Schuld**, meaning guilt or fault. Literally, Entschuldigung means something close to "de-guilting," which is a strangely honest way to think about it: you are formally removing the fault from the moment, not performing an apology.

## How to actually say it, without the textbook accent

Written out, Entschuldigung looks intimidating. Spoken, it breaks into four manageable pieces: **ent-SHOOL-dee-goong**. The stress lands on the second syllable, and the ending is a soft "goong," not a hard "gung" like the English word "gun."

If that is still a mouthful in the moment, Berliners themselves often clip it. **"'Tschuldigung"** (dropping the first syllable entirely) is completely normal casual speech, and nobody will think less of you for using the short version. There is also a formal and informal split worth knowing:

- **Entschuldigung** — safe in every situation, formal or casual, to a stranger or a shopkeeper.
- **Entschuldige** / **entschuldige bitte** — informal, for someone your age or a friend.
- **Entschuldigen Sie** / **Entschuldigen Sie bitte** — more formal, extra polite, useful with police, older Berliners, or official staff.

As a visitor, you do not need to master that split. Plain **Entschuldigung** is the correct default almost everywhere in Berlin, including everywhere on this list.

## The three Berlin moments where you will actually need it

Three situations come up on almost every walking day in central Berlin, and they are not the same kind of "excuse me."

![The U5 platform at Alexanderplatz U-Bahn station, with passengers waiting near the green pillars](blog-drafts/images/entschuldigung-berlin/optimized/03-alexanderplatz-u5-platform.jpg)
_Alexanderplatz alone has three U-Bahn lines and an S-Bahn station stacked on top of each other. Most of a Berlin day's "Entschuldigung" moments start on a platform like this one._

**Getting past someone.** This is the U-Bahn and S-Bahn doors, a narrow museum corridor, a crowded market stall. You say it while moving, briefly and clearly, and people make room almost automatically because the word itself signals "I am passing, not pushing."

**Getting someone's attention.** This is asking a Späti owner a question, flagging a waiter, or stopping a stranger for directions. Here Entschuldigung comes first, as an opener, before the actual question. "Entschuldigung, sprechen Sie Englisch?" lands better than jumping straight into English, even though most people in the city centre will answer in English either way.

**Recovering from a small mistake.** Stepping on someone's foot, blocking a doorway without noticing, cutting in a queue by accident. Here it is closer to true "sorry," said once, clearly, and then you move on. Repeating it three times in English is the tourist habit that reads as overcorrection rather than politeness.

## Entschuldigung vs. Tut mir leid vs. Verzeihung

Three different German phrases get translated as "sorry" in a dictionary, and they are not interchangeable.

**Entschuldigung** is the everyday, functional one: excuse me, pardon me, sorry for the small thing that just happened. Use it constantly.

**Tut mir leid** ("it does me sorrow," roughly) is heavier. It is what you say when you actually feel bad about something, more like genuine regret than a passing bump on the platform. Save it for moments that call for real sympathy, not for squeezing through a door.

**Verzeihung** comes from *verzeihen*, "to forgive," and it is older and more formal than Entschuldigung. You will hear it from older Berliners or read it on a sign, but as a visitor you can use Entschuldigung in every place Verzeihung would also work.

If you only remember one, it is Entschuldigung. It is the word that fits the most situations with the least risk of sounding either too casual or too dramatic.

## Small words that pair naturally with it

A handful of short words turn Entschuldigung from a single move into a real interaction, and they are worth learning together because you will use all of them within seconds of each other.

- **Danke** (thank you) — the automatic reply once someone lets you through.
- **Bitte** (please, or you're welcome) — works both ways, before and after.
- **Kein Problem** (no problem) — what you will often hear back, sometimes shortened to just "passt schon" (it's fine).

None of these require grammar. They are reflexes, the same way "cheers" or "no worries" are reflexes in English, and Berliners register them instantly as an attempt at basic courtesy rather than fluency.

## The mistake most tourists make with this word

The most common mistake is not mispronouncing it. It is not using it at all, and defaulting entirely to English "sorry" for every version of the situation, from passing someone to genuinely apologizing to opening a question. It works, because Berlin is used to visitors, but it keeps every interaction one small step slower and more effortful than it needs to be.

The second most common mistake runs the other way: using Entschuldigung to open every single sentence, even when you are not interrupting anyone. If you walk up to an empty counter with nobody ahead of you, you do not need it. Save it for the moment you are actually asking for someone's attention or space, and it keeps its impact.
