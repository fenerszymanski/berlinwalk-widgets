Most people never think about **Berlin emergency numbers** until the moment they suddenly need one, and that is the worst time to be guessing. Someone feels faint on a hot U-Bahn platform at Alexanderplatz, a wallet vanishes in a crowd near Warschauer Straße, or a small burn happens in a holiday flat kitchen. In that first minute you do not want to be searching your phone in a second language. You want the one number, and you want to know it works.

The good news is that Berlin, like the rest of Germany, keeps this simple. There are really only two numbers to memorise, and a short list of others that are useful to know before you actually need them. I have walked this city almost every day for years, and the honest truth is that serious emergencies are rare for visitors. But knowing exactly who to call turns a scary moment into a manageable one.

## Berlin emergency numbers: 112 and 110

If you remember nothing else from this page, remember these two.

**112 is for a medical emergency or a fire.** It reaches the ambulance and the fire brigade. Call it when someone is seriously hurt, has collapsed, is having chest pain or trouble breathing, or when there is a fire. This is the single European emergency number, so the same 112 works across the whole EU, not only in Berlin.

**110 is for the police.** Call it for a crime in progress, a theft or assault, a threat to your safety, or a serious traffic accident where the road needs to be secured.

Both numbers are **free**, and both work from any phone. You do not need credit, a German SIM, or even an unlocked screen. On most phones you can reach 112 straight from the locked emergency-call screen. In Berlin the dispatchers can handle English, so do not let the language stop you from calling. Stay on the line, give your location first, then say what happened.

A quick note that surprises a lot of visitors: **Germany does not use 911.** If you dial 911 out of habit, nothing useful happens. And 112 will not get you the police, just as 110 will not send an ambulance. Match the number to the problem: injury or fire is 112, crime is 110.

## When it is serious but not a 112 emergency: 116117

This is the number that saves your evening more often than 112 ever will. **116117 is the on-call medical service** for when you genuinely need a doctor but it is not life-threatening: a high fever, a nasty ear infection, a sprain, a migraine that will not lift. It runs around the clock, including nights and weekends, exactly when normal doctors' practices in Berlin are closed.

Think of it this way. If the situation is frightening or could be dangerous, that is 112. If you simply need medical advice or a doctor and it is 9pm on a Sunday, that is 116117. Calling 112 for a cold blocks the line for someone in real danger, and the dispatcher will usually redirect you anyway. If you are still unsure whether you need a doctor at all, my guide on [seeing a doctor in Berlin as a visitor](/post/doctor-in-berlin) walks through how the system actually works.

## Pharmacies at night and on Sundays

Berlin pharmacies, the shops marked with a big red **A** for _Apotheke_, mostly keep normal shop hours and close on Sundays. But the city always keeps a rotation of **emergency pharmacies** open overnight and on Sundays and public holidays, so you are never truly stuck without medicine.

To find the one nearest you right now, check the official Notdienstfinder run by the [Apothekerkammer Berlin](https://www.akberlin.de/patientinnen/notdienstfinder). Expect a small emergency service fee for handovers late at night, on Sundays and on holidays. It is a few euro, not a scam. For the full picture on how pharmacies work here, including English help and what they can hand out without a prescription, I have a separate [Berlin pharmacy guide](/post/pharmacy-in-berlin).

## If your wallet, cards or phone are stolen

Pickpocketing is the most common thing that actually goes wrong for visitors, usually in a crowd: a packed U-Bahn door, a busy tram, the queue at a big sight. It is rarely dramatic, and it is almost never dangerous. But it needs two quick moves.

First, **block your cards immediately.** Germany has one central card-blocking hotline, **116 116**, that can freeze bank and credit cards day or night. From outside Germany, dial **+49 116 116**. Your own bank's app or number works too, and often faster, so use whichever you can reach first.

Second, **file a police report.** For a straightforward theft with no danger, you do not need to call 110. You can report it at any police station, or online through the Berlin police Internetwache. Ask for the loss report, the _Verlustanzeige_. You will want that document number for your travel insurance claim, and it is the paperwork that makes replacing things far smoother later.

![A busy crowd of people on a Berlin U-Bahn station platform](blog-drafts/berlin-emergency-numbers/images/optimized/05-berlin-emergency-numbers-crowd.jpg)

Crowded platforms and train doors are where pickpockets work. Keep your phone and wallet zipped away in the crush.

One thing worth separating: if you simply **left a bag on the U-Bahn or tram**, that is not a theft and not an emergency. That is lost property, handled by the transport lost-and-found, and I cover exactly how to chase it in my [lost property in Berlin guide](/post/lost-property-berlin). Knowing which situation you are in saves you calling the wrong place. ## If you lose your passport

A missing passport feels like the end of the trip. It is not, but it does take a couple of steps. Report it lost or stolen to the police first and get that loss report, then contact **your own country's embassy or consulate in Berlin.** Most major countries have one here, and they are the only ones who can issue an emergency travel document to get you home. Do this on a weekday if you can, because embassy consular hours are short and an emergency passport is not an instant over-the-counter thing.

The practical move: photograph your passport, ID card and travel insurance details before you fly, and store the pictures somewhere you can reach without your phone, such as your email. I have watched that one habit turn a lost-passport panic into a calm embassy appointment.

## Poison and mental-health crises

Two more numbers that are good to have and easy to forget.

For **suspected poisoning**, a swallowed cleaning product, the wrong dose of medication, an unknown mushroom, Berlin has a dedicated poison emergency line at the Charité: **030 19240**, staffed around the clock. For anything immediately life-threatening, it is still 112 first.

For a **mental-health or psychological crisis**, your own or someone else's, the [Berliner Krisendienst](https://www.berliner-krisendienst.de/en/) offers free, confidential support and runs region-based crisis lines that are reachable at any hour. Their website lists the right number for each part of the city, and they can help in a calm, non-judgemental way. If there is any immediate danger to life, call 112.

## A calm plan for the moment it happens

Here is the honest local perspective after years of walking Berlin with groups. You almost certainly will not need any of this. Berlin is a big, safe, well-run city, and the most likely problem on your trip is a pickpocket in a crowd, not anything that reaches an ambulance.

So do not carry fear around with you. Do one small thing instead: save **112** and **116117** in your phone before you go, screenshot this page, and photograph your key documents. If something does go wrong, take one breath, decide whether it is danger (112), crime (110) or just a problem to sort out, and act from there. That single moment of clarity is worth more than any list.

When you join me on the [walking tour](/), I am happy to point out the nearest pharmacy or station along the way, because knowing where things are is half of feeling safe in a new city. The rest is just knowing which number to call, and now you do.
