Almost every guest on my tour asks some version of the same question: where is Berlin's old town? And the honest answer, which I give standing between concrete blocks at Alexanderplatz, is that the war and the postwar planners took most of it. But Berlin keeps a secret on its western edge. There is a real walled old town inside the city limits, with a moated Renaissance fortress next to it, and most visitors never go. It is called Spandau, it is older than Berlin itself, and a normal AB ticket gets you there.

That last part matters more than it sounds. Spandau feels like a separate small town, and locals joke that Spandauers say they are "going to Berlin" when they ride into the centre. But it sits inside fare zone B. No extension ticket, no zone C, none of the planning a Potsdam trip needs. You tap the same ticket you used this morning and half an hour later you are standing on a market square that got its town charter in **1232**, five years before Berlin first shows up in any document.

## Why Spandau Berlin is worth half a day

Berlin is a city of fragments. I have written before about [why Berlin has no beautiful old town](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/why-berlin-doesn-t-have-a-beautiful-old-town-and-why-that-s-the-point) and what the [rebuilt Nikolaiviertel](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/nikolaiviertel-rebuilt-old-town) really is. Spandau is the counterexample. The street plan is medieval, a stretch of the 14th-century town wall still stands at full height, the church in the middle is 14th-century brick, and the oldest town house in all of Berlin holds the tourist office.

My advice: do not treat Spandau as a list of sights to tick off. Treat it as one connected walk of about half a day. Citadel first, then the old town across the bridge, then the quiet Kolk corner that most people miss even when they make it this far. The whole loop is flat, compact and stroller-friendly, and in summer the moat and the Havel water do a lot of the work.

## The Citadel: a complete star fort with Berlin's oldest building

![The star-point of a citadel bastion in the foreground with the Havel river and the roofs of Spandau old town behind it](blog-drafts/spandau-berlin/images/optimized/zitadelle-bastion-altstadt.jpg)

One of the four bastions, with the old town just across the water. Fortress and town are a ten-minute walk apart.

The Zitadelle Spandau is one of the best-preserved Renaissance fortresses in Europe, and unlike most famous forts it was never blown apart and rebuilt. The four bastions, the moat and the gatehouse are the real 16th-century layout. Italian military engineers designed it between **1559 and 1594** to guard the meeting point of the Havel and Spree rivers, which is the whole reason a town existed here in the first place.

Inside the walls, the thing to climb is the **Juliusturm**. The tower is about 700 years older than the fortress around it, built in the early 13th century as the keep of the medieval castle, which makes it the oldest surviving building in Berlin. The spiral staircase is tight, the platform view runs across the moat, the old town and half the Havel valley, and the name still means something in German politics: from **1874 to 1919** the tower stored 120 million gold marks of French war reparations, the empire's emergency fund, so a "Juliusturm" is still what German commentators call a fat state reserve.

The surprise inside is the permanent exhibition **Unveiled. Berlin and its monuments**, which collects the political statues Berlin took down and did not know what to do with, including the huge granite head of the Lenin monument that stood in Friedrichshain until 1991. Here, unusually for a museum, you are allowed to touch most of the exhibits. It is the strangest room in Berlin for thinking about how cities edit their own memory. The fortress has darker layers too, including years as a military research site under the Nazis, and the exhibitions do not hide that.

![The curved gable of the citadel gatehouse with the colorful Prussian eagle coat of arms above the entrance](blog-drafts/spandau-berlin/images/optimized/zitadelle-gatehouse-crest.jpg)

The Prussian crest over the single gatehouse. One way in, one way out, which is the point of a fortress.

The practical details, checked in July 2026: open **Friday to Wednesday 10:00 to 17:00, Thursday 13:00 to 20:00**, last entry 30 minutes before closing. Admission is **4.50 euros**, reduced 2.50, family ticket 10, children under six free, and one ticket covers the museums, the exhibitions and the Juliusturm. Two warnings from experience. The citadel is a summer concert venue, and on event days museum hours can shift, so check the [official citadel site](https://www.zitadelle-berlin.de/en/your-visit/) on the day. And on Thursdays nothing opens before 13:00, which ruins a morning plan that started with the fortress.

Getting there is its own small pleasure: the U7 station is literally called **Zitadelle**, and the fortress gate is a five-minute walk from the platform.

## The old town: what to actually look at

Cross the bridge on Am Juliusturm and you are in the Altstadt, a compact oval of medieval streets that today works as a normal shopping district. That mix confuses people who expect a museum village. Ignore the chain-store ground floors and read the street plan instead: the lanes still bend the way they bent 700 years ago.

![The brick-gothic north side of St. Nikolai church in Spandau old town with its stepped buttresses in sunshine](blog-drafts/spandau-berlin/images/optimized/nikolaikirche.jpg)

St. Nikolai, the 14th-century brick church at the centre of the old town oval.

Three stops deserve real time. First, **St. Nikolai** on Reformationsplatz, a 14th-century brick-gothic church that earned its place in German history in **1539**, when Elector Joachim II took communion in both bread and wine here and brought the Reformation into Brandenburg. The church is usually open around midday and the parish runs occasional tower climbs; the statue of Joachim outside tells you how seriously Spandau takes the story. It is also a useful comparison piece if you have already seen [St. Mary's Church](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/st-mary-s-church-the-medieval-survivor-in-the-shadow-of-the-tv-tower) in the centre with me.

![Reformationsplatz square in Spandau with a small green park, trees and a row of colorful old town houses](blog-drafts/spandau-berlin/images/optimized/reformationsplatz.jpg)

Reformationsplatz, the calmest square in the old town and a natural coffee stop.

Second, the **Gotisches Haus** on Breite Straße, a Gothic town house from the 15th century and the oldest surviving residential building in Berlin. Entry is **free**, the ground floor is Spandau's tourist information with maps and honest advice, and the upper rooms show the town history exhibition. In summer it is open Monday to Saturday from 10:00 and Sundays from 12:00.

![The restored facade of the Gotisches Haus in Spandau, a late-medieval brick town house that now holds the tourist information](blog-drafts/spandau-berlin/images/optimized/gotisches-haus.jpg)

The Gotisches Haus on Breite Straße. Free entry, and the best first stop if you want a paper map in your hand.

Third, walk the market streets themselves, Breite Straße and Carl-Schurz-Straße, down to the **Havel riverside at Lindenufer**. This is where Spandau stops feeling like an errand district and starts feeling like a river town, with swans, moored boats and benches facing the water. In December the same streets host one of Berlin's biggest Christmas markets, which is worth remembering if you are reading this for a winter trip.

## The Kolk: the corner most visitors miss

![A cobbled lane in the Kolk quarter of Spandau running alongside the six-metre-high remnant of the medieval town wall](blog-drafts/spandau-berlin/images/optimized/stadtmauer-kolk.jpg)

Hoher Steinweg in the Kolk. The brick wall on the right is Spandau's 14th-century town wall at its original height.

North of the main road, squeezed between the Altstadt and the citadel bridge, is the **Kolk**, the oldest inhabited corner of Spandau and the part that still looks the way people imagine a Brandenburg town. Cobbled lanes, a handful of crooked houses, the small 1848 church of St. Marien am Behnitz, and along **Hoher Steinweg** a surviving stretch of the medieval town wall, about six metres of 14th-century brick standing at full height with the remains of a defensive tower behind it.

There is no ticket and no museum here. You walk it in fifteen quiet minutes, and it is the best free exhibit in the district. I bring a coffee from the Altstadt and take the wall lane on the way to the citadel bridge; that order turns a transfer walk into the most atmospheric part of the loop.

## Getting there in 2026, and the weekend trap

For most of 2026 the simple advice is: **take the U7**. Spandau has three useful stations on that line. Zitadelle drops you at the fortress, Altstadt Spandau puts you under the old town, and Rathaus Spandau is the terminus next to the big mainline station. From Mitte, the S-Bahn is usually faster: the **S3 or S9** runs from Alexanderplatz and Hauptbahnhof straight to Berlin-Spandau station in about half an hour, and the whole trip is inside [zone B on a normal AB ticket](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-ab-abc-ticket-zones).

But this year has an asterisk. The Stadtbahn construction that I covered in the [leaving Berlin by train guide](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/leaving-berlin-by-train) mostly affects regional and long-distance trains, and long-distance trains are skipping Berlin-Spandau station entirely for much of the year. That does not touch a normal sightseeing trip. What does touch it are a few **construction weekends when the Stadtbahn S-Bahn itself stops**, including 24 to 27 July and 31 July to 3 August 2026, from Friday evening to late Sunday night. On those weekends, do not fight the replacement buses. Ride the U7 and arrive relaxed. Check the [VBB construction page](https://www.vbb.de/unterwegs-im-vbb/baustellenkalender/) if your dates are close to a weekend.

## What about Spandau Prison?

People ask, so here is the honest answer: there is nothing to visit. The Allied prison on Wilhelmstraße, where Rudolf Hess was the last inmate, was demolished in **1987**, deliberately, so it could never become a shrine. The rubble was ground down and scattered, and a British forces shopping centre was built on the site. Spandau's real history is the 800-year-old town, not the prison, and the town is the part you can still walk through.

If medieval Berlin is the thread you are pulling, that is literally the first hour of my [free walking tour](https://www.berlinwalk.com/book-berlin-walking-tour/berlin-free-walking-tour-tip-based): about 2 hours through the historic centre from Alexanderplatz, where I show you what survived in Mitte, and Spandau makes the perfect same-week companion trip to see what a complete version looks like. For the founding-date rivalry between the two towns, the [1237 story](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/why-is-berlin-founding-year-1237) is a good follow-up read.
