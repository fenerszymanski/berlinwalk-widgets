Most people meet Berlin from above by accident. You come out of the U-Bahn at Alexanderplatz, look up at the TV Tower, see the online price is now over 24 euros, and quietly decide the view can wait. That is a fair reaction, but it also misses the point: Berlin has five different public high views, and the most famous one is the most expensive by a wide margin.

I get asked which one is worth it often enough that it is worth laying out properly. The honest answer is that it depends on whether you care most about height, money, open air, or not having to book. Below is what each one actually costs in 2026, how you get up, and which I would send you to.

## The five best Berlin observation decks, from 24 to 203 metres

Berlin is famously flat, so a paid viewpoint is one of the few ways to actually read the city: how the old centre sits against the Spree, how far the Tiergarten stretches, how the socialist high-rises line up along Karl-Marx-Allee. The five public options range from the free Reichstag dome at roughly 24 metres to the enclosed TV Tower deck at 203 metres, and they feel completely different once you are up there.

## Fernsehturm (TV Tower): the highest, and the one to book

![The Berlin TV Tower rising above the Mitte rooftops in low golden light](blog-drafts/images/berlin-observation-decks/optimized/02-berlin-tv-tower-fernsehturm.jpg)
_The Fernsehturm at 203 metres is the highest public view in Berlin, and the one most worth booking ahead._

The Fernsehturm is the tower you can see from half of Berlin, and its enclosed deck at 203 metres is the highest public view in the city. A high-speed lift gets you up in about 40 seconds, and there is a slowly revolving restaurant, Sphere, just above the deck if you want to eat with the skyline turning past you.

Here is the catch that surprises people. **Standard adult entry starts around 24.50 euros online and uses dynamic pricing, so the price climbs as your date gets closer and for popular evening slots.** The walk-up queue at Alexanderplatz can also be long in summer. My advice: if you specifically want the highest, most iconic Berlin view, book a timed slot on the [official TV Tower site](https://tv-turm.de/en/) a few days ahead. It is both cheaper and faster than turning up and hoping. If you are still unsure it is for you, I wrote a longer honest take in [is the Berlin TV Tower worth it](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/is-the-berlin-tv-tower-worth-it).

## Panoramapunkt at Potsdamer Platz: the best value

![View over Berlin from the Panoramapunkt terrace showing the Berliner Dom and the TV Tower](blog-drafts/images/berlin-observation-decks/optimized/03-berlin-panoramapunkt-view.jpg)
_Looking across Mitte from the open Panoramapunkt terrace at Potsdamer Platz. My pick for most visitors._

If you want my single recommendation for most visitors, it is the Panoramapunkt on the Kollhoff Tower at [Potsdamer Platz](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/potsdamer-platz-berlin). **A standard adult ticket is 9 euros, reduced 7 euros, and you ride the fastest lift in Europe to the top in about 20 seconds.**

What makes it worth it is that the top is an open terrace at 100 metres, not a glass box. You feel the wind, there is a café, and there is an open-air exhibition about the history of Potsdamer Platz right where the Wall once cut the square in two. You can walk up and buy a ticket, or skip the short line with an online ticket from the [official Panoramapunkt site](https://panoramapunkt.de/en/). For nine euros, this is the easiest yes on the list.

## Park Inn Panorama Terrasse: the cheapest roof and the best TV Tower photo

![Wide open-air sunset panorama over Berlin seen from the Park Inn roof at Alexanderplatz](blog-drafts/images/berlin-observation-decks/optimized/04-berlin-park-inn-panorama-terrasse-sunset.jpg)
_Sunset from the open Park Inn roof at Alexanderplatz. The cheapest ticketed roof, and it faces the TV Tower head-on._

Almost nobody plans for this one, which is exactly why I like it. The Park Inn hotel on Alexanderplatz opens its 39th-floor roof, the Panorama Terrasse, at around 120 metres. **Entry is about 6 euros, it is a genuine open-air rooftop, and it is almost never crowded.**

The reason to come here is not just the price. It faces the TV Tower straight on, so it is the best place in the city to photograph the Fernsehturm itself rather than shoot from under it. Take the lift from the lobby, pay at the top, and you have an open roof more or less to yourself. If you like your views from ground level too, the free options in [the best views in Berlin you can find on foot](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/the-best-views-in-berlin-you-can-find-on-foot) pair well with a rooftop day.

## Reichstag dome: the free one you have to plan

![The mirrored central cone of the Reichstag glass dome against the sky](blog-drafts/images/berlin-observation-decks/optimized/05-berlin-reichstag-dome.jpg)
_The Reichstag glass dome. Free, architecturally special, and the one viewpoint you must register for in advance._

The Reichstag dome is the only free view on this list, and it is genuinely special: a glass dome by Norman Foster over the working parliament, with a spiral ramp you walk up and a roof terrace looking across the government quarter and the Tiergarten. The catch is not money, it is planning. **You must register online in advance with your passport or ID, and in summer the free slots go days ahead.**

Do not treat it as a spontaneous walk-up, because you will be turned away at the entrance without a booking. Reserve a slot on the [official Bundestag registration page](https://www.bundestag.de/en/visittheBundestag/dome/registration-245686), bring the same ID you registered with, and note that the dome closes for occasional maintenance weeks. It is more architecture than skyline, but for free, with that building under you, it is worth the small effort. My full walkthrough is in [how to visit the Reichstag dome for free](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/how-to-visit-the-reichstag-dome-for-free-and-why-you-should-book-now).

## Victory Column: 285 steps for about four euros

![The golden Victory Column standing in the green Tiergarten under a blue sky](blog-drafts/images/berlin-observation-decks/optimized/06-berlin-victory-column-siegessaeule.jpg)
_The Siegessäule in the Tiergarten. About four euros, 285 steps, and the straight sightline down to the Brandenburg Gate._

The Victory Column, the Siegessäule, sits in the middle of the Tiergarten, and its viewing platform is a narrow open balcony at 51 metres under the golden statue. **There is no lift, just 285 steps, and entry is around 4 euros in cash, so bring coins.**

The reward is a different kind of view: a sea of green, the straight sightline down Straße des 17. Juni to the Brandenburg Gate, and Bellevue Palace off to one side. It is the most physical option and the least about skyscrapers, which is the point. If you are already walking the park, more on that in my [Victory Column guide](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/victory-column-berlin). Worth noting: the Berliner Dom also offers a dome walk with a decent view, covered in my [Berliner Dom tickets guide](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berliner-dom-tickets), if you would rather climb a cathedral than a column.

## So which Berlin view should you actually pay for?

Here is how I would decide, quickly:

- **You want the highest, most iconic view and will plan ahead:** the TV Tower, booked online.
- **You want the best all-round view for the money:** the Panoramapunkt at 9 euros, open air, no fuss.
- **You want a cheap open roof and a great TV Tower photo:** the Park Inn Panorama Terrasse at 6 euros.
- **You want a free, architectural view and can register ahead:** the Reichstag dome.
- **You want a cheap climb and green Tiergarten views:** the Victory Column, 285 steps and coins.

For most first-time visitors on a normal budget, I send them to the Panoramapunkt, and I tell them to save the TV Tower for a return trip when they can book a clear evening. ## Where this fits with a walking tour

None of these viewpoints are on my [free walking tour](https://www.berlinwalk.com/book-berlin-walking-tour/berlin-free-walking-tour-tip-based), which stays at street level in the medieval core around Alexanderplatz, Museum Island and Hackescher Markt for about 2 hours. That is on purpose: the ground is where the history is. But once you have walked the centre and understand what you are looking at, going up makes far more sense. The TV Tower and the Park Inn roof are both a two-minute walk from where the tour begins, so a viewpoint is an easy thing to add before or after.
