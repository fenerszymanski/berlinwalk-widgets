If you stand on the Kottbusser bridge in Berlin on a Tuesday or Friday, you look down at one of the best afternoons the city gives away for free. Along the Maybachufer, on the Neukölln bank of the Landwehrkanal, a long line of stalls sells oranges, olives, warm gözleme and bolts of fabric while the crowd moves slowly between them. This is the Turkish Market, and half the visitors I meet either miss it completely or turn up on the wrong day and find an empty street.

It is worth getting right, because the Turkish Market Berlin locals actually use is a real neighbourhood market, not a tourist set piece. Here is how to visit it well: which day, what time, what to buy, and how to fold it into a Berlin afternoon without wasting the good hours.

## What the Turkish Market on Maybachufer actually is

The official name is the Wochenmarkt am Maybachufer, but almost everyone calls it the Türkischer Markt, or just the Turkish Market. It grew out of Berlin's large Turkish community and now runs to around 180 traders on a full day, selling everything from Anatolian cheese to house plants.

One thing confuses a lot of first-timers: **this is a Neukölln market, even though people call it "Kreuzberg's Turkish market."** The stalls sit on the Maybachufer, which is the Neukölln side of the canal. The quiet, leafy residential bank across the water is Kreuzberg. The two districts face each other across the Landwehrkanal here, and the market sits right on that seam. It is one of my favourite bits of Berlin geography to point out, because it explains why the area feels like two neighbourhoods at once.

## When to go: the days and times that matter

This is the part people get wrong, so keep it simple. **The food market runs on Tuesday and Friday, 11:00 to 18:30.** Those are the only two days the fruit, cheese, spice and street-food stalls are out. Turn up on a Monday or a Wednesday and you will find a normal, quiet canal-side road.

Within those two days, the market changes character hour by hour, and matching your visit to what you actually want makes a real difference:

- **Late morning (about 11:30 to 13:00)** is the calm window. Produce is fresh, you can reach the stalls, and traders have time to answer a question. This is the slot I recommend for a first visit.
- **Friday afternoon** is the loud, full, atmospheric version. Great for the experience, slower for careful shopping, and easy to lose the person you came with.
- **The last half hour before 18:30** is when leftover produce gets discounted to clear the tables. Bring a bag and small notes and you can walk away with cheap figs or tomatoes, if any are left.

## What to buy and eat

The Turkish Market is at its best when you treat it as a slow graze rather than a shopping list. On the produce side you will find mounds of tomatoes, aubergines, herbs and citrus, usually cheaper and fresher than the nearby supermarkets, plus olives by the scoop, dried fruit and nuts, big wheels of Turkish white cheese, and fresh flatbread.

![A produce stall at the Maybachufer market piled with oranges and tomatoes](blog-drafts/images/turkish-market-berlin-maybachufer/optimized/02-produce-stall-oranges.jpg)

Produce is the heart of the market, and often better value than the supermarket around the corner.

The street food is the real reason to come hungry. Look for **gözleme** (thin stuffed flatbread cooked on a hot dome), **börek**, falafel, fresh pomegranate or orange juice, and in summer, stalls selling hot boiled corn on the cob. None of it is expensive, and eating as you wander is completely normal here.

![A colourful stall selling hot boiled corn on the cob with a bright yellow sign at the Berlin market](blog-drafts/images/turkish-market-berlin-maybachufer/optimized/03-boiled-corn-stall.jpg)

Boiled corn, or "Süt Mısır," is a classic warm snack to eat while you walk the stalls.

## Cash, bags and simple market manners

A few habits make the whole visit smoother. **Bring cash, and bring it in small notes and coins.** Most produce and street-food stalls are cash-only, and hunting for an ATM halfway through the market wastes the good light. If you are keeping an eye on your daily spend, the market is a genuinely cheap Berlin afternoon; my [realistic Berlin daily budget](/post/is-berlin-expensive-a-realistic-daily-budget-for-2026-tourists) puts it in context.

Keep bags in front of you in the Friday crush, do not stop dead in the narrow gaps between stalls, and if you want to ask a price, a simple "Wie viel?" and a smile goes a long way. Traders are quick and friendly, but they are working, so decide roughly what you want before you reach the front.

![A mint-green three-wheeled falafel truck and food stall at the Maybachufer market](blog-drafts/images/turkish-market-berlin-maybachufer/optimized/04-falafel-ape-truck.jpg)

Small food trucks and stalls line the market, so come hungry and graze as you go.

## Saturday is a different market

Here is the trap that catches sewers and souvenir hunters: **on Saturday the same riverside hosts a separate fabric market, the Stoffmarkt, 11:00 to 17:00, not the food market.** If you turn up on a Saturday expecting olives and gözleme, you will instead find bales of fabric, buttons, ribbons and haberdashery. It is a lovely market in its own right if you sew or want colourful photos, but it is not the Tuesday and Friday experience.

![A stall hung with rolls of brightly coloured fabric at the Saturday market on Maybachufer](blog-drafts/images/turkish-market-berlin-maybachufer/optimized/05-fabric-textile-stall.jpg)

The Saturday Stoffmarkt swaps food for fabric: great for makers, wrong day for a snack run.

## How to fit it into a Berlin day

My advice: do not build a whole day around the market. Give it a couple of unhurried hours and let it lead into the neighbourhood. From the Maybachufer you can walk the Landwehrkanal, drift up toward Hermannplatz or the cafés around Weserstraße, and see the Neukölln that locals actually spend their weekends in. Both are Berlin; they just show you very different sides of it, and two hours at each is a good rhythm.
