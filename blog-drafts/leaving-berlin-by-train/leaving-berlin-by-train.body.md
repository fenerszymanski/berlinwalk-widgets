Most visitors leave Berlin at least once. A day in Dresden, a night in Hamburg, an afternoon in Potsdam, a longer hop to Prague or Poland. For years the answer was simple: walk into Hauptbahnhof, look up at the glass hall, get on the train crossing above your head.

That answer is wrong for most of 2026, and it is wrong in a way that makes people miss trains.

![The glass barrel-vault roof over the upper cross-city platforms at Berlin Hauptbahnhof, with tracks curving away and travellers on the platform](blog-drafts/leaving-berlin-by-train/images/optimized/cover-hauptbahnhof.jpg)

The upper level of Hauptbahnhof, where trains cross the city east to west. These are the platforms that closed to regional and long-distance trains in June 2026.

Between **14 June and 12 December 2026** the Stadtbahn, the elevated line that carries trains straight through the middle of Berlin between Charlottenburg and Ostbahnhof, is closed to regional and long-distance trains. Not reduced. Closed. Zoologischer Garten and Ostbahnhof have no long-distance stop at all for those six months, and most trains that used to run across the city now dive into the tunnel under Hauptbahnhof instead, or start from stations you have probably never had a reason to visit.

I have watched people run the wrong way inside Hauptbahnhof this summer, up towards the bright glass hall, when their train was leaving four levels below them. So before anything about prices: work out which station, and which level.

## Leaving Berlin by train in 2026: which station you actually need

![A train crossing the brick-arch Stadtbahn viaduct beside the River Spree, with the Berlin TV Tower in the background](blog-drafts/leaving-berlin-by-train/images/optimized/stadtbahn-viaduct.jpg)

The Stadtbahn viaduct along the Spree. The S-Bahn still runs these tracks all year; regional and long-distance trains do not.

The closure pushed traffic onto two things that still work: the north-south tunnel under the city, and the outer ring.

**Berlin Hauptbahnhof, lower level.** This is where most long-distance trains stop during the closure. Hauptbahnhof is really two stations stacked on each other. The upper platforms run east to west along the Stadtbahn, and those are the closed ones. The lower platforms, the ones Germans call *tief*, run north to south in a tunnel, and that tunnel is doing nearly all the work this year. It is four levels and a long escalator run down from the street, so give yourself more time than the station's reputation suggests.

![The deep lower level of Berlin Hauptbahnhof, showing numbered platforms 3 to 6, an ICE waiting at one platform and a red regional train blurring through the middle](blog-drafts/leaving-berlin-by-train/images/optimized/hauptbahnhof-tief.jpg)

The lower level, platforms 1 to 8. If your ticket says Berlin Hauptbahnhof this year, this is almost certainly where you need to be.

**Berlin-Charlottenburg.** A quiet suburban station handed a much bigger job. Some ICE trains to North Rhine-Westphalia and Frankfurt am Main start or finish here from 14 June, and the regional RE1 and RE7 lines start and end here instead of running across the city. If you are staying anywhere around Kurfürstendamm, this is usually your fastest way out of Berlin right now.

![The brick corner of Berlin-Charlottenburg station with the green Berlin-Charlottenburg lettering and the green S-Bahn roundel, framed by red autumn leaves](blog-drafts/leaving-berlin-by-train/images/optimized/charlottenburg.jpg)

Berlin-Charlottenburg, two S-Bahn stops west of Zoologischer Garten. Small, easy to underestimate, and in 2026 the western way out of the city.

**Berlin Gesundbrunnen.** The northern gateway, sitting on the ring and the tunnel at the same time. Eurocity trains between Berlin and Poland run via Gesundbrunnen and Lichtenberg from 25 August.

**Berlin Südkreuz.** The southern end of the same tunnel. Trains to Düsseldorf and Köln run from and to Südkreuz via the lower level of Hauptbahnhof.

**Ostkreuz and Lichtenberg.** The eastern diversions. Intercity trains on the Dresden to Berlin to Rostock line are routed via Ostkreuz.

Two traps are worth naming because both cost people trains. Hamburg services **cannot stop at Spandau** during the closure, so the usual western shortcut is gone. The other trap is assuming a familiar route still means a familiar Berlin station: use the station and platform printed on your ticket, even if last year's journey left from somewhere else.

![The glass frontage of Berlin Ostbahnhof with the station name in large lettering above the entrance doors](blog-drafts/leaving-berlin-by-train/images/optimized/ostbahnhof.jpg)

Ostbahnhof still looks like a main station and sits walking distance from the East Side Gallery. Until December it is an eastern terminus for regional trains, with no long-distance stop at all.

## The three fares, and which one is actually right for you

Deutsche Bahn sells long-distance travel at three price levels, and the gap between them is not really about money. It is about how much your plans might change.

**Super Sparpreis** normally starts at **17.99 euros** in second class. Selected short domestic journeys can start at 6.99 euros, and DB is also running a limited 2026 Last Minute offer at that price. It is the strictest fare: you are bound to the long-distance train printed on your ticket. Outside DB's three-hour immediate-cancellation window, ordinary exchange and cancellation are excluded. If you miss it for a reason on your side, you normally cannot use the ticket on a later long-distance train; DB disruption and passenger-rights rules are separate. The current conditions are on [DB's Super Sparpreis FAQ](https://www.bahn.de/faq/pk/angebot/sparpreis/super-sparpreis).

**Sparpreis** starts at **21.99 euros**. Same train binding, but you can change or cancel until the day before travel for a 10 euro fee.

**Flexpreis** costs the most and buys you the day. You can take any train on that route on your booked date, break the journey, and stop off on the way. Cancellation is free until the day before and 30 euros from the first day of validity. It also **includes the City-Ticket** for local transport in participating cities, which Super Sparpreis and Sparpreis make you buy separately.

![An ICE 4 high-speed train standing at a platform under the glass roof of Berlin Hauptbahnhof with passengers waiting nearby](blog-drafts/leaving-berlin-by-train/images/optimized/ice-4-hauptbahnhof.jpg)

An ICE at the upper Hauptbahnhof platforms. Through 2026 you will meet it downstairs instead, or at Charlottenburg, Südkreuz or Gesundbrunnen.

My advice: match the fare to how certain you are, not to how cheap you want to feel. If you are going to Dresden on a fixed morning and coming back on a fixed evening, Super Sparpreis is genuinely good value and you should book it early, because the cheapest seats sell first. If you are travelling on your last day in Berlin, with a museum you might overrun and luggage you might mishandle, buy Flexpreis and stop worrying about the clock. I have made the opposite choice before and spent the last hour of a good trip watching a departure board instead of the city.

## The Deutschlandticket trap that most English guides still get wrong

The Deutschlandticket costs **63 euros a month** since January 2026, and it is superb value for what it actually covers: every regional train in Germany, meaning RE, RB and IRE, plus every S-Bahn, U-Bahn, tram and bus. If you are in Berlin for a longer stay, one ticket covers all your local travel and a genuine amount of the surrounding countryside.

As a national rule, it does not cover ICE, IC or EC. Berlin and Brandenburg used to have a few local exceptions, which is why the next detail matters.

Here is the part that changed and that a lot of advice online has not caught up with. Until the timetable change on **14 December 2025**, VBB regional tickets and the Deutschlandticket were accepted on certain DB long-distance trains inside Berlin and Brandenburg. That was the old local trick: take an Intercity towards Elsterwerda or Cottbus, or an ICE towards Prenzlau, and ride it on your regional ticket because the region had a deal with DB. DB Fernverkehr ended that contract, and since December 2025 the acceptance is gone across the region.

So if a guide tells you to hop on the IC to Elsterwerda with your Deutschlandticket, that advice is a fare evasion fine waiting to happen. The regional replacement is real, though: the RE8 now runs Elsterwerda to Berlin Hauptbahnhof in roughly an hour and a half.

## When the slow train genuinely wins

I am not against the fast train. But if you already hold a Deutschlandticket, the regional network out of Berlin is the most underrated thing in German travel, and the maths is blunt: the regional train costs you nothing extra, and the ICE does not.

Regional lines reach Brandenburg's lakes, Lutherstadt Wittenberg, Frankfurt an der Oder, the Baltic coast and Rostock, and plenty of the day trips people pay for. The catch during the closure is that you may need to reach one end of the city first. No RE or RB train runs between Charlottenburg and Ostbahnhof until December, so most lines now start or finish at one edge and you use the S-Bahn to get there. That is 20 to 30 minutes of Berlin, not a lost day.

The honest version of the trade-off: the fast train buys you time, the regional train buys you money, and in 2026 both of them cost you a bit of extra walking. Decide which one you actually have less of.

A few practical things worth knowing before you book:

- **Book early for Super Sparpreis.** Cheap seats are a quota, not a discount, and they disappear first on Friday and Sunday trains.
- **Seat reservations are optional in Germany, not compulsory.** A single second-class reservation costs 5.50 euros and is not included in a second-class fare of any kind, including Flexpreis. Worth it on busy long routes, especially with luggage.
- **Check your departure station on the day.** Construction plans move, and the station on your ticket is the one that counts.
- **The S-Bahn keeps running the Stadtbahn.** Only regional and long-distance trains are affected, apart from a handful of construction weekends with replacement buses. Your Alexanderplatz to Friedrichstrasse ride is fine.
