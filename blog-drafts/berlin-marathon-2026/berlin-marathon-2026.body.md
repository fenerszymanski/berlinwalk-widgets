Most people who get caught out by the Berlin Marathon never intended to watch it. They booked a Sunday in Berlin, walked out of the hotel at ten, and found a metal barrier where the road used to be, a stream of runners going past it, and no obvious way to the other side.

My advice: do not treat marathon Sunday as a day that got ruined. Treat it as a day with a line drawn through it. Find out which side of that line you are on, learn the two or three places where you can get across, and the rest of the day works normally. I have watched visitors lose two hours to a problem that a single U-Bahn ride would have solved.

## Berlin Marathon 2026: the date, the start and what it actually does to the city

The Berlin Marathon 2026 runs on **Sunday 27 September 2026**. The runners start at **09:15**, and the start and finish are both on Straße des 17. Juni, on the stretch between the Brandenburg Gate and the Kleiner Stern. Wheelchair racers and handbikes go earlier, at around 08:50.

Those are the numbers the [organiser](https://www.bmw-berlin-marathon.com/en/) and [berlin.de](https://www.berlin.de/en/events/10337131-2842498-berlinmarathon.en.html) confirm. What neither of them puts on the front page is the part that matters if you are a visitor: the course is a 42 kilometre loop through the middle of the city, and for most of the day it is a wall.

The route touches Tiergarten, Mitte, Friedrichshain, Kreuzberg, Neukölln, Schöneberg, Steglitz, Wilmersdorf and Zehlendorf. That is not a corner of Berlin you can avoid. That is Berlin.

## The road closes long before anyone runs past it

This is the single most useful thing to understand. A street on the marathon route is not closed while runners are on it. It is closed from early morning until the last participant has gone through and the barriers have been cleared, and that can be seven or eight hours.

Working from the closure pattern Berlin's traffic centre published for the 2025 race, the day looks roughly like this. Unter den Linden between Pariser Platz and Friedrichstraße goes first, from about 06:00. Mitte and Charlottenburg-Wilmersdorf follow from around 07:45. Friedrichshain and Kreuzberg from 08:00. Tempelhof-Schöneberg from 08:15.

**Then nothing reopens for hours, and it does not reopen in the order you would expect.** The leaders finish a little after 11:15, but the leaders finishing changes nothing, because tens of thousands of people are still out on the course. Friedrichshain and Kreuzberg come back first, from about 14:00. The south follows around 16:30. Mitte and the west hold on until roughly 17:30. Unter den Linden is last, at about 19:00.

The exact 2026 windows are published by the organiser and by the [Berlin traffic information centre](https://viz.berlin.de/) in September. Use the times above to plan the shape of the day, then check the official closure map in the week before you travel.

## The U-Bahn is how you cross the course

Berlin has a very specific advantage on marathon day: the closures are entirely on the surface. The U-Bahn and S-Bahn run normally, and they run under the course. So does the Tiergarten tunnel.

That means the question is never "how do I get across this road". It is "which station is on my side, and which station is on the other side". Get on, go one or two stops, come up. You have crossed a closed marathon course without seeing a barrier.

There are also stewarded crossing points on the surface, and they are worth knowing because they save you a fare. In 2025 the marked Sunday crossings were at Kurfürstendamm and Tauentzienstraße just before Rankestraße, at Wittenbergplatz, at Potsdamer Platz by the U-Bahn station traffic light, at Leipziger Straße and Friedrichstraße, and at Französische Straße and Friedrichstraße. Expect something very similar in 2026.

My advice: **do not try to walk the long way round.** The course is 42 kilometres and it loops. Walking around it is not a plan. If you are not certain which layer of the network gets you under it, my [U-Bahn versus S-Bahn explainer](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/u-bahn-vs-s-bahn-berlin) and the wider [Berlin public transport guide](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-public-transport-explained-for-tourists-u-bahn-s-bahn-tram-bus) both make the map easier to read before you are standing at a barrier deciding.

One warning about buses and trams. They keep running, but on marathon Sunday they are diverted, and the diversions change through the day as the police release sections. A bus that appears in your app may not take the route the app is drawing. Trains are the reliable layer.

## Where to stand if you decide to watch

If you are in Berlin anyway, watching an hour of it is genuinely worth doing. This is a World Marathon Major and the course record, Eliud Kipchoge's 2:01:09 from 2022, was set on these streets.

The obvious spots are obvious for a reason, and they are crowded. The Reichstag sits at around km 6.5 and Friedrichstadtpalast at km 8, so both fill up early in the race when the field is still dense. The Brandenburg Gate finish at km 42 is the most photographed and the least pleasant, because everyone is there.

My advice: **go west and go late.** The Memorial Church is at about km 35 and Potsdamer Platz at km 38.5. By the time the main field reaches them the crowd has thinned, you can get to the front of the barrier, and you see people who have been running for four hours, which is a more interesting thing to watch than the leaders going past in a blur.

If the race is your reason for the trip, book early. Late September fills up, and the whole city prices accordingly. My guide to [what to book in advance in Berlin](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/what-to-book-in-advance-in-berlin) covers what genuinely needs reserving, and [Berlin in September](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-in-september-2026) covers what else is happening that month.

## Saturday is the part nobody warns you about

Almost everything written about this race is about Sunday. But the marathon weekend starts on **Saturday 26 September**, and Saturday is the day that quietly collides with an ordinary sightseeing plan.

There is a 5K along the last five kilometres of the marathon route, a mini marathon, a children's race, and the inline skating marathon, which uses the full course. In 2025 the skating race closed Straße des 17. Juni, Großer Stern, Ernst-Reuter-Platz, Otto-Suhr-Allee, Hofjägerallee, Reichpietschufer, Potsdamer Straße, Leipziger Straße, Jerusalemer Straße, Markgrafenstraße, Französische Straße, Glinkastraße and Unter den Linden through the middle of the afternoon.

Read that list again. That is Potsdamer Platz, Leipziger Straße and Unter den Linden, on a Saturday afternoon, in the exact area most visitors spend their first full day. Unter den Linden between Pariser Platz and Friedrichstraße was closed from 07:00 to 18:00.

**If you have one Saturday in Berlin and it is that Saturday, do the historic centre in the morning and leave the afternoon for something east of Alexanderplatz.** That is not a compromise. It is just better sequencing.

## The Tiergarten is a building site for about two weeks

This one surprises people who come in September for reasons that have nothing to do with running.

The start and finish area is not built in a morning. In 2025, Straße des 17. Juni between Yitzhak-Rabin-Straße and the Brandenburg Gate was closed from **13 September** and did not reopen until **24 September**, days after the race. Großer Stern and Yitzhak-Rabin-Straße joined from 17 September. The government quarter streets around Ebertstraße and Scheidemannstraße went from the Thursday evening before.

So if you visit Berlin in the second half of September, the [Victory Column](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/victory-column-berlin) and the central axis through the [Tiergarten](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/tiergarten-berlin) may be surrounded by fencing, grandstands and trucks. It is not damaged and it is not closed to pedestrians, but it does not photograph the way you pictured it.

## What I would actually do that weekend

If you are in Berlin on 27 September 2026 and you are not running, here is the plan I would give a friend.

Get up early and use the quiet hour. Before 08:00 the city is still normal, and the Brandenburg Gate area on a closed street with no traffic is a genuinely good moment. Then pick one side of the course and stay on it until the afternoon.

Museum Island, Hackescher Markt and the area east of it stay workable, and the museums are indoors and unaffected. Kreuzberg and Friedrichshain come back first, so an afternoon there is safe. Go and watch twenty minutes of the race somewhere in the west around midday, because it is genuinely something to see. Then let the city hand itself back to you.

If you are planning a walk of your own through that area on marathon weekend, check the closure map first and keep the route on one side of the line. ## The short version

The Berlin Marathon 2026 is on Sunday 27 September, it starts at 09:15 on Straße des 17. Juni, and it puts a closed 42 kilometre loop through the middle of Berlin for most of the day.

You are not going to beat it, and you do not need to. Trains run under it, the east reopens first, and the west is the better place to watch. Check the official closure map the week before you go, decide which side of the line your Sunday lives on, and the marathon becomes a thing you saw rather than a thing that happened to you.
