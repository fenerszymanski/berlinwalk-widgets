## The short answer: yes, you can buy Berlin transport tickets on your phone

There are no ticket gates in Berlin and no turnstiles. You buy a ticket, you get on, and an inspector may or may not check it. That means a ticket living in an app works exactly like a paper one, and it is usually faster to buy.

Everything sells digitally: a single ride, a day ticket, the group day ticket, the airport ABC ticket, even the nationwide Deutschlandticket. You pay with a card, PayPal, Apple Pay or Google Pay, so you do not need a German bank account and you do not need to find cash. For most visitors, **the phone is now the easiest way to buy Berlin transport tickets**, full stop.

The one thing to get right is *which* ticket, because the app will happily sell you the wrong zone. That is what the rest of this guide is for.

## Which app should you download?

You only need one, but it helps to know what each is for. All of them sell the same VBB-tariff tickets at the same official prices, so this is about which interface suits your trip.

**BVG Fahrinfo** is the one-app pick for most tourists. It plans your route *and* sells the ticket at the end of it, so you search "where am I, where am I going," and it offers you the correct ticket for that journey. Start here.

**BVG Tickets** is the buy-only app. No route planning, just a clean list of tickets. It is the fastest way to grab a single or a day ticket when you already know what you want.

**Jelbi** is BVG's all-in-one mobility app. Alongside normal tickets it books e-scooters, shared bikes, mopeds and car-sharing on the same account. Get it if you want more than trains and buses.

**VBB Bus & Bahn** and **DB Navigator** are the ones to reach for when you leave the city proper, because they handle the regional trains to Potsdam and out to BER cleanly. DB Navigator is also the app you already have if you booked a train into Berlin.

## Which ticket to tap in the app

Fares changed on 1 January 2026, so here are the current numbers and when each one makes sense. The full official list lives on the [Berlin.de fares page](https://www.berlin.de/en/public-transportation/1772016-2913840-tickets-fares-and-route-maps.en.html), and my [Berlin public transport guide](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-public-transport-explained-for-tourists-u-bahn-s-bahn-tram-bus) explains zones in more depth.

- **Single ticket AB, €4.00.** One journey across the city, changes included, valid up to 2 hours in one direction. The default for a quick hop.
- **Short trip (Kurzstrecke), €2.80.** Three rail stops or fewer with no change, or six bus and tram stops. Cheap, but easy to overshoot.
- **24-hour ticket AB, €11.20.** Unlimited rides for 24 hours from the start time. It pays off from roughly the third ride, so on a real sightseeing day it usually wins.
- **24-hour group ticket AB, €35.30.** Up to five people on one ticket for 24 hours. For a family or a small group this is the quiet money-saver.
- **Deutschlandticket, €63 per month.** A monthly subscription that covers all local and regional transport nationwide. Worth it only if you are riding daily for about a week or more, and note it does not include zone C.

The trap is zones. **Zone AB covers the whole city, which is all most visitors ever need. You only add zone C for BER Airport or Potsdam.** If your route touches either, buy the **ABC** version: single €5.00, or a 24-hour ABC ticket for €12.90.

## Do you need to validate a phone ticket?

This is the question that makes people hover nervously by the yellow and red boxes on the platform. The answer is clean: **no, a ticket you buy in the app does not need stamping.**

Those boxes are validators for old-style paper tickets, the kind that come blank and need a timestamp before your first ride. A mobile ticket is already validated. When you buy a single in the app it is live from the moment of purchase, or from a start time you pick, so you simply buy it and board. My separate guide on [validating tickets on Berlin trains](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/do-you-really-need-to-validate-your-ticket-on-berlin-trains) covers the paper case in full.

Two things do matter, though:

- **A screenshot is not a valid ticket.** Inspectors check the live ticket in the app, and some carry a scanner for the barcode. Keep the real ticket open, not a photo of it.
- **Buy before you board.** Buy on the platform, not once the doors close and your signal drops in the tunnel. Riding while you are still tapping "purchase" counts as riding without a ticket, and the fine is €60.

![A U-Bahn platform at Kaiserin-Augusta-Strasse in Berlin, with the station sign on the wall and a few passengers waiting](blog-drafts/images/buy-berlin-transport-tickets-on-your-phone/optimized/03-u-bahn-platform.jpg)

The right moment to buy is here, standing on the platform with signal, before the train arrives.

## Paying without a German bank account

You do not need to be a local to pay. The BVG apps take international credit and debit cards, PayPal, Apple Pay and Google Pay, and for single and day tickets you can usually buy as a guest without setting up a full account. The [official BVG app overview](https://www.bvg.de/en/subscriptions-and-tickets/all-apps) lists what each app accepts.

The one exception is the Deutschlandticket, which is a registered subscription rather than a one-off purchase, so it does ask you to make an account and set up recurring payment. If you go that route, set a reminder to cancel by the 10th of the month so it does not quietly renew after you fly home.

A practical note on connectivity: buy your ticket while you still have wifi or a signal, because once you are deep in a U-Bahn tunnel you may not be able to complete a purchase. If you are sorting out a local SIM or eSIM, my [Berlin eSIM and wifi guide](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/esim-sim-wifi-berlin-2026) covers the easy options.

## The mistakes I see tourists make

Most phone-ticket trouble in Berlin comes down to four small things, and all four are avoidable.

- **Buying ABC when you only need AB.** The airport app screen sometimes defaults to a route that adds zone C. If you are staying in the city, that is a euro wasted on every ride. Check the zone before you pay.
- **Forgetting BER is zone C.** The opposite mistake. Coming from the airport, you *do* need ABC, so a plain AB single will not cover you and an inspector on the airport line will treat it as no ticket.
- **Screenshotting the ticket.** It feels safe. It is not valid. Keep the live ticket open.
- **Buying on the train.** Once the doors shut and the signal drops, you cannot always finish the purchase, and by then you are technically fare-dodging. Buy on the platform.

The S-Bahn out to BER runs through hubs like Ostkreuz. For anything airport or Potsdam bound, remember to switch your app to zone ABC.
