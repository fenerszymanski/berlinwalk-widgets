## Do you need a visa to visit Berlin? The short answer

Berlin is in Germany, and Germany is part of the Schengen Area, so there is no such thing as a separate "Berlin visa." Whatever rule applies to Germany applies to your Berlin trip.

For a short holiday, start with three groups:

- If you are an **EU, EEA or Swiss citizen**, you do not need a visa or ETIAS, and the Schengen 90/180 cap does not apply. For a short visit, carry a valid passport or national ID card. Stays longer than three months can involve residence conditions and registration.
- If you hold a passport on Germany's **visa-exempt list**, including the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Japan and South Korea, you do **not** need a visa for a tourist trip. You can normally enter for up to **90 days in any 180-day period**.
- If your passport is **not** on that list, you normally need a **Schengen short-stay visa** before travel. A residence permit, long-stay visa, EU-family status or special travel document can change the answer, so check your exact document rather than relying on a country name alone.

That is the starting point for the vast majority of tourists. The rest of this guide is about the details that trip people up: how the 90-day limit is counted, and the two 2026 border changes, EES and ETIAS, that everyone is asking about.

## The one rule that covers most visitors: 90 days in any 180

If you are visa-exempt, you are allowed to be in the Schengen Area for a maximum of **90 days within any rolling 180-day period**. For a normal Berlin holiday of a few days or a couple of weeks, you will not come close to this limit, so you can stop reading this section with an easy mind.

It matters if you are travelling around Europe for a long stretch, or making several trips close together. Two things catch people out:

- The 90 days are **cumulative across the whole Schengen Area**, not per country. A week in Berlin, a week in Paris, and a week in Rome all count toward the same 90.
- The 180-day window is **rolling**, not a fixed calendar block. On any given day, the authorities look back over the previous 180 days and add up how many you have already spent inside Schengen. Leaving for a weekend does not reset the count.

If you are anywhere near the limit, count carefully before you book, because overstaying is taken seriously and can mean fines or an entry ban. If a short-stay visa sticker authorises fewer than 90 days, the sticker controls and you should not use a 90-day result to extend it.

## Who is visa-free, and who needs a Schengen visa

The visa-exempt list is long, and it covers many of the countries whose travellers I meet on the tour. US, UK, Canadian, Australian, New Zealand, Japanese and South Korean passports are among the familiar examples, but document conditions and individual status can matter. Use the German Federal Foreign Office's [current visa table](https://www.auswaertiges-amt.de/en/visa-service/231148-231148) for the actual check.

If your country is not on the list, the route is a **Schengen short-stay (type C) visa**. You apply through the German mission responsible for your region, usually via an appointment and a set of documents: passport, travel insurance, proof of accommodation, and evidence you can support the trip. Apply well ahead, because appointment slots and processing times vary a lot by country and season.

One detail worth stating plainly: a Schengen visa issued by any member state lets you visit Germany, but if Germany is your main destination you should normally apply through the German authorities. When in doubt, use the [German Federal Foreign Office](https://www.auswaertiges-amt.de/en/visa-service/231148-231148) and the [EU's own travel portal](https://travel-europe.europa.eu/index_en), not a lookalike agency site charging a fee.

## What changed in 2026: EES is now live at the border

This is the part that is genuinely new, and where most of the current confusion comes from. Since **10 April 2026**, the EU's **Entry/Exit System (EES)** is fully operational at Schengen borders, and Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER) has been registering arrivals since late 2025.

EES is a biometric border system for most non-EU nationals making a short stay. It does not cover every non-EU traveller: long-stay visa and residence-permit holders are among the exemptions. The [official EES FAQ](https://travel-europe.europa.eu/ees/faq) explains the full scope. Here is what it means in practice:

- If you are travelling **without a short-stay visa**, the system records your **facial image and four fingerprints** with your passport details. Children under 12 are not fingerprinted.
- If you hold a **short-stay visa**, EES stores your facial image. Your fingerprints were already collected for the Visa Information System and are not stored again in EES.
- On later trips, the checks are quicker because your record already exists.
- There is **nothing to apply for and nothing to pay** for EES. It happens at the border, automatically. You do not prepare anything in advance.

The one real effect on your day is **time**. [BER warns that longer waits can occur](https://ber.berlin-airport.de/en/flying/controls/id-and-passport-control/ees.html) while travellers complete EES registration. My honest advice: do not book anything tight for your first afternoon, and if you have a connection or a timed ticket, give yourself a generous buffer. A calm first hour is worth more than a rushed one.

## ETIAS: the new authorization that is not required yet

ETIAS is the other name in the headlines, and the single most useful thing to know is this: **as of mid-2026, ETIAS is not yet in force.** You cannot apply for it right now because the system has not launched, and you do **not** need it for a Berlin trip today.

ETIAS is expected to start in the **last quarter of 2026**. The launch will be followed first by a transitional period of at least six months, when travellers should apply but will not be refused solely because they do not have ETIAS if they meet the other entry conditions. An additional grace period of at least six months follows, when ETIAS is generally required but a limited first-entry exception applies. The [official ETIAS timeline](https://travel-europe.europa.eu/etias/ltr/about-etias/news-corner/revised-timeline-ees-and-etias.html) will carry the final dates.

When applications open, here is roughly what it will look like for visa-exempt travellers:

- ETIAS is a **travel authorisation**, not a visa. You apply **online** before you travel. Most applications are expected to be processed within minutes, but some will take longer.
- The fee is **20 euros**. It is **valid for three years** or until your passport expires, whichever comes first, and covers multiple trips.
- Travellers **under 18 or over 70** are exempt from the fee but still need to apply.

So if you are travelling in 2026, plan for EES at the border and simply keep an eye on the ETIAS start date for future trips. Ignore any website telling you to "apply for ETIAS now" and charging you for it, because the real system is not open yet.

## A simple pre-trip checklist

If you want the whole thing in one breath, here is the order I would go through it:

- **Check your passport.** It should be valid for at least three months beyond your departure date and issued within the last ten years. This is the most common avoidable problem, so do it first.
- **Confirm your exact visa status.** Check the official German table, including any residence permit, long-stay visa or family-status exception. If a short-stay visa is required, book the appointment early.
- **Count your days** if you are on a longer European trip. Add every previous Schengen entry and exit, because the 180-day window moves each day.
- **Expect EES at the border** if you are a non-EU national making a covered short stay. Nothing to apply for, but allow extra time on arrival.
- **Check the official ETIAS date** for future trips. It is not required today, and applications are not open.
