## Do you need a visa to visit Berlin? The short answer

Berlin is in Germany, and Germany is part of the Schengen Area, so there is no such thing as a separate "Berlin visa." Whatever rule applies to Germany applies to your Berlin trip.

For a short holiday, the answer splits into two groups:

- If you hold a passport from a **visa-exempt country** (the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Japan, South Korea, and around 55 others), you do **not** need a visa for a tourist trip. You can enter and stay up to **90 days** without one.
- If your passport is **not** on that visa-exempt list, you need to apply for a **Schengen visa** at a German embassy or consulate before you travel.

That is the whole decision for the vast majority of tourists. The rest of this guide is about the details that trip people up: how the 90-day limit is counted, and the two 2026 border changes, EES and ETIAS, that everyone is asking about.

## The one rule that covers most visitors: 90 days in any 180

If you are visa-exempt, you are allowed to be in the Schengen Area for a maximum of **90 days within any rolling 180-day period**. For a normal Berlin holiday of a few days or a couple of weeks, you will not come close to this limit, so you can stop reading this section with an easy mind.

It matters if you are travelling around Europe for a long stretch, or making several trips close together. Two things catch people out:

- The 90 days are **cumulative across the whole Schengen Area**, not per country. A week in Berlin, a week in Paris, and a week in Rome all count toward the same 90.
- The 180-day window is **rolling**, not a fixed calendar block. On any given day, the authorities look back over the previous 180 days and add up how many you have already spent inside Schengen. Leaving for a weekend does not reset the count.

If you are anywhere near the limit, count carefully before you book, because overstaying is taken seriously and can mean fines or an entry ban. I built the tool further down this page to do exactly that counting for you.

## Who is visa-free, and who needs a Schengen visa

The visa-exempt list is long, and it covers most of the countries whose travellers I meet on the tour. If you hold a passport from the US, UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, most of Latin America, Japan, South Korea, Singapore, Israel, or a handful of others, you are visa-free for tourism.

If your country is not on the list, the route is a **Schengen short-stay (type C) visa**. You apply through the German mission responsible for your region, usually via an appointment and a set of documents: passport, travel insurance, proof of accommodation, and evidence you can support the trip. Apply well ahead, because appointment slots and processing times vary a lot by country and season.

One detail worth stating plainly: a Schengen visa issued by any member state lets you visit Germany, but if Germany is your main destination you should apply through the German authorities. When in doubt, the official source is the German Federal Foreign Office and the [EU's own travel portal](https://travel-europe.europa.eu/index_en), not a lookalike agency site charging a fee.

## What changed in 2026: EES is now live at the border

This is the part that is genuinely new, and where most of the current confusion comes from. Since **10 April 2026**, the EU's **Entry/Exit System (EES)** is fully operational at Schengen borders, and Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER) has been registering arrivals since late 2025.

EES is a biometric border system for non-EU travellers. Here is what it means in practice on your first entry:

- Instead of an officer stamping your passport, you register at a **self-service kiosk or a booth**, where the system records your **facial image and fingerprints** along with your passport details.
- On later trips, the checks are quicker because your record already exists.
- There is **nothing to apply for and nothing to pay** for EES. It happens at the border, automatically. You do not prepare anything in advance.

![A row of Entry/Exit System self-service registration kiosks in an airport arrivals area](blog-drafts/do-you-need-a-visa-to-visit-berlin/images/optimized/03-ees-kiosks.jpg)
_EES kiosks like these register a first-time traveller's face, fingerprints and passport data instead of a paper stamp._

The one real effect on your day is **time**. Because the first registration takes longer than an old-fashioned stamp, arrivals halls across Europe have seen slower passport queues through 2026, especially at peak summer hours. My honest advice: on arrival at BER, do not book anything tight for your first afternoon, and if you have a connection or a timed ticket, give yourself a generous buffer. I have watched people sprint out of that arrivals hall stressed for no reason. A calm first hour is worth more than a rushed one.

## ETIAS: the new authorization that is not required yet

ETIAS is the other name in the headlines, and the single most useful thing to know is this: **as of mid-2026, ETIAS is not yet in force.** You cannot apply for it right now because the system has not launched, and you do **not** need it for a Berlin trip today.

When it does start, expected in the **last quarter of 2026** with a grace period before it becomes mandatory, here is roughly what it will look like for visa-exempt travellers:

- ETIAS is a **travel authorization**, not a visa. You apply **online** before you travel, and most approvals are expected within hours.
- The fee is **20 euros**. It is **valid for three years** or until your passport expires, whichever comes first, and covers multiple trips.
- Travellers **under 18 and over 70** are exempt from the fee but still need to apply.

So if you are travelling in 2026, plan for EES at the border and simply keep an eye on the ETIAS start date for future trips. Ignore any website telling you to "apply for ETIAS now" and charging you for it, because the real system is not open yet.

## A simple pre-trip checklist

If you want the whole thing in one breath, here is the order I would go through it:

- **Check your passport.** It should be valid for at least three months beyond your departure date and issued within the last ten years. This is the most common avoidable problem, so do it first.
- **Confirm your visa status.** Visa-exempt passport? Nothing to arrange. Not on the list? Book a Schengen visa appointment early.
- **Count your days** if you are on a longer European trip, using the 90-in-180 rule.
- **Expect EES at the border.** Nothing to prepare, but allow extra time on arrival.
- **Note the ETIAS date** for future trips. Not needed yet in 2026.
