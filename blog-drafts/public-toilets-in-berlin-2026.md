## How Berlin's Public Toilet System Works

The city's public toilet network is called [Berliner Toiletten](https://www.berlin.de/sen/uvk/mobilitaet-und-verkehr/infrastruktur/oeffentliche-toiletten/), operated by Wall GmbH for the State of Berlin. As of February 2026, there are 486 toilet facilities listed as publicly accessible across the city, including 278 automatic Berliner Toiletten cabins that you see on sidewalks, plus staffed centers and a few historic green octagonal kiosks.

A standard Berliner Toilette is fully automatic, accessible, and self-cleaning. You pay 50 cents, the door unlocks, you go in, the door closes behind you and the cabin cleans itself after you leave.

You can pay by coin, contactless bank card, Apple Pay, Google Pay, or directly inside the Berliner Toilette app.

If you are visually impaired, mobility impaired, or otherwise have a disability that requires a EuroKey (also called the European disability key), you can open Berliner Toiletten for free.

Tourists do not usually carry a EuroKey. If that applies to you, the practical answer is the 50 cent coin or contactless card.

## The 107 Free Toilets

In 2026, Berlin announced that 107 of the 278 automatic Berliner Toiletten will become permanently free and gender-neutral, following a successful two-year pilot. The remaining 171 still cost 50 cents per use.

The free cabins are spread around the city, not concentrated in one district. You cannot count on the closest cabin being a free one. The simplest way to know in advance is the Berliner Toilette app.

For tourists this matters in two ways:

- If you only have small change, do not panic. There is a real chance the nearest cabin is one of the free ones.
- Do not assume "all Berlin toilets are free now." Most of them are still 50 cents.

## Train Station Toilets

Most major Berlin train stations have larger staffed toilet centers, separate from the street network. The most useful for tourists are:

- Berlin Hauptbahnhof
- Friedrichstrasse
- Alexanderplatz
- Zoologischer Garten
- Ostbahnhof
- Potsdamer Platz
- Suedkreuz

These are usually operated by Rail&Fresh and cost around 1 euro. You pay at a turnstile or to an attendant. They are bigger, generally cleaner than the average city center cabin, and the entrance area often gives you a discount voucher for shops inside the station.

If you have a long train journey ahead, paying for a station toilet at Hauptbahnhof is usually worth it. The cabin street network is faster and cheaper, but for families with children or anyone with a suitcase, the station toilets are easier.

## Department Stores, Malls, and Chain Cafes

Some of the easiest tourist toilet stops in central Berlin are not the official public ones at all.

- **Department stores**: KaDeWe in West Berlin, Galeria on Alexanderplatz, and Galeries Lafayette near Friedrichstrasse all have customer toilets. They are clean, usually free or very low cost, and on multiple floors.
- **Shopping malls**: Mall of Berlin near Potsdamer Platz, Bikini Berlin in the West, Alexa near Alexanderplatz. These have multiple toilets, often free, often with baby-changing rooms.
- **Chain cafes and restaurants**: Starbucks, Dean & David, Vapiano, and McDonald's locations near tourist sights usually have customer toilets. Some require a receipt code printed when you buy something.

This is not "secret tourist information." It is just how a lot of city visitors actually solve the problem in practice. The official network is reliable, but department stores and malls are often the most comfortable option if you are already nearby.

## Museum Toilets

If you are visiting any of the major museums, the toilets are inside. You do not pay separately, you pay through the museum entry. For an idea of which museums are worth that ticket and which are free, see my guide to [Berlin museums that are actually free in 2026](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/which-berlin-museums-are-free-2026).

The free historical sites I recommend, such as the Topography of Terror, the Berlin Wall Memorial at Bernauer Strasse, and Tranenpalast, all have toilets on site. For tourists building a no-ticket history day, these are useful anchor points for the body as well as for the history.

## Park and Outdoor Toilets

Berlin has spent years on a slow program of putting toilets back into parks. As of 2026, work continues on extending the pilot of free park toilets across more of the city's larger green spaces.

You can usually find at least one toilet in or near:

- Tiergarten
- Volkspark Friedrichshain
- Volkspark Hasenheide
- Treptower Park
- Tempelhofer Feld

These are not always premium experiences, especially in summer. They are functional, they exist, and they save you from walking back into the city in a hurry.

## The Berliner Toilette App

For tourists, the most useful single tool is the official [Berliner Toilette app](https://www.berliner-toilette.de/), available for iOS and Android.

It does three things well:

- It shows the nearest cabins on a live map.
- It tells you which are currently in service.
- It lets you pay through the app instead of with a coin.

If you are walking with kids, an older relative, or anyone who does not like waiting, install the app on the morning of your sightseeing day. It removes most of the stress.

You can also use general "toilet finder" apps and Google Maps, which often label public toilets near major sights. The official app is more accurate for the Berliner Toiletten network specifically.

## Accessibility

The Berliner Toiletten network is designed to be barrier-free. The automatic cabins are wheelchair accessible and include grab rails inside.

People with a EuroKey can open the cabins for free. If you are travelling with a wheelchair user, the official network is the most predictable option in central Berlin. Train station toilet centers also have accessible cubicles, although the route in from the platform can be longer.

If you need detailed accessibility information for a specific stop, check the official Berlin senate page on [public toilets](https://www.berlin.de/sen/uvk/mobilitaet-und-verkehr/infrastruktur/oeffentliche-toiletten/) before you travel.

## Toilets Along Common Tourist Routes

Most first time visitors walk a similar central route: Brandenburg Gate, Reichstag, Holocaust Memorial, Potsdamer Platz, Unter den Linden, Museum Island, Alexanderplatz. Some come from the West, around Zoo and KaDeWe. Many also pair their day with a free walking tour.

Practical toilet anchors along that route:

- **Hauptbahnhof**: full station toilet center, paid, very reliable.
- **Reichstag area**: limited curbside cabins. Plan ahead if you are queuing for the dome.
- **Brandenburg Gate**: street-side Berliner Toiletten cabins nearby, plus toilets inside the Mall of Berlin a short walk south.
- **Potsdamer Platz**: Mall of Berlin and the Sony Center area have multiple options.
- **Friedrichstrasse**: station toilet center, plus Galeries Lafayette nearby.
- **Museum Island and Lustgarten**: limited free options outside. Pay for a museum if you are going in, or walk to Hackescher Markt for cafes.
- **Alexanderplatz**: Galeria department store, the Alexa shopping center across the road, and station toilets.

## What to Carry

You do not need much. The practical kit is:

- One 50 cent coin and one 1 euro coin in an accessible pocket.
- A contactless card or phone that can pay 50 cents without drama.
- Tissues or wet wipes in your bag for anything that does not go to plan.
- The Berliner Toilette app installed before you start your day.

Hand sanitizer is not strictly necessary. The automatic cabins are self-cleaning, and museums and malls have sinks. Bring it if you prefer it.

## A Note on Etiquette

A few small things that surprise some visitors:

- It is normal to tip a toilet attendant 20 to 50 cents, even if the toilet itself charged nothing. There is usually a small plate on the counter.
- Cafe and restaurant toilets are for customers. If you really need one, the simplest answer is to buy a small drink first.
- Do not use the cabin doors as somewhere to wait for friends. They are timed and may lock you out.

None of this is dramatic. Berlin is a practical city about this kind of thing.

## My Honest Advice

Most of the worry around Berlin toilets comes from not knowing the system exists. Once you know there is a real network, that it costs about 50 cents, that 107 of the cabins are free, and that train stations and malls are always backup options, the whole question becomes small.

Carry a coin. Install the app. Use the museum and mall stops you already pass anyway.
