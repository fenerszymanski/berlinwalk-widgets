# Berlin Restaurant Phrases: How to Order, Pay and Handle Allergies

You do not need fluent German to eat well in Berlin. You need a few **Berlin restaurant phrases** that work under pressure: at a bakery counter, in a busy cafe, at a street-food window, or when the bill arrives and you suddenly forget every word you learned on the plane.

Berlin is used to English. Central restaurants, coffee shops, museum cafes and many bars will often switch languages before you finish your first sentence. VisitBerlin's [restaurant overview](https://www.visitberlin.de/en/restaurants-berlin) gives a good sense of how broad the city's food scene is, from German classics to international kitchens. But the small German lines below still matter. They make the interaction warmer, help when staff are busy, and are especially useful for allergy, payment and take-away moments where pointing is not enough.

## Berlin Restaurant Phrases You Actually Need

The mistake is trying to memorize a whole phrasebook. In real Berlin food situations, you need four building blocks:

- **A polite opening:** "Hallo" is enough.
- **A soft ordering phrase:** "Ich hätte gern..." means "I would like..."
- **One practical question:** card payment, take-away, ingredient, or recommendation.
- **One clean ending:** "Die Rechnung bitte" for the bill.

If you already read my guide to [German menu words](/post/how-to-read-a-german-menu-without-panicking), think of this as the spoken version. That article helps you decode the page. This one helps you say the useful sentence at the right moment.

## The One Ordering Formula That Works Almost Everywhere

The safest phrase is:

**Ich hätte gern...**

It means **"I would like..."** and it is softer than a direct "I want." Use it at bakeries, cafes, snack bars and casual restaurants.

Examples:

- **Ich hätte gern einen Kaffee.** - I would like a coffee.
- **Ich hätte gern ein Croissant.** - I would like a croissant.
- **Ich hätte gern die Currywurst mit Pommes.** - I would like the currywurst with fries.
- **Ich hätte gern das Tagesgericht.** - I would like the dish of the day.

If you forget the item name, point and say:

**Das da bitte.**

That means **"that one there, please."** It is not elegant, but it works beautifully at bakery counters.

![A cafe table set for coffee and food in Berlin](berlinwalk-widgets/blog-drafts/images/berlin-restaurant-phrases/assets/optimized/02-berlin-restaurant-phrases-cafe-table-1600.jpg)

At a seated table, start with the polite phrase. At a counter, keep it shorter and point if the queue is moving.

## Bakery And Cafe Phrases

Berlin bakeries can move fast. Decide before you reach the counter, then use one clear line.

**Hallo, ich hätte gern zwei Schrippen und ein Croissant, bitte.**

That means: "Hello, I would like two bread rolls and a croissant, please."

Useful bakery and cafe words:

- **ein Brötchen** or **eine Schrippe** - a bread roll
- **ein Croissant** - a croissant
- **ein Stück Kuchen** - a slice of cake
- **ein Kaffee** - a coffee
- **ein Cappuccino** - a cappuccino

For take-away, say:

**Zum Mitnehmen bitte.**

For eating there, say:

**Für hier bitte.**

This also helps in places where staff ask quickly: **"Für hier oder zum Mitnehmen?"** That means "for here or to take away?"

![Fresh bread and pastries in a Berlin bakery display](berlinwalk-widgets/blog-drafts/images/berlin-restaurant-phrases/assets/optimized/03-berlin-restaurant-phrases-bakery-bread-1600.jpg)

In a bakery, the best German is compact: greeting, item, number, and for here or take-away.

## Street Food And Casual Restaurant Phrases

Berlin street food is international, direct and fast. You may hear German, English, Turkish, Arabic, Vietnamese and several other languages in one short queue. Use German if it helps; switch to English if the person behind the counter does.

For a classic Berlin snack, you can say:

**Einmal Currywurst mit Pommes, bitte.**

That means: "One currywurst with fries, please."

For döner or other counter food:

- **Mit allem?** - With everything?
- **Ohne Zwiebeln, bitte.** - Without onions, please.
- **Mit scharfer Soße?** - With spicy sauce?
- **Einmal vegetarisch, bitte.** - One vegetarian, please.

For more eating ideas, my [Berlin food guide](/post/what-to-eat-in-berlin-12-must-try-local-foods) gives you the dishes first. Use the phrase pattern here once you know what you want.

At a street-food window, short phrases win. Decide the item first, then add one change such as "without onions."

## Allergy And Dietary Phrases: Say It Clearly

If a food issue is serious, do not rely on charm, pointing or hoping the English menu is complete. The [European Commission](https://food.ec.europa.eu/food-safety/labelling-and-nutrition/food-information-consumers-legislation_en) explains that EU food information rules include mandatory allergen information for non-prepacked foods, including restaurants and cafes. Still, the practical Berlin rule is simple: **show the written phrase and ask staff to check.**

Use this structure:

**Ich bin allergisch gegen...**

That means: "I am allergic to..."

Useful lines:

- **Ich bin allergisch gegen Nüsse.** - I am allergic to nuts.
- **Ich bin allergisch gegen Milch.** - I am allergic to milk.
- **Ich vertrage kein Gluten.** - I do not tolerate gluten.
- **Ist das ohne Schweinefleisch?** - Is this without pork?
- **Haben Sie etwas Veganes?** - Do you have something vegan?
- **Können Sie das bitte prüfen?** - Could you please check that?

If the answer feels uncertain, choose something else. A busy snack bar is not the right place for a risky guess.

## Asking For The Bill, Paying By Card, And Tipping

The bill phrase is short:

**Die Rechnung bitte.**

That means: "The bill, please."

To ask about card payment:

**Kann ich mit Karte zahlen?**

That means: "Can I pay by card?"

Berlin is much more card-friendly than it used to be, but small bakeries, kiosks, bars and snack counters can still surprise you. Carrying some cash remains useful, especially outside polished central restaurants.

For tipping, [Berlin.de](https://www.berlin.de/en/tourism/travel-information/1758234-2862820-tipping.en.html) notes that tipping in restaurants, cafes and bars is customary but not compulsory. In practice, you usually round up when paying. Instead of leaving coins on the table, say the final amount or say:

**Stimmt so.**

That means: "Keep the change."

Example: the bill is EUR 18.40, you hand over EUR 20 and say **"Stimmt so."** For a deeper guide, read my [tipping in Berlin](/post/tipping-in-berlin) article before you travel.

## What Not To Overthink

Do not worry about perfect grammar. Most staff care whether they understand your order, not whether your dative case is beautiful.

Do not begin every sentence with "Entschuldigung" unless you are interrupting someone. A simple **"Hallo"** is usually fine at the start of an interaction.

Do not translate "I want" directly as your default. **"Ich will..."** is grammatically possible, but it can sound blunt in a service situation. **"Ich hätte gern..."** is safer.

And do not panic if the answer comes back in English. That is normal Berlin, not a failure.

## A Simple Phrase Plan For Your Berlin Day

Before you leave the hotel, save these five lines:

1. **Ich hätte gern...** - I would like...
2. **Das da bitte.** - That one there, please.
3. **Zum Mitnehmen bitte.** - To take away, please.
4. **Kann ich mit Karte zahlen?** - Can I pay by card?
5. **Die Rechnung bitte.** - The bill, please.

That is enough for breakfast, coffee, a quick lunch, a snack and the bill.

- **Zum Mitnehmen bitte.** - To take away, please.

- **Haben Sie etwas Veganes?** - Do you have something vegan?

- **Können Sie das bitte prüfen?** - Could you please check that?

Use fewer phrases, but use them confidently. That is the real trick.
