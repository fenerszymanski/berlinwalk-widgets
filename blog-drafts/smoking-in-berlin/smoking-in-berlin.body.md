You sit down at a small Berlin bar, light a cigarette, and nobody blinks. Two minutes later you step onto a U-Bahn platform, light another out of habit, and you can be fined about 40 euro. Same street, same person, two very different rules. That gap is exactly what trips up visitors here.

Berlin is not a free-for-all, and it is not as strict as most people expect either. It keeps a few of its own rules that other German cities dropped years ago, and in 2024 the country changed its cannabis law on top of that. So a fair question deserves a clear answer, and that is what this guide is for.

## The basic rules for smoking in Berlin

Here is the shape of it before the detail. Germany bans smoking inside enclosed public places like restaurants and workplaces. Berlin softens that in one specific case, the small neighbourhood bar. Public transport is completely off limits. And the outdoors, meaning terraces, streets and parks, is where smokers have always been fine.

If you remember one thing about smoking in Berlin, remember this: when you are not sure, step outside. On a pavement or a terrace you are almost never breaking a rule. That single habit will keep you clear of every fine in this article.

## Smoking inside bars: the Berlin exception

This is the part that surprises people. In most of Germany every bar is smoke-free indoors. Berlin kept a loophole.

A bar can legally allow smoking inside if it is a single room under 75 square metres, serves no prepared hot food, and admits nobody under 18. These places are called a Raucherkneipe, a smoking pub, and they usually mark it with a sticker on the door. They tend to be the small, dim, lived-in corner bars rather than the busy gastropubs.

The moment a bar has a real kitchen or a second room, the exception falls away and it must be smoke-free inside. Owners who ignore the rule risk fines of around 1,000 euro, so most enforce it properly. If you want a smoke-free night, pick a bar that serves warm food. If you want to smoke indoors, look for that Raucherkneipe sign.

## Restaurants, hotels and other enclosed spaces

Restaurants and cafés are simple: no smoking and no vaping inside, full stop. When hot food is served there is no legal smoking section. Nearly every one has outdoor seating anyway, and that is where people smoke.

Hotels are almost all non-smoking now. A few keep a small number of designated smoking rooms, but you have to ask for one when you book. Light up in a normal non-smoking room and you will usually find a cleaning charge of 100 to 250 euro on your bill. Use the balcony or step outside, and ask reception where smoking is allowed if you are unsure.

## Public transport is completely smoke-free

This is the one that catches people, because the habit of a quick cigarette while waiting is so automatic.

Smoking and vaping are banned on every tram, bus, U-Bahn and S-Bahn, and on the platforms too. Underground platforms are fully smoke-free. Some open-air platforms have a small painted smoking area, and that marked box is the only spot where it is allowed. A cigarette on a normal platform can bring an on-the-spot penalty of around 40 euro. The [S-Bahn rules of conduct](https://sbahn.berlin/en/plan-a-journey/rail-stations/rules-of-conduct/) spell this out, e-cigarettes included.

My advice is boring but it works: finish your cigarette upstairs, at street level, before you head down. Rides across the centre are short, so you are rarely waiting long. If you want the wider picture on transport penalties, my guide to the [Berlin U-Bahn fine](/post/berlin-u-bahn-fine) covers how these checks actually happen.

## Where you can smoke freely: terraces, streets and parks

Now the good news, and honestly the answer most visitors are looking for. Outdoor café and bar terraces are the natural home of smokers in Berlin. Smoking and vaping are allowed at most open-air tables, and you will see it everywhere on a summer evening.

Streets, pavements and parks are fine too. There is no general outdoor smoking ban. The only thing I would ask, as someone who walks this city every day, is to use the ashtray or take your butts with you. Berlin has been tightening up on littering, and cigarette ends are the single most common thing on the ground.

## Vaping and e-cigarettes

Keep this simple: treat your vape exactly like a cigarette. The non-smoker protection rules and the transport ban cover e-cigarettes and heated tobacco the same way as tobacco. So if you cannot smoke somewhere, assume you cannot vape there either. There is no separate, more relaxed rule for vaping indoors or on the U-Bahn.

## Cannabis in Berlin: legal to carry, not to buy

Germany changed its cannabis law on 1 April 2024, and the questions have not stopped since. Here is the honest picture for a visitor.

Adults aged 18 and over may possess up to 25 grams of cannabis in public, and it is decriminalised for personal adult use. That is real. But there is a catch that surprises almost everyone: you cannot legally buy it here as a tourist. There are no cannabis shops or coffeeshops in Germany. The only legal supply is a non-profit cannabis social club, and those require German residence and a waiting period, so they are closed to short-term visitors. Buying from a street dealer stays illegal, and that is exactly where the fake and unsafe product is. If someone approaches you in a park offering to sell, treat it the way you would any other [Berlin tourist scam](/post/berlin-tourist-scams).

Where you can use it in public is limited, and this is where visitors get fined without realising. Cannabis use is banned within 100 metres of schools, playgrounds, daycare centres and sports grounds, and in pedestrian zones between 07:00 and 20:00. It is also not allowed around children or on public transport. Central Berlin is full of playgrounds, so in practice the safe answer is an ordinary park or side street in the evening, well clear of those zones. The official [Cannabis Act FAQ](https://www.bundesgesundheitsministerium.de/en/topics/cannabis/faq-cannabis-act) has the full detail if you want to read the source. And never combine it with driving or cycling; that is a serious offence here.

## A few habits from someone who walks the city daily

None of this is meant to make Berlin sound like a place of traps. It genuinely is relaxed by most standards. But the rules are enforced, so a little awareness saves you money and hassle.

Step outside when you are unsure. Read the door of a bar before you light up inside. Never smoke on a platform. Keep cannabis away from children, schools and daytime shopping streets. And carry your butts to a bin. That is the whole thing.

On my free walking tour through the old centre we pass plenty of these corner bars and terraces, and this exact kind of practical question comes up all the time. Berlin rewards visitors who understand how it actually works rather than how they assume it should, and the same is true for a night out.
