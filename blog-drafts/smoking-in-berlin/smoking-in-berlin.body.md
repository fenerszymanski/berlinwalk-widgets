You sit down at a small Berlin bar, light a cigarette, and nobody blinks. Two minutes later you step onto a U-Bahn platform, light another out of habit, and security can stop you, remove you or charge a contractual penalty. Same street, same person, two very different rules. That gap is exactly what trips up visitors here.

Berlin is not a free-for-all, and it is not as strict as most people expect either. It keeps a few of its own rules that other German cities dropped years ago, and in 2024 the country changed its cannabis law on top of that. So a fair question deserves a clear answer, and that is what this guide is for.

## The basic rules for smoking in Berlin

Here is the shape of it before the detail. Berlin bans tobacco smoking in enclosed public places such as restaurants and workplaces, with narrow exceptions for a separate smoking room or a qualifying small neighbourhood bar. Public transport is off limits outside designated smoking areas. Outdoors, terraces, streets and most parks are generally where smokers go, subject to the venue's own rules.

If you remember one thing about smoking in Berlin, remember this: when you are not sure, step outside, check the sign and respect the venue's rules. Outdoor places can still have site-specific restrictions, so an ashtray or a quick question to staff is a better guide than an assumption.

## Smoking inside bars: the Berlin exception

This is the part that surprises people. Smoking rules vary by German state, and Berlin kept a narrow exception for small drinks-led bars.

A bar can legally allow smoking inside if it is a single room under 75 square metres, has no separate side room, prepares no food on site, admits nobody under 18, displays the required sign and notifies the authority. These places are called a Raucherkneipe, a smoking pub. They tend to be the small, dim, lived-in corner bars rather than the busy gastropubs.

If a bar prepares food on site or has a separate side room, it cannot use the one-room Raucherkneipe exception. A venue with at least two suitable rooms may instead designate one fully enclosed, clearly marked secondary room for smoking. The main room stays smoke-free. Owners who ignore the law can be fined up to 1,000 euro, so look for the sign and ask before you light up.

## Restaurants, hotels and other enclosed spaces

The main areas of restaurants and cafés are smoke-free. A venue with at least two suitable rooms may have a fully enclosed, clearly marked secondary smoking room, but it cannot be the main dining room and you should never assume one exists. Vaping is not covered by every tobacco-smoking clause in exactly the same way, yet venues can ban it through their house rules and many do. Ask first or step outside.

Hotel rooms are mainly a matter of the property's booking terms and house rules. If your room is marked non-smoking, do not smoke or vape there; the hotel may charge the cleaning or damage fee stated in its terms. A balcony is not automatic permission either, so ask reception where smoking is allowed.

## Public transport is completely smoke-free

This is the one that catches people, because the habit of a quick cigarette while waiting is so automatic.

Smoking and vaping are banned on every tram, bus, U-Bahn and S-Bahn, and on the platforms outside designated smoking areas. Underground platforms are fully smoke-free. Some open-air S-Bahn stations have a marked smoking area, and that box is the only place to use. Breaking the rule can mean removal and an operator penalty; the amount depends on the operator and current conditions. The [S-Bahn rules of conduct](https://sbahn.berlin/en/plan-a-journey/rail-stations/rules-of-conduct/) spell this out, e-cigarettes included.

My advice is boring but it works: finish your cigarette upstairs, at street level, before you head down. Rides across the centre are short, so you are rarely waiting long. If you want the wider picture on transport penalties, my guide to the [Berlin U-Bahn fine](/post/berlin-u-bahn-fine) covers how these checks actually happen.

## Where you can smoke freely: terraces, streets and parks

Now the good news, and honestly the answer most visitors are looking for. Outdoor café and bar terraces are the natural home of smokers in Berlin. Many open-air tables allow smoking, and you will see it everywhere on a summer evening, but a venue can make its terrace smoke-free. Check the sign or ashtrays before you light up.

Berlin has no blanket citywide ban on outdoor tobacco smoking, but that does not make every pavement or park automatic permission. Playgrounds, schools, events, venues and individual sites can apply stricter rules. Check signs, keep your distance from other people and use an ashtray or take your cigarette ends with you.

## Vaping and e-cigarettes

Keep this simple in practice: treat your vape like a cigarette unless a venue clearly says otherwise. Berlin's tobacco-smoking law does not automatically describe every e-cigarette situation in the same words, but transport operators explicitly ban vaping outside designated smoking areas and private venues can do the same through house rules. If you cannot smoke somewhere, assuming you cannot vape is the safest move.

## Cannabis in Berlin: legal to carry, not to buy

Germany changed its cannabis law on 1 April 2024, and the questions have not stopped since. Here is the honest picture for a visitor.

Adults aged 18 and over may possess up to 25 grams of cannabis in public within the Cannabis Act's limits. Sale, transfer and many consumption locations remain prohibited. There are no general recreational cannabis shops or coffeeshops in Germany. Cultivation associations may supply only eligible members who have lived or ordinarily stayed in Germany for at least six months, and their statutes must require at least three months of membership. A short-term visitor therefore has no lawful retail purchase route. Buying from a street dealer remains illegal. If someone approaches you in a park offering to sell, treat it the way you would any other [Berlin tourist scam](/post/berlin-tourist-scams).

Where you can use it in public is limited, and this is where visitors get the rule wrong. Cannabis consumption is prohibited in the immediate presence of minors; in schools, playgrounds, child and youth facilities and publicly accessible sports grounds; and within sight of their entrances. It is also prohibited in and within sight of cultivation associations. Under the federal rule, a place more than 100 metres from the relevant entrance is no longer considered within sight. Pedestrian zones are restricted between 07:00 and 20:00. Do not assume that an ordinary park or side street is automatically legal: check for children, protected entrances, signs and local house rules first. The official [Cannabis Act FAQ](https://www.bundesgesundheitsministerium.de/en/themen/cannabis/faq-cannabis-act/page) has the current detail. Do not drive or cycle after using cannabis.

## A few habits from someone who walks the city daily

None of this is meant to make Berlin sound like a place of traps. It genuinely is relaxed by most standards. But the rules are enforced, so a little awareness saves you money and hassle.

Step outside when you are unsure. Read the door of a bar before you light up inside. Use only a marked smoking area at a station. Keep cannabis away from minors, protected entrances, cultivation associations and daytime pedestrian zones, and do not drive or cycle after using it. Carry your cigarette ends to a bin.
