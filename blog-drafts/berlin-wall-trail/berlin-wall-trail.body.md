Every few weeks someone on my tour tells me they are going to walk the Berlin Wall Trail. Then they ask which end to start from, and I have to say the thing nobody wants to hear: the trail is 160 kilometres long, and the Wall is not on most of it.

That is not a reason to skip it. It is a reason to pick properly. The Berliner Mauerweg rings the whole of former West Berlin, so about 50 kilometres runs through the city and roughly 110 kilometres passes through forest, lakes, farmland and quiet outer suburbs. The three official sections with the highest concentration of surviving Wall and border sites total about 19 kilometres. That is section length, not 19 continuous kilometres of concrete.

My advice: do not plan this by distance. I have watched people spend an afternoon on a beautiful, completely empty stretch of the outer ring because it looked convenient on a map, then run out of daylight before reaching anything built. Pick the section by what is still standing on it, then work out the trains.

## What the Berlin Wall Trail actually is

The Mauerweg is a signposted walking and cycling route that follows the course of the border around West Berlin. It is a loop, not a line, and it is signed in both directions, so there is no wrong way round.

The official flyers put it at 160 kilometres. The Berlin Senate publishes it as three routes: a Stadtroute of about 50 kilometres from Hermsdorf through the centre to Schönefeld, a Südroute of about 45 kilometres on to Griebnitzsee, and a Westroute of about 65 kilometres back up through Spandau to Hermsdorf. You can download all three [route flyers and the official GPS tracks](https://www.berlin.de/sen/uvk/mobilitaet-und-verkehr/verkehrsplanung/radverkehr/radverkehrsnetz/radrouten/flyer/) from the Senate mobility department.

There is a wrinkle worth knowing before you start googling. For years the reference was berlin.de's Wall portal, which split the route into 14 numbered sections and gave each one its own page. **That portal has been taken offline.** Those URLs now serve a notice saying the content has moved, and it points to a memorial-sites map that has no route or stage data at all. A lot of English-language guides still link to those dead pages, so if you find a beautifully detailed 14-stage breakdown somewhere, check whether its sources still load.

The 14-section structure is still the most useful way to think about the trail, which is why I have kept it in the tool below. It just is not published by the city any more.

## If you have one afternoon, walk Nordbahnhof to Mauerpark

This is the answer for most visitors, and it is barely 2 kilometres.

Start at S Nordbahnhof, which the S1, S2, S25 and S26 all reach, plus the M10 tram. Walk the length of the [Bernauer Strasse memorial](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-wall-memorial-bernauer-strasse), which runs 1.4 kilometres along the street. Somewhere in the middle of it is the reason to come: about 70 metres where the **full depth of the border system still stands together**, outer wall, death strip, patrol road, signal fence, lighting and hinterland wall. That combination does not survive anywhere else in Berlin. Everywhere else you see one layer and have to imagine the rest.

There is a watchtower in the grounds, and the Chapel of Reconciliation, built where a church was blown up in 1985 because it stood in the way of the sightlines. Then keep going and the memorial hands you over to Mauerpark, which was death strip and is now the Sunday flea market and the open-air karaoke bowl.

Roughly 30 minutes of walking and an hour and a half of stopping. Come out at U Eberswalder Strasse on the U2.

**The practical advantage is the opening hours.** The outdoor grounds are open daily from 08:00 to 22:00 and everything is free, according to the [Berlin Wall Foundation's visitor information](https://www.stiftung-berliner-mauer.de/en/berlin-wall-memorial/visit/information). The Visitor Centre keeps shorter hours, Tuesday to Sunday 10:00 to 18:00. No other Wall site in the city gives you a fourteen-hour window, which makes this the one section that still works at 08:30 or at dusk.

## If you want border machinery without the crowds, go to Schlesischer Busch

This is the section I send people to when they have already done Bernauer Strasse, or when they want the border to feel like infrastructure rather than a memorial.

Take the U1 or U3 to Schlesisches Tor and walk east. You cross the Oberbaumbrücke, which was a pedestrian border crossing during the division and is now just a nice bridge full of people, and then you reach the **Führungsstelle Schlesischer Busch** on Puschkinallee.

It is worth understanding what that building was. It is not a watchtower. It is a command post, four floors of precast concrete, and from it the GDR ran 18 watchtowers and the electronic security systems for this whole border sector. Berlin's heritage office [lists it as a protected monument](https://www.berlin.de/landesdenkmalamt/denkmale/highlight-berliner-mauer/mauer-denkmale/fuehrungsstelle-schlesischer-busch-649766.en.php), and a slab section of the actual wall standing beside it has been separately protected since 2005.

Then walk on along the Flutgraben. This 1.1 kilometre stretch was rebuilt in 2023 with four new information steles in Corten steel, and it is honestly **the best-signed kilometre on the entire 160**, because it is the part the upgrade has reached. A cherry-lined bank path takes you to the Lohmühlenbrücke, and you can finish at S Treptower Park for the S8, S9, S41, S42 or S85. Continue into [Treptower Park](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/treptower-park-berlin) if you have the time.

About 3 kilometres, 40 minutes of walking, and far fewer people than Checkpoint Charlie.

## The classic centre line, if you want the famous stops

If your priority is the recognisable Berlin Wall sights rather than the best-preserved ones, walk the centre from the Brandenburg Gate to the East Side Gallery. It is about 8 kilometres and it will take most of a morning with stops.

You follow the double row of cobblestones that marks the Wall's course through the government quarter. You pass the [Topography of Terror](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/topography-of-terror-berlin) on Niederkirchnerstrasse, where about 200 metres of original Wall still stands along the excavated Gestapo cellars. You get the Peter Fechter memorial on Zimmerstrasse, then Checkpoint Charlie, and you finish at the [East Side Gallery](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/east-side-gallery-berlin-guide), 1,316 metres of painted Wall and the longest surviving stretch anywhere in the world.

**Start before 10:00 if you want photographs.** By midday the East Side Gallery is a moving wall of phones and the Checkpoint Charlie junction is at its worst.

If you want to understand what you are tracing before you walk it, my guide to [how Berlin was divided](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/how-berlin-was-divided-a-simple-guide-to-east-vs-west) covers the geography, and the [interactive map of where the Wall stood](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/where-was-the-berlin-wall-interactive-map) is useful for orienting yourself before you commit to a route.

## The outer loop is real, and it is mostly not about the Wall

I want to be fair to the other 110 kilometres, because people who ride the full loop tend to love it.

The Spandauer Forst is a shaded forest path. The Gatower Rieselfelder are old sewage fields that are now full of birds. At Groß Glienicker See, young forest has grown straight over where the death strip ran. South of the city you get the Buckow field landscape, which the official flyer describes in terms of autumn kite-flying, and a cherry tree avenue along Lichterfelder Allee. It is a genuinely good long ride through countryside most visitors never see.

![The Berlin Wall Trail as a smooth asphalt path through Berlin Suedpark, lined with blossoming trees](blog-drafts/berlin-wall-trail/images/optimized/05-mauerweg-suedpark.jpg)
_The Mauerweg through Suedpark. Pleasant, easy riding, and a fair picture of what most of the loop is really like._

It is just not a history trip. Two exceptions are worth naming:

- **The Chris Gueffroy stele** at the Britzer Zweigkanal, for the last person shot dead at the Wall, in February 1989, nine months before it opened. It sits on a quiet stretch of the southern route and it lands harder for that.
- **The Nieder Neuendorf tower** near Hennigsdorf, a GDR border tower converted into a free four-level museum. In 2026 it opens from 4 April to 3 October, Tuesday to Sunday and public holidays, 10:00 to 18:00. [The town's own page](https://www.hennigsdorf.de/Stadtleben/Kunst-und-Kultur/Grenzturm-Nieder-Neuendorf/) directs you there on bus X36 to Am Oberjägerweg, not by train.

There is a third surviving tower closer in, the Günter Litfin memorial at Kieler Strasse 2, but check the calendar before you walk over: it opens **only at weekends, 11:00 to 17:00, from 1 May to 31 October**.

## What the Senate says about riding it, and why it matters

This is the line I wish more English guides quoted. All three official route flyers carry the same warning:

**Der Mauerweg ist kein ausgebauter Radweg.** The Mauerweg is not a developed cycle path. The flyers go on to say the surface alternates between asphalt, forest floor, cobbles and field track, that small diversions exist where the path is damaged and unrideable, and that occasionally it means getting off and pushing.

Compare that with the tourism copy you will find elsewhere, which tends to describe a mostly asphalted patrol path ideal for cycling and needing no particular fitness. Both descriptions come from official Berlin websites. The Senate's is the one that matches the ground.

My practical read, after sending a lot of people out there:

- **A share bike or basic city rental is fine for the whole Stadtroute.** Nordbahnhof through the centre to Warschauer Strasse and on to Treptow is pavement, park path and canal path. No problem.
- **For the Südroute fields or the Westroute forest, get something with wider tyres.** The Alt-Staaken cobbles and the Gatow sand will make a share bike miserable.
- **There is genuinely original patrol road surface in a few places**, notably the customs road along the Nordgraben up to Klemkestrasse, and a labelled stretch of Kolonnenweg near Rudow. Bumpy concrete slabs, which is exactly the point.

If you are sorting out a bike first, my guide to [renting a bike in Berlin](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/renting-a-bike-in-berlin) covers the providers, and [Berlin's bike lanes for tourists](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-bike-lanes-tourists) covers the riding culture you are about to join.

## The ticket detail that catches people out

Two things, and they are both easy to get wrong.

**Your bike needs its own ticket, and the Deutschlandticket does not include it.** Every bicycle carried on the VBB network needs a bicycle fare ticket on top of your own valid ticket. [BVG's own bicycle page](https://www.bvg.de/en/bicycle) sets out the rest: bikes go on the U-Bahn, tram, ferry and the night buses N1 to N9, plus the S-Bahn, but **not on normal daytime buses**. Board only at doors marked with a bike symbol, expect a maximum of two bikes per marked area per carriage, and hold onto your bike for the journey. Prams and wheelchairs have priority, so if the space is needed you can be asked to get off. A fully folded folding bike counts as hand luggage and travels free.

**Half the trail is not in Berlin.** Hennigsdorf, Hohen Neuendorf, Stolpe, Teltow, Kleinmachnow, Griebnitzsee and Potsdam are all Brandenburg, which means zone C. Since **the BC ticket option was abolished on 1 January 2026** there is no cheaper middle option, so any outer-loop endpoint is an ABC journey, and the bike ticket has to match the same zones. My [Berlin AB and ABC ticket zones guide](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-ab-abc-ticket-zones) explains the boundaries, and if you are weighing up passes, [the Deutschlandticket for tourists](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/deutschlandticket-berlin-tourists) covers where it does and does not help.

## What is closed or awkward in 2026

The trail is in the middle of a rebuild programme running to 2027, and as of mid-2026 a bit more than half of it is done. That leaves a few things to plan around, and the [Grün Berlin project ticker](https://gruen-berlin.de/projekte/infrastruktur/berliner-mauerweg/projekt-ticker) is where the current state is published.

- **Hahneberg, in Spandau**, was still under construction in Grün Berlin's 29 June 2026 update. Check the project ticker for the current diversion before committing to this stretch.
- **The shore path at Groß Glienicker See is interrupted**, as the current official flyer states directly.
- **The Griebnitzsee lakeside path is partly blocked by residents**, in a legal dispute that has run for years. The city of Potsdam [routes walkers](https://www.potsdam.de/de/uferweg-griebnitzsee) via Rudolf-Breitscheid-Strasse, Karl-Marx-Strasse and Virchowstrasse instead. This is the main reason I would treat Griebnitzsee as a [Potsdam day trip](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/potsdam-from-berlin-day-trip) rather than a Wall walk, even though the Glienicker Brücke and the preserved wall remnants on that shore are genuinely worth seeing.
- **Getting to Griebnitzsee changed this summer.** The Stadtbahn between Charlottenburg and Ostbahnhof is [closed to regional and long-distance trains from 14 June to 12 December 2026](https://www.vbb.de/unterwegs-im-vbb/baustellen-bei-regional-und-s-bahn/stadtbahnsperrung-2026/), which disrupts the RE1 out that way. The S-Bahn is not affected, so take the S7 or S1.

Signage is the other thing in flux. The route is marked with white-on-grey Berliner Mauerweg signs, and since 2023 a new Corten steel system of steles and markers has been rolling out, with around 130 more planned across the full route. That is good news eventually and uneven right now. **Download the official GPX track for your section before you go** rather than trusting the signs to be consistent.

## The mistakes I see most

- **Expecting continuous Wall.** It is not a wall you walk beside. It is a line you follow, with the Wall surviving at a handful of points. The Berlin Wall Foundation is blunt about this: the Wall has largely disappeared from the cityscape. If you want the full picture of what is left, I keep a running guide to [where the Wall stood and what survives](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/the-berlin-wall-where-it-stood-and-what-s-left-in-2026).
- **Choosing a section by how convenient the start looks.** Check the far end too. Section 11 finishes at Staaken, which has no S-Bahn at all, only the RB21 and RE4. Section 8 finishes at Lichtenrade, which is on the S2 and nothing else. A missed connection out there is a long wait on an empty platform.
- **Booking a whole day for the outer loop and expecting history.** Ride it because a forest and a lake shore are a good way to spend a summer day. Not because you will find the Wall out there.
- **Planning from a dead website.** Worth repeating. If the guide you are reading links to berlin.de/mauer, its facts have not been checked recently.

## Where this fits with the tour

My [free walking tour](https://www.berlinwalk.com/book-berlin-walking-tour/berlin-free-walking-tour-tip-based) covers the historic centre rather than the Wall trail, so the two do not overlap much. That is deliberate, and it makes them fit together well.

Several guests have done exactly that: the tour in the morning, Bernauer Strasse in the afternoon, with the division making a lot more sense in the second half because they had the pre-1961 city in their heads first.

If you only have one Berlin day and you are choosing, do the centre first. The Wall is easier to understand once you know what it was cutting through.
