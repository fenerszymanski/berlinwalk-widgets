# Alternative Transport in Berlin: MILES, Scooters, Bikes and Ride Apps

Alternative transport in Berlin looks exciting from the outside: MILES cars on the street, scooters around every busy corner, rental bikes near stations, Uber-style taxi apps, Jelbi hubs, BVG route planners, Google Maps, Apple Maps, and a small army of provider apps asking for registration.

The trick is not downloading everything. The trick is knowing which option solves which problem.

For most Berlin sightseeing days, [public transport](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-public-transport-explained-for-tourists-u-bahn-s-bahn-tram-bus) is still the backbone. U-Bahn, S-Bahn, tram and bus cover the city well, and a valid ticket works across the main network. But public transport is not always the whole answer. Sometimes the fastest Berlin move is a 10-minute bike ride, a short scooter hop to a tram stop, a taxi app after midnight, or a MILES car for a planned errand outside the central sightseeing rhythm.

This guide is the practical version: which app to open, which vehicle to avoid, and when the boring old train is still better.

## Alternative transport in Berlin: the short answer

If you only remember one thing, remember this order:

- **Use BVG, Jelbi or Google Maps first** to understand the route.
- **Use walking or public transport** for most central sightseeing.
- **Use a bike or e-bike** for sunny 2 to 5 km trips without luggage.
- **Use an e-scooter** only for short, simple hops where you have bike-lane space and no bags.
- **Use MILES or car sharing** for planned car needs, not casual central sightseeing.
- **Use a taxi or taxi app** for luggage, bad weather, late-night fatigue, awkward final legs or groups splitting the fare.

Berlin is not a city where a car automatically makes life easier. Central driving can mean traffic, one-way streets, parking rules, environmental zones and provider boundaries. For visitors, the best alternative transport is usually the one that connects neatly with public transport, not the one that replaces it completely.

## Start with the map layer, not the vehicle

Before choosing MILES, scooter, bike or taxi, open the map layer. That sounds obvious, but it prevents most bad Berlin transport decisions.

For normal public transport, use BVG, VBB, Google Maps or Apple Maps. The official [Berlin.de ticket page](https://www.berlin.de/en/public-transportation/1772016-2913840-tickets-fares-and-route-maps.en.html) explains the basic zone logic: AB covers the Berlin urban area up to the city limits, while ABC includes BER Airport and Potsdam. The same page also lists the current single and 24-hour ticket prices, so you can compare a paid ride with public transport before you panic-buy convenience.

For mixed mobility, [Jelbi](https://www.jelbi.de/en/home/) is the most Berlin-specific app to understand. It combines public transport with sharing options: e-scooters, bikes, cars, e-mopeds and taxis. The real value is not that every trip must happen inside Jelbi. The value is that it shows Berlin as a connected mobility system rather than a pile of separate apps.

My habit in Berlin is simple:

- First map the route.
- Then check whether public transport already solves it.
- Then add a bike, scooter, car share or taxi only if it improves the trip clearly.

That little pause saves money and embarrassment.

## Jelbi: the easiest Berlin mobility app to understand

Jelbi is the app I would put first on this list for visitors who want one Berlin-specific mobility layer. The [Jelbi site](https://www.jelbi.de/en/home/) describes it as one registration for bus, train, e-moped, e-scooter, bike, car and taxi. That is exactly the tourist problem: you do not want ten separate accounts just to move across town.

The current [Jelbi mobility partner list](https://www.jelbi.de/en/mobility-partners/) includes public transport, e-scooters such as Voi, Lime, Bolt and Dott, bike sharing from Nextbike, Lime and Bolt, e-mopeds from Emmy, car sharing from MILES and SIXT share, and taxis through Taxi Berlin. It also notes that not every partner is always bookable directly in the app, so treat Jelbi as a strong starting point rather than magic.

Jelbi stations are especially useful near S-Bahn and U-Bahn stops. They make the idea of a combined trip more realistic: train for the long part, shared bike or scooter for the last kilometre, taxi if the final leg is a mess.

Use Jelbi when:

- You want one Berlin mobility overview.
- You are open to mixing modes.
- You are near an S-Bahn or U-Bahn station with shared vehicles around it.
- You want to compare public transport with a bike, scooter, taxi or car share.

Skip it when:

- You already know you just need one normal BVG ticket.
- You are in a hurry and do not want registration friction.
- A provider app is already installed and shows a better live vehicle nearby.

## MILES and car sharing: useful, but not a casual tourist taxi

MILES is the car sharing name visitors notice quickly because the cars are visible on Berlin streets. Berlin.de's [MILES overview](https://www.berlin.de/en/getting-around/carsharing/5909693-5889084-miles.en.html) explains the basic model: you find and reserve a vehicle via the app, and the price is based on kilometres rather than only rental time. MILES also offers different vehicle types, from smaller cars to vans, depending on availability.

The key tourist warning: MILES is not the same decision as a taxi. It means you are driving, parking, following traffic rules, handling provider boundaries, and accepting responsibility for the car. That can be great for the right errand and annoying for the wrong sightseeing day.

MILES' own [Berlin help page](https://support.miles-mobility.com/hc/en-us/articles/4408702259730-Berlin) says BER Airport is included in the Berlin business area and that rentals can start or end at indicated car sharing parking spaces. The [MILES parking guide](https://www.miles-mobility.com/en-de/parking-guide) also stresses the app's parking zones, Jelbi/BVG station parking, dedicated car-sharing signs and business-area limits.

That makes MILES good for:

- A planned trip where a car genuinely helps.
- A small group that needs door-to-door flexibility.
- A van or larger vehicle need.
- A late or awkward route if you are comfortable driving in Berlin.
- Some BER or outer-area use cases, after checking the app rules.

It is usually bad for:

- Museum Island, Hackescher Markt, Alexanderplatz, Brandenburg Gate or other central sightseeing clusters.
- A quick dinner transfer where a taxi would remove the parking problem.
- Anyone who is tired, jet-lagged, nervous in city traffic or unsure about German signs.
- A spontaneous tourist ride before your account, license and payment setup are verified.

My honest view: MILES is a powerful Berlin tool, but it is not the first tool for a normal visitor day. Treat it like a car, not like a bigger scooter.

## E-scooters in Berlin: short hops only

E-scooters are everywhere, which makes them look easier than they are. They are good for one narrow job: short, simple, low-luggage trips where you have a safe route and a legal place to ride.

Berlin.de's [electric scooter sharing overview](https://www.berlin.de/en/getting-around/electric-scooter-sharing/) lists providers such as Bolt, Dott, Lime and Voi, and it gives the rule tourists most often break: no e-scooters on sidewalks or pedestrian zones. The [Federal Ministry of Transport FAQ](https://www.bmv.de/SharedDocs/EN/Articles/StV/Roadtraffic/light-electric-vehicles-faq.html) explains that these vehicles should use cycling infrastructure where it exists, and the carriageway if not. Footpaths are not a fallback just because the street feels uncomfortable.

Berlin Police also states the practical safety rules clearly in its [e-scooter flyer](https://www.berlin.de/polizei/_assets/aufgaben/anlagen-verkehrssicherheit/flyer_e-scooter_englisch.pdf): minimum age 14, no passengers, alcohol limits apply, no phone use without hands-free equipment, no helmet requirement but helmets save lives, and parking should not obstruct others.

Use an e-scooter when:

- The trip is roughly 1 to 3 km.
- You are alone.
- You have no suitcase.
- You can use bike lanes or calm streets.
- You are sober and alert.
- The weather is dry.

Avoid an e-scooter when:

- You are carrying luggage.
- You are with children or a group.
- The route crosses chaotic central streets.
- It is raining or icy.
- You have been drinking.
- You only feel safe by riding on the sidewalk.

For visitors, a scooter should feel boringly easy. If it feels like a dare, do not take it.

## Bike sharing and e-bikes: often better than scooters

If you are comfortable in city traffic, bike sharing is often the best alternative transport in Berlin. It is slower than the fantasy version of a scooter, but usually more stable, more practical for slightly longer distances, and more pleasant on green routes through Tiergarten, Kreuzberg, Prenzlauer Berg or along the Spree.

Berlin.de's [bike sharing overview](https://www.berlin.de/en/getting-around/bikesharing/) lists current rental-bike options such as Dott, Nextbike, Bolt and LimeBike. The same source makes the important point: coverage, registration and pricing models vary by provider, so you should check the app map before building a day around one brand.

The sweet spot for rental bikes is a 2 to 5 km daylight trip without bags. Think: Prenzlauer Berg to Museum Island, Kreuzberg to Tempelhofer Feld, Friedrichshain to the East Side Gallery, or a relaxed route through Tiergarten.

Use bike sharing when:

- You are confident cycling in a city.
- You want to see neighborhoods, not just transfer between stations.
- The weather is decent.
- Your route has bike lanes, park paths or calmer streets.
- You are not carrying luggage.

Avoid bike sharing when:

- You are anxious in traffic.
- You do not know the route and the streets look hostile.
- You need to arrive clean and dry.
- It is late, wet, icy or windy.
- You are trying to ride through dense pedestrian areas.

My rule: if the bike ride looks like part of the Berlin day, take the bike. If it looks like a stress test, take the train.

## Uber-style taxi apps in Berlin

Taxi and ride apps are the cleanest alternative when the problem is comfort, luggage, late-night fatigue or a difficult final leg.

Uber's [Berlin taxi page](https://www.uber.com/de/en/r/cities/taxi/berlin-be-de/) says the app can request local taxis in Berlin, with cashless payment and upfront prices. Berlin.de's [taxi page](https://www.berlin.de/en/public-transportation/1756978-2913840-taxi-phone-numbers-fares-rules.en.html) explains the regulated taxi system: official taxis use fixed city tariffs and a taximeter inside Berlin, and taxis can be ordered by phone, app, at taxi stands or hailed on the street.

That means the app is often about convenience, not automatic savings. You get pickup clarity, card payment and a price view. You do not get permission to ignore basic taxi logic.

Use a taxi app when:

- You have luggage.
- It is late and the public route has awkward transfers.
- It is raining hard.
- Your group can split the fare.
- You want cashless payment and a clear pickup point.

Use a normal taxi rank when:

- You are at a main station, hotel, BER Airport or a large taxi stand.
- A licensed taxi is already waiting.
- The app price looks silly.

For the detailed fare and airport rules, use my full guide to [taxis and Uber in Berlin](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/taxi-in-berlin) or the [Berlin Taxi or Uber Cost Checker](https://www.berlinwalk.com/tools/berlin-taxi-uber-cost-checker).

## E-mopeds and other shared vehicles

Berlin also has e-moped sharing, most visibly Emmy through the Jelbi ecosystem. E-mopeds can be useful if you are licensed, confident, sober, and understand the vehicle rules. They are not my first recommendation for most visitors because the friction is higher: account setup, license checks, helmets, traffic confidence and more responsibility than a bike or scooter.

For a tourist, e-mopeds make sense only if you already ride confidently at home and the app setup is done before you need the vehicle. Do not make your first unfamiliar-traffic experiment on a busy Berlin avenue.

There are also classic bike-rental shops, hotel bikes and guided bike tours. Those can be better than app bikes if you want a proper half-day ride, a child seat, a helmet, a lock, service support or a planned route. App sharing is best for short flexible movement. Traditional rental is better for a real cycling day.

## Common tourist situations and the best transport choice

Here is the practical version I would give a friend visiting Berlin.

**Alexanderplatz to Brandenburg Gate:** take U5 or walk if you want the full Unter den Linden axis. Do not rent a car. A scooter is possible only if you are confident and the route feels calm.

**Museum Island to Hackescher Markt:** walk. It is too short to overthink.

**Kreuzberg to Tempelhofer Feld:** bike or public transport. A bike can be lovely if the weather is good.

**Late dinner in Neukolln to a hotel in Mitte:** check public transport first. If the app shows awkward transfers, compare taxi or Uber.

**BER Airport to central Berlin:** train if luggage is manageable and you are not exhausted. Taxi if comfort matters more than cost. MILES only if you genuinely want to drive and understand the parking/end-rental rules.

**A rainy evening with two tired people:** taxi app. Save the analysis.

**A sunny afternoon with no plan:** bike or walk. Berlin rewards slow movement.

**A shopping errand, bulky luggage or moving something:** MILES or another car share can make sense if you are set up and confident driving.

**A 1 km last-mile gap from a station:** Jelbi, bike, scooter or walking. Pick the safest route, not the most novel vehicle.

## What tourists get wrong

The first mistake is assuming alternative transport is always faster. It often is not. Registration, finding a vehicle, checking parking zones, unlocking, riding carefully and ending the trip can take longer than a simple U-Bahn ride.

The second mistake is using the wrong vehicle for the job. Scooters are not luggage tools. Car sharing is not a taxi. Taxi apps are not public transport replacements. Bike sharing is not fun if you are frightened by the route.

The third mistake is ignoring the final 300 metres. Berlin route planners can make a trip look clean until you realize the last stretch involves cobblestones, rain, a dark walk, luggage, roadworks or a vehicle drop-off boundary.

The fourth mistake is copying locals without the local context. A Berliner on an e-bike may know the quiet parallel street, the bridge ramp, the construction detour and the bad junction. You may not. That does not mean you cannot ride. It means you should choose easier routes.

## How this fits a BerlinWalk day

The BerlinWalk public tour starts at the World Clock on Alexanderplatz and ends near Hackescher Markt. For that route, public transport or walking is almost always the right answer. The start and end are central, connected and not worth solving with a car.

Where alternative transport helps is around the edges of your day: getting from a hotel to the meeting point, continuing after the tour to food in Prenzlauer Berg, crossing to the East Side Gallery, making a late dinner plan, or choosing a bike ride when the weather is beautiful.

The tour itself is about **2 hours**, and it gives you a mental map of central Berlin. Once Alexanderplatz, Museum Island, the Spree and Hackescher Markt make sense on foot, the app map becomes less abstract. You stop seeing Berlin as dots and start seeing it as distances.

That is the real mobility upgrade.

## My honest recommendation

Download fewer apps, but use them better.

Start with BVG or Jelbi for the route. Keep Google Maps or Apple Maps for orientation. Add one bike/scooter app only if you actually plan to ride. Use MILES when a car solves a real car problem. Use Uber, Free Now, Bolt Taxi or Taxi Berlin when comfort, luggage or late-night simplicity matters.

For a first Berlin trip, the best setup is:

- BVG or Jelbi for public transport and mixed mobility.
- Google Maps or Apple Maps for walking orientation.
- One bike or scooter app if you are comfortable riding.
- One taxi app as backup.
- MILES only if you are genuinely likely to drive.

Berlin is easy to move through when you do not let the apps make the decision for you.
