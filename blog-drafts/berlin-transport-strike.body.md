Berlin is a city where a transport warning can make the whole morning feel broken before the day has actually gone wrong. You walk into Hauptbahnhof, Alexanderplatz or Friedrichstraße, see red text on the board, and suddenly every plan looks fragile.

My advice: do not try to beat the entire Berlin transport system. I have made that mistake before, and it turns a normal delay into a tired, phone-refreshing mess. First find out which part of the network is affected, then make the day smaller. That is usually the better Berlin transport strike plan.

## Berlin transport strike: first make the city smaller

A Berlin transport strike does not always mean Berlin has stopped. It usually means one part of the city has become harder to use.

That distinction matters. Berlin is not one single transport network. The U-Bahn, S-Bahn, trams, buses, regional trains and airport trains do not always fail together. A BVG strike can leave the S-Bahn running. An S-Bahn problem can still leave the U-Bahn useful. A regional train cancellation does not mean you cannot enjoy Mitte, Kreuzberg, Prenzlauer Berg, Charlottenburg or the historic centre around Museum Island.

My advice: before you change your whole day, change the scale of the day. If your original plan was Alexanderplatz, Museum Island, Potsdamer Platz and Charlottenburg, cut it down. Pick one area and do it properly. Berlin is much easier when you stop dragging a broken route across the map.

If you need the basic system before the backup plan, start with my [Berlin public transport guide](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-public-transport-explained-for-tourists-u-bahn-s-bahn-tram-bus). It explains how U-Bahn, S-Bahn, tram and bus fit together before you have to make a quick decision.

## Check the source, then stop checking

The useful first move is not opening five apps. It is checking the right official source once.

For U-Bahn, tram and bus problems, use [BVG traffic news](https://www.bvg.de/en/connections/traffic-news) or the [BVG strike page](https://www.bvg.de/en/strike). For S-Bahn changes, use the [S-Bahn Berlin timetable changes page](https://sbahn.berlin/en/plan-a-journey/timetable-changes/). For a mixed route across Berlin and Brandenburg, the [VBB app](https://www.vbb.de/en/driving-information/apps/vbb-app-bus-bahn/) is usually the most practical option.

After that, decide. Do not spend half the morning comparing apps that are all describing the same bad news in slightly different ways.

My rule is simple: if one system is down, look for the next layer. If several systems are messy, stop crossing the city and choose a smaller Berlin day.

## If you are already central, walking may be the best transport

Central Berlin rewards people who stop fighting the map.

If you are near Alexanderplatz, Museum Island, Unter den Linden, Brandenburg Gate or Hackescher Markt, you can still have a strong Berlin day without solving every train problem. From Alexanderplatz, walk past Rotes Rathaus, continue toward Berlin Cathedral, cross Museum Island, and end around Hackescher Markt. That is not a backup plan in the sad sense. It is a good Berlin route.

You get East German city planning, Prussian architecture, the river, museum views, courtyards, cafes and enough history to make the transport problem feel much smaller.

My advice: if the app asks you to make two transfers for a short central journey, walk. Berlin often makes more sense above ground anyway. For a broader version of this idea, my guide to [alternative transport in Berlin](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/alternative-transport-berlin) is useful when public transport is not the easiest answer.

## If BVG is affected, look for the S-Bahn

BVG usually means U-Bahn, tram and bus. If BVG is on strike, do not automatically assume every train is useless. The S-Bahn may still be running, and that can save the day.

This is why stations like Alexanderplatz, Friedrichstraße, Hackescher Markt, Ostkreuz, Südkreuz, Zoologischer Garten and Hauptbahnhof are worth understanding. They give you options. You may need to walk 10 or 15 minutes at the end, but that is usually better than waiting for a bus that is not coming.

My advice: do not build your day around a replacement bus unless the official app clearly shows it and the stop is easy to find. Replacement buses can work, but they can also turn a simple trip into a confusing hunt for a temporary sign.

If the problem is not only the strike but also which ticket you need, keep my [Berlin AB or ABC ticket guide](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/berlin-ab-abc-ticket-zones) open. Ticket-zone mistakes become easier to make when you are rushing.

## If the S-Bahn or regional trains are affected, stay more local

S-Bahn and regional train problems are different because they often affect longer movement. That is when visitors lose time trying to keep a plan that no longer fits the day.

If you were planning Potsdam, Sachsenhausen, Wannsee or a long cross-city route, pause before pushing ahead. It may still be possible, but it may not be worth the stress unless that trip is the main reason for your day.

Inside Berlin, the U-Bahn can still be useful. It is often enough for hotel to museum, museum to lunch, lunch to evening plan. It may not take you everywhere, but it can keep the day alive.

My advice: when long-distance movement gets fragile, choose a neighborhood instead of a heroic route. Kreuzberg, Mitte, Prenzlauer Berg, Charlottenburg or Neukölln can each fill several hours if you stop treating them as quick stops.

## BER Airport needs a different level of caution

Airport travel is the one place where I would not be relaxed about a Berlin transport strike. Missing a museum slot is annoying. Missing a flight is expensive.

BER Airport is connected by S-Bahn, regional trains and airport express services, but these routes can change during strikes or construction. Check the [official BER public transport page](https://ber.berlin-airport.de/en/orientation/getting-here/public-transport.html) on the day you travel.

My advice: if there is an active strike warning, add a real 90-minute buffer. Not a hopeful 20 minutes. Not "I think it will be fine." A real 90 minutes.

If the train route looks unstable, consider a taxi or ride-hail for the airport leg, especially early in the morning or late at night. If a long-distance train delay causes a bigger problem, check [DB passenger rights](https://int.bahn.de/en/booking-information/passenger-rights).

## The moment to stop waiting

There is a specific Berlin platform moment that every visitor recognizes. The train says 5 minutes. Then 8. Then 13. Then the screen changes, and nobody around you looks surprised.

That is the moment to stop being polite with the delay.

If the wait is short and the station is calm, stay. If the delay keeps growing, check one alternative. If the board says "Störung", "Ausfall" or "Warnstreik", do not wait for the perfect answer. Move.

My advice: after about 15 minutes, waiting often becomes the mistake. Walk to another station, switch to another system, or cut the route down. Berlin will not reward you for standing still at the wrong platform.

## Taxis help, but do not make them the whole plan

Taxis and ride-hail can be useful during a strike, but they are not magic. When everyone has the same idea, prices rise, cars disappear, and traffic around major stations can become slow.

Use a taxi for the part of the trip where it really matters: the airport, a fixed reservation, a late-night return, or a gap between two working transport lines. Do not use it just because the first train is delayed.

For normal situations, I have a separate guide to [taxis in Berlin](https://www.berlinwalk.com/post/taxi-in-berlin). On strike days, I would treat taxis as a final piece of the plan, not the whole plan.

## The plan I would use before leaving the hotel

Before leaving your hotel or apartment, take two minutes.

Check whether the problem is BVG, S-Bahn, regional trains or airport transport. Pick one main area for the next few hours. Screenshot the route while you still have battery and calm internet. Leave early for anything fixed: airport, train, museum slot, restaurant booking or tour start.

That is enough. You do not need to master Berlin transport in one morning. You just need to avoid the worst version of a strike day: standing in a station, refreshing the same bad route, while a better Berlin day is still possible a few streets away.
